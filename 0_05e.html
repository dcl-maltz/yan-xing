Yanxing: The Rekindling of Han · Preface

Preface (V)

← Preface (IV) Contents →

Since Luoyang was vulgar and the pursuers were close behind, why not step beyond the city gates and let the world open wide? Ji Ying had already decided to wander the land, yet when Changde asked where they should go, she could not name a destination. She only said, “Somewhere with fewer ordinary people and people with lofty characterss would be good.”

“In earlier years,” Changde said, “your father found the hermits Sun Deng and Wang Lie1 living in earthen caves in the Taihang and Wangwu Mountains. Perhaps those two are still there. But you are still young…”
She hesitated and did not finish the sentence.

Ji Ying thought, “Father once said that those two still thought his knowledge insufficient. If I go seeking them, it would be presumptuous. They might not even receive someone as ordinary as me. Besides, if I walk the exact same path as Father, that is not truly independent. I would not deserve to be his daughter.”
So she asked, “Are there any lofty people who are not quite so lofty?”

Changde pressed her lips together and thought for a moment.
“South of the city there are many inns where merchants and travelers come and go. People who have seen the world in every direction gather there. We could ask around.”

Ji Ying nodded.

The East Market, West Market, the Imperial Academy, and the palace city of Luoyang all lay north of the Luo River. To reach the southern quarter, one had to cross a stone bridge a hundred zhang long that spanned the river. It was already late at night. A thin crescent moon hung in the east, and few people were on the road. Two Imperial Academy students disguised as young men walked across the bridge. The night patrol soldiers simply assumed they were students returning home and did not question them.

Under the dim moonlight, the yellow silk qin bag Ji Ying carried in her arms shone faintly with silver light.

Changde touched the brocade wrapping and said, “This brocade silk is beautiful. It suits Master Ji’s qin perfectly.”

Ji Ying smiled.
“Zhong Hui gave it to my father. The man himself was unworthy, but the gift was acceptable.”

“Really?”

Ji Ying then told her the story from six years ago—how her father had been forging iron in the Yuntai Mountains when Zhong Hui came to visit, and Ji Kang completely ignored him. Ji Ying had still been young then, so the detail she remembered most clearly was that the brocade cloth used to wrap Zhong Hui’s treatise The Four Foundations of Talent and Nature. The qin bag was kept, while the treatise itself had been used as toilet paper.

Changde burst into laughter.
“This story about Master Ji and Zhong Hui must be written down so that later generations will know it. As for what use the Four Foundations of Talent and Nature truly had—Heaven knows, Earth knows, you know, and I know. That is enough.”

Ji Ying then remembered how the Seven Sages of the Bamboo Grove had once discussed that treatise. Ji Kang had proposed four ranks of human nature: the first seek beauty, the second seek goodness, the third seek truth, and the fourth are not convetional.

Changde asked, “Have you heard the line from the Laozi: ‘The Way of Heaven shows no partiality; yet it often sides with the good’? Do you believe Heaven rewards the good and punishes the evil?”

Ji Ying thought, “My father did good deeds and tried to clear the name of his friend Lü An, yet wicked men plotted against him and had him killed. Now the evil of the world runs rampant and arrogant.”
So she answered, “I certainly wish that good is rewarded—but I do not believe it.”

Changde nodded.
“I know why you do not believe. But I believe it. And I suspect your father believed it as well.”

Ji Ying frowned.
“If good and evil truly bring rewards and punishments, then how is that different from the foolish tales told by pedantic scholars? Stories about filial sons moving Heaven and Earth so that carp leap from frozen rivers for their parents to eat? My father despised such superstition.”

“Where should I begin?” Changde stopped walking. “Your father once debated with Ruan Deru, the governor of Henei, in the discussion called On Dwellings Without Auspicious or Inauspicious Influence upon Life.2 Let us begin there.”

The two young women reached the center of the bridge and leaned together against the stone railing, watching the moon and the river.

Changde looked up at the stars.
“Governor Ruan argued that people cannot see the nature of life and death, nor understand fortune and misfortune. Because of this ignorance they chase after luck—choosing house sites, arranging burials, consulting yin and yang numbers, casting divinations, asking spirits and ghosts about everything.”

Ji Ying showed a look of contempt.

“But your father opposed Governor Ruan,” Changde said. A faint glimmer shone in her eyes.

“My father supported superstition?”

“Not exactly. Master Ji did not support superstition—but he did not approve of dismissing it lightly. He said that the spirits are so distant that we humans cannot know whether they understand human fortune and misfortune. And besides, a person’s life may not be fixed by what is given at birth. Who knows whether later actions and words might change it?”

Ji Ying’s eyes suddenly brightened.
“I remember now! My father once told a similar story. One man feared death and looked up at Heaven, worrying that the sky might collapse. A second man said Heaven nurtures all things like its own children—why would it destroy them? A third man said that everything eventually breaks; Heaven is also a thing, and though we do not know when, it too will break someday. A fourth man said: how do you know it must break—or must not break? Do not trouble yourself over things that cannot be known.”

Changde laughed happily.
“That is precisely your father’s four ranks of human nature! Governor Ruan sought truth and disliked superstition, but does that mean supernatural things can never happen? Since we cannot know, it is better not to worry too much.”

The two girls stood on the bridge and watched the boats moored along both banks of the Luo River—some carrying passengers, some transporting goods. Cool gusts of wind swept across the stone bridge, making them feel the chill, so they continued walking.

Ji Ying asked, “If my father was uncertain, why do you still believe he thought Heaven rewards the good and punishes the evil?”

“In that debate,” Changde replied, “your father quoted an ancient saying: ‘In a household that accumulates good deeds, blessings will surely remain.’ The more good one accumulates, the more gradually good fortune will answer. But the retribution ordinary people talk about—immediate payback, tooth for tooth—does not necessarily occur.”

Ji Ying thought for a moment.
“But my father often quoted: ‘Heaven and Earth are not benevolent; they treat everything as straw sacrifices.’ If we are nothing more than straw sacrifices—trampled flat or burned away—why would good deeds bring reward?”

Changde nodded.
“Your father truly believed that the spirits are distant. Whether the year brings gentle rains or floods, droughts, or locust plagues has no clear cause in human goodness. That is why he said Heaven and Earth are not benevolent. But…”

Changde took Ji Ying’s right hand. Ji Ying had just cut off her long hair; the back of her neck felt cold, yet Changde’s palm felt warm.

“Do you know why I helped you?”

Ji Ying thought, “I have only known her for half a day.”
So she answered, “Because you studied under my father for many years, and my temperament resembles his in some ways.”

“Do you know why Master Ji, who could have wandered with hermits and avoided Zhong Hui’s persecution, chose instead to come to the Imperial Academy in Luoyang?”

Ji Ying thought for a moment.
“Because my father believed teaching the classics at the Academy was more worthwhile.”

“Yes. To us students, to have a prestigious scholar like Master Ji—someone willing to stand in danger just to pass on his learning—that itself is a good deed. Even if that deed did not bring good fortune to Master Ji himself.”

Ji Ying seemed to understand.
“So you take care of me to repay my father’s good deeds.”

Changde only smiled and did not answer.

Ji Ying remembered the day before.
“In the East Market, three thousand students of the Academy pleaded for my father’s life. My father’s learning neither helped nor harmed their careers. They came simply to repay him.”

“Quite so.” Changde said.

Warmth rose in Ji Ying’s heart, and her eyes grew moist.

“So the reward of goodness does not lie with spirits—it lies in human hearts.”

Changde squeezed Ji Ying’s hand.
“And this reward continues far into the future. Whenever later generations read of Master Ji forging iron in the mountains without caring for worldly opinion, or of him pleading for a friend imprisoned unjustly and dying for that cause, they will be moved by that proud and independent spirit. They will carry on his will, so that the hearts of the people do not collapse in a corrupt age.”

The two of them crossed the stone bridge. The lights of the inns were already close at hand, yet they paused by the riverbank and watched the Luo River flowing east. Ji Ying’s wide sleeves fluttered, and the moon’s reflection broke into fragments across the rippling water like a strip of white silk.

“Wait,” Ji Ying suddenly said, puzzled. “How did my father know that the reward of good and evil is real? Should he not have said that since we cannot know whether such rewards exist, we should simply stop worrying about it?”

Changde thought for a moment.
“Tell me, do you think ‘good’ means benefiting oneself or benefiting others? And does ‘evil’ mean harming oneself or harming others?”

Ji Ying let out an “Oh.”
“I see. If goodness means benefiting others, and human nature is that you help me and I help you, then good deeds naturally bring good return—and the same for evil. So Heaven’s Way lies in human hearts.”

She felt she had suddenly grasped a profound truth and showed the little dimple she had worn since childhood.

“If we say ‘I do not know’ or ‘I am uncertain’ about everything, that is not necessarily wisdom. It is only pretending to be enlightened.”

Changde looked at her with admiration.
“At such a young age, and you can already understand such profound principles!”

By now the southern quarter of Luoyang was deep in night.

Inns for merchant caravans lined both sides of the street. Colorful banners hung low. Most travelers had already taken rooms to rest, and only a few inns still showed dim candlelight.

Changde was wondering whom to ask when she saw six or seven ox carts stopped by the roadside. Tall stacks of cargo chests filled the carts and the ground beside them. Servants were loading wooden crates onto the carts. The master of the caravan sat sideways on an ox cart—a fat man wearing a fur hat and brocade robe, with two drooping moustaches and an energetic expression.

Changde politely asked the merchant whether, in all his travels across the world, he had heard of hermits living in different regions.

The fat merchant showed his yellow teeth.
“On Mount Yuquan in Jing Province, Mount Penglai in the Eastern Sea, and Motian Ridge in Yinping—there are immortals who have ascended to heaven.”

Ji Ying rolled her eyes upward.

Changde said, “We are not looking for immortals. We are looking for hermits.”

The merchant replied, “On Yuntai Mountain in Henei there are the Seven Sages of the Bamboo Grove.”

Ji Ying asked, “Are there hermits who do not live near Luoyang?”

The merchant thought for a moment.
“Mount Wudang in Jing Province, Mount Emei in Shu, and Mount Tiantai in Yue3.”

Ji Ying asked, “How do those hermits compare with the Seven Sages of the Bamboo Grove on Yuntai Mountain?”

“The world is vast,” the merchant replied. “Among the common people there are many extraordinary individuals—old and young, men and women—each with their own quirks.”

Changde and Ji Ying exchanged delighted glances.

“You two are still so young. Instead of studying, you want to go on some cultivation with hermits?” The merchant shook his head. “Those places are far from human settlements. You cannot reach them, and even if you did, you would not survive there.”

“You cannot reach them either?” Ji Ying asked in return.

“What would hermits buy from me? My caravan only travels between Luoyang and Chengdu in the state of Shu.”

Changde’s eyes lit up.
“Chengdu? The capital of Shu?”

“Indeed. Only by traveling between the capitals of two states can the profits support such a long journey.” The merchant pointed at several large wooden chests beside the road. “Much of the brocade worn in Luoyang is brought back by me from Chengdu. That bag of yours looks like Shu brocade as well.”
He pointed at Ji Ying’s qin bag.

“Are there any great hermits living in Chengdu?” Ji Ying asked.

“How could anyone live in seclusion in a capital city?” The merchant suddenly paused. “But come to think of it—there actually is.”

Ji Ying thought, “If Chengdu is no different from Luoyang in people’s hearts, it would be terribly dull.”
So she asked, “Is Sima Zhao in Chengdu?”

The merchant’s expression darkened. He glanced around and lowered his voice.
“There is no such person in Chengdu.”

“How does one get to Chengdu?”

The merchant chuckled and stroked his moustache.
“Before asking how to get there, you should ask how far it is—and whether you can make the journey.”

“A thousand li?” Ji Ying blurted out.

The Shu brocade merchant burst into laughter.
“Far more than that!”

He pulled out a rolled oxhide map from a small bamboo basket.
“Chengdu is three thousand li from Luoyang. It takes three months to travel.”

Ji Ying inhaled sharply. She had grown up in the Yuntai Mountains, and the journey to Luoyang had taken only five days.

Changde took the map and examined it carefully.
“Judging from this map, it is not quite three thousand li—perhaps only two thousand.”

The merchant scratched his head.
“You know better than I do? How do you calculate that?”

Changde traced a route across the map with her finger while calculating aloud:
“From Luoyang west to Chang’an in the Guanzhong region4. Then further west past Wuzhang Plains, south through the old San Pass road to Yang’an Pass, then down the Jinniu Road, passing Jiameng Pass, Jianmen Pass, and Mianzhu Pass. After that comes Chengdu. At most two thousand li.”

The merchant laughed heartily, his belly shaking.
“You scholars know only half the story! Wei and Shu have been fighting for years. The passes you mention are all contested military strongholds—strong bows, heavy crossbows, traps everywhere. How could merchants pass through there? Warfare is cruel—robbery, murder, corpses lying in the wilderness. Who would dare go that way and seek death?”

Changde flushed with embarrassment.
“Then how should one travel?”

The merchant began reciting place names while swaying his head:
“First go south past Liang County, Luyang, Zhi County, Wan County, and Xinye to Xiangyang. Then travel west upstream along the Han River past Zhuyang and Xi County. From Xicheng in Weixing Commandery turn southwest, cross the Shu border passes, continue southwest past Yihan and Dangqu, enter Micang Road through Dianjiang to Jiangzhou, then take the Qi-dao Road northwest past Deyang and Guanghan to Fuxian, and finally go south to Chengdu.”

Ji Ying and Changde studied the map. Indeed, this great detour made the journey nearly three thousand li.

Changde asked Ji Ying, “How about going with this caravan to Chengdu?”

Ji Ying felt tempted and nodded eagerly.

But the merchant waved his hands.
“You have not even asked me! Too troublesome. Not convenient.”

“How much money would make it convenient?” Changde asked.

The merchant shook his head.
“It is not about money. Truly inconvenient. My caravan only accepts ‘our own people.’”

“What do you mean by ‘our own people’?”

“You are complete strangers. My carts carry valuable goods. How can I know whether you are trustworthy?”

“We are students who study the writings of sages and the wise. How could we possibly do anything bad?” Changde said.

“When profit is involved, anyone can do bad things. If a reliable person recommended you, I might consider it—but my caravan leaves for Chengdu early tomorrow morning.”

Changde stepped closer and spoke softly.
“I belong to the Yang clan of Mount Tai. My aunt is the wife of the Zhongwu Grand General, and my father is the Director of the Palace Library. Would that suffice?”

The Shu brocade merchant looked doubtful.
“If you merely claim it, does that make it true?”

Changde reached inside her belt and produced a jade pendant. A phoenix soared among clouds, its emerald surface flawless.

The merchant said, “Your father is Yang Shuzi, Sir Yang5?”

Changde nodded slightly.

“But Sir Yang has no sons.” Suddenly the merchant slapped his forehead in realization.
“In Luoyang these days it is fashionable—women dressed as men, men dressed as women! Your gentle manner of speech reveals you are a woman.”

Changde blushed and placed a finger to her lips.

“Miss Yang wishes to go to Chengdu. Would Sir Yang agree?”

“It is my cousin who wishes to go.” Changde placed a hand on Ji Ying’s shoulder.

The merchant squinted at Ji Ying.
“This one is also a woman?”

Displeased, Ji Ying stared back at him.

The merchant rubbed his belly.
“The Yang clan once did my family a favor. Since you are a relative of Sir Yang, I will only charge travel expenses.”

He took out a small palm-sized counting frame from the bamboo basket and began calculating.
“To Chengdu—two hundred guan6.”

Changde’s mouth fell open.
“Two hundred guan? That could buy a small house!”

The merchant laughed.
“Three months of food and lodging, tolls, guards, and occasional bribes! If you endure some hardship and share a room with the servants, it can be cheaper—one hundred and fifty guan.”

Ji Ying thought, “Father forges swords for several days and earns only four or five guan.”

“Please be kind, uncle,” Changde pleaded. “My family will owe you a favor.”

The merchant rolled his round eyes.
“You may ask around. No one will take you to Chengdu for less. In fact, I doubt anyone else would dare accept you.”

Changde turned to Ji Ying helplessly.
“I must borrow money from my aunt.”

“We leave early tomorrow morning. There must be no delay.”

Ji Ying thought, “Father taught me smithing and the qin so that I would not need to depend on the world, so that I could live as I wished. Changde has already helped me so much—how can I let her spend money for me again?”

“No need to borrow,” Ji Ying said, shaking her head.
“As we pass through inns along the road, I can play the qin for money. Whatever I earn will go to you.”

The Shu brocade merchant frowned slightly.
“You are a qin musician? But the inns are full of drinkers. They do not like listening to music—they prefer looking at pretty girls.”

Ji Ying gritted her teeth and forced a charming smile.
“What if a pretty girl plays the qin?”

The merchant leaned closer to examine her face.
“You have fine bone structure. With a bit of makeup, smiling at guests like that, saying a few flattering words—‘Thank you, honored sir, for your gift’—that would work.”
He examined Ji Ying’s hands.
“Your fingers are slender. They do look like a musician’s hands. Is your arm injured?”

“It does not matter,” Ji Ying replied.

She opened the silk wrapping and laid the qin across the ox cart. Though her left arm was tightly wrapped and moved stiffly, she could still press the strings with her fingers.

With her right hand she began to pluck casually. The music was deep and solemn—the melody of “High Mountains.” Ji Ying had studied the qin since childhood and knew this piece by heart. She barely looked at the instrument as her ten fingers moved rapidly. One note had not yet faded before another arose—plucking, brushing, hooking, striking. The music changed swiftly like flying shadows.

Tonight the “High Mountains” were far from calm. It seemed as if cliffs rose suddenly from the earth, stones falling, forests shaking, startling a flock of white egrets into flight.

Changde stared at Ji Ying in amazement.

The merchant nodded repeatedly.
“Not bad. It would be even better if you played more slowly. What other pieces do you know?”

Ji Ying recited casually:
“‘White Snow,’ ‘Clear Horn,’ ‘Green Water,’ ‘Pure Zhi,’ ‘Tang Yao,’ ‘Weizi,’ ‘Western Qin,’ ‘Lingyang,’ ‘Ba People,’ ‘Eastern Wu,’ ‘Mount Tai,’ ‘Flying Dragon,’ ‘Deer’s Cry,’ ‘Kun Bird,’ ‘Wandering Strings,’ ‘Lady Wang Zhao,’ ‘Chu Consort,’ ‘Thousand Miles,’ ‘Parting Crane’…”

The merchant’s eyes shone.
“If you truly play well, I can bring you to banquets held by magistrates and governors. If the officials enjoy the music, business negotiations go more smoothly. The reward would be far more than a few coins.”

Ji Ying did not like the thought of flattering officials.
“Only if you take me to Chengdu—to seek hermits.”

The merchant replied, “All your earnings will belong to me. Do not regret it later.”

Ji Ying and Changde looked at each other in delight.

Suddenly they heard soldiers shouting behind them and the urgent pounding of hooves. Ji Ying turned around and saw distant torchlights flickering on the Luo River stone bridge, illuminating a procession of carriages and riders. A large banner waved at the front of the convoy.

Seeing that they were government troops, Ji Ying said quickly,
“Why not go inside the inn first, and listen to me play a few more pieces?”

“Very well,” the merchant said. “But I am waiting for this official. After he leaves, I will come listen.”
He pointed toward the approaching convoy.

“Then we will go inside first.”

Ji Ying held the qin in her left arm and pulled Changde along as they hurried into the inn. Many crates were stacked densely and high before them. They slipped between the cargo boxes. The wooden chests were carved with designs of pines rising above clouds, and even the openwork carvings of flowers and grasses were finely done. The fragrance of camphorwood filled the air.

The noise of the approaching convoy grew louder—shouts of men and horses.

Suddenly a soldier bellowed,
“The Commandant of the Capital Region, Marquis of Dongwu7, has arrived!”

Ji Ying looked around quickly. The inn had only the main entrance and no other exit.
She thought in alarm, How does Zhong Hui know I am here?

Ji Ying and Changde pushed the wooden crates in front of them together, leaving only a slit as narrow as a fingernail through which they could watch outside.

Zhong Hui, dressed in blue brocade robes, stepped down from his ornate carriage. He strode forward with imposing authority, surrounded by soldiers, and approached the Shu brocade merchant. The merchant climbed down from his cart and bowed in greeting. The two men stood no more than ten steps from Ji Ying’s hiding place.

“Thank you for coming personally, my lord,” the merchant said. “Is there good news?”

Zhong Hui raised him up with both hands.
“Sir Tian will not return to Luoyang next time. You must go to Chang’an.”

“Chang’an? The Commandant of the Capital Region will also oversee Guanzhong?”

Zhong Hui gave a cold laugh.
“Zhenxi Grand General8, granted the imperial axe and authority, and Commander of all military affairs in Guanzhong.”

The merchant froze for a moment, then bowed again.
“Congratulations! Does that mean the matter is about to proceed?”

Zhong Hui returned the bow.
“It is thanks to Sir Tian risking danger and enduring hardship that the court now understands the opportunity is rare. Only then did they make their decision.”

“I dare not claim such credit,” the merchant said repeatedly, shaking his head. “I am only willing to be your loyal servant. What do you require for this journey?”

Zhong Hui replied,
“The arrow is already on the bowstring. It must be released. But we must first know where the target lies.”

The merchant nodded in understanding.

Hidden behind the crates, Ji Ying realized that Zhong Hui had not come to search for her, and she felt somewhat relieved.

Zhong Hui patted the merchant on the shoulder.
“If the matter succeeds—Hujun9, leading ten thousand troops.”

“Oh dear…” the merchant groaned, shaking his head repeatedly. “I do not know how to command soldiers. Such responsibility is too heavy.”

Zhong Hui threw back his head and laughed.
“A merchant must also manage his subordinates. It is no different from leading his detachments. Sir Tian will not risk himself to charge in to battles. Deeds that will be recorded in history are always contested by many. If someone else takes the chance to claim the merit earned by Sir Tian’s hard work, that would be a pity. What do you say?”

The merchant sighed and displayed a smile. He was about to answer when Zhong Hui suddenly changed expression and pointed toward the merchant’s ox cart.

“Where did this yellow brocade bag come from?”

Ji Ying’s heart skipped a beat.

The merchant paused before answering.
“…Goods from Chengdu. Does Your Excellency like this color?”

Zhong Hui grabbed the bag and examined it carefully.
“How did this bag end up with you?”

“Perhaps a servant moved goods and placed it there casually.”

Zhong Hui frowned, his voice sharp.
“Call out all your men and question them. I want the entire story. There may be a spy in your caravan!”

“That cannot be. They are all trusted men.” The merchant scratched his head.

“Call them at once! Better to kill by mistake than let one escape!”

“Uncle Zhong, greetings!”

Changde’s voice rang out.

At some point Changde had stepped out and now stood before Zhong Hui.
“Uncle Zhong, that silk bag is mine. I had just gone inside for a break.”

Zhong Hui stared at her with piercing eyes.
“So it is Yang Changde. Why are you out so late by yourself? How is Sir Yang these days?”

“Thank you for asking. My father has been slightly ill recently, but it is nothing serious. I only came to the south of the city for a walk after studying too long.”

Zhong Hui was silent for a moment before saying slowly,
“I will pay him a visit another day.”

Changde reached out to take the bag back, but Zhong Hui suddenly asked again,

“Do you know where this bag came from?”

“I bought it in the West Market.”

“From whom?”

“I do not remember.”

“Think carefully.”

“It has been several years. I truly do not remember.”
Changde coughed lightly.

Zhong Hui’s eyes never left her face.

Changde avoided his gaze.

“Hiding a criminal is a serious crime. Do you know that, Changde?”

Changde looked straight back at him.

A faint sneer appeared at the corner of Zhong Hui’s mouth.

“The Yang clan has been honorable for generations. Do not let it be ruined by you. Tell me who owns this bag, and I will not pursue the matter.”

Changde took a deep breath and answered with a trembling voice,
“Accusing a good man of being a criminal—no matter how many generations pass—that too cannot be called honor.”

Zhong Hui remained expressionless. He looked once at Changde, then at the merchant. Without another word he turned slowly and stepped back into his ornate carriage.

Soon the shouts of men and horses faded away, and the dust settled in the street.

The merchant quickly shoved the brocade bag back into Changde’s arms.
“Miss Yang will ruin my affairs!”

Changde pressed her hand to her chest and apologized repeatedly.

After a long while, Ji Ying emerged from behind the crates and pulled Changde aside.

“This merchant works for Zhong Hui. I should not go with him.”

Changde thought for a moment and whispered,
“He does not know who you are. And he did not betray you just now.”

Ji Ying wanted nothing more to do with Zhong Hui and only shook her head.

Changde added,
“He leaves for Chengdu tomorrow. Such an opportunity may not come again.”

The merchant stroked his moustache.
“Your cousin truly is one of our own people, yes? Otherwise I would not dare take her.”

Changde took Ji Ying’s hand.
“Absolutely trustworthy.”

Ji Ying felt Changde’s cold fingers squeezing her palm.

She thought, Now that Zhong Hui is suspicious, it would be wrong to endanger Changde’s whole family. Let their noble clan remain in Luoyang and live their noble life. Once I reach Chengdu, I will have nothing more to do with this merchant. That is better than staying in Luoyang.

So she nodded and said,
“I have no interest in court politics or warfare. I only play the qin. The rest does not concern me.”

The merchant was silent for a moment before reluctantly nodding.

While the servants continued loading crates, Ji Ying played several more pieces in the inn.
“Yangchun” was warm and relaxed, “Baixue” slowly melted.10 She deliberately played more slowly, earning repeated praise from the merchant. Everyone felt somewhat reassured and began discussing the journey for the next day.

Changde asked shyly,
“Since you often travel to Chengdu, I would like to ask about someone.”

The merchant smiled.
“Although Shu is small, Chengdu has twenty thousand households. Do you know this person’s ancestral commandery, name, courtesy name, or official position? Write them down.”

Changde took some rough paper and began writing.

Ji Ying continued playing, her thoughts drifting to the days in the bamboo grove of Yuntai Mountain—her father teaching her the qin, her mother teaching her younger brother to read. Now heaven and earth separated them; soon their family would scatter like stars.

“Ah,” the merchant said softly after reading Changde’s note. His face quickly returned to normal.
“What does Miss Yang wish to know about him?”

“…Any news will do,” Changde answered.

The merchant swallowed.
“By Heaven’s creation—you have asked the right person. He… was still alive this April.”

Changde’s lips trembled. Tears ran down her face.

After a long moment she took another deep breath and asked in a shaking voice,
“Has he married?”

Ji Ying understood and laughed quietly to herself. Her finger slipped slightly on the string, and a note rang wrong—as if in the middle of spring a clump of white snow had fallen from a branch.

The merchant showed his teeth but hesitated to speak. At last he only shook his head slowly.

Changde seemed half smiling and half crying, yet her tears flowed even harder.

The Shu brocade merchant sighed deeply.
“The two states are enemies. It involves military secrets and the lives of many people. It is not something to discuss freely. I can only tell you this—if Heaven protects him, he may return soon.”

Changde wiped away her tears, trembling with emotion.

The merchant thought for a moment and added,
“Swords and spears show no mercy, and human hearts change easily. Who knows fortune or disaster for any of us? Tonight there is music—tonight there is drink.”

He took out a jug, pulled out the stopper, and gulped from it.

Suddenly Changde leaned forward with both hands on the mat and pleaded,

“Could you carry a letter to him?”

The merchant shook his head violently.
“Absolutely not! Far too dangerous!”

“It is only a letter,” Ji Ying said, stopping her playing. “Why be so nervous?”

“The borders are searched carefully. If they accuse me of ‘treasonous correspondence with the enemy,’ it would mean extermination of three families!”

Ji Ying thought, Even a love letter counts as treason? If they read it aloud, perhaps they would get goosebumps.

“I will not write about state affairs,” Changde pleaded.

The merchant rolled his eyes.
“…Very well. Thirty characters at most. Write each character small—no bigger than a fingertip.”

“Thank you! Thank you!”

Changde began writing to her beloved.

Ji Ying thought, Although worldly rules do not bind me, if I am to travel far I should still say farewell to my family.

She took a sheet of rough paper, cut it in half with a knife, and quickly wrote two letters.

The first read:

“For the great kindness of raising me, I will never forget it all my life. Past quarrels fade like morning dew.
I follow Father’s great aspiration—gathering wild herbs in mountain valleys, letting my hair loose among cliffs and caves.
If Mother finds a worthy man to entrust herself to, she need not worry about worldly reputation. Follow your own nature.
—Your daughter Ying.”

The second read:

“Father once said: ‘A man without ambition is not truly a man.’
I wish to roam the four seas, like the great fish Kun of the northern ocean or the great bird Peng of the southern ocean. I cannot remain another day trapped in a filthy pen of pigs and dogs.
Care for Mother in my place and hold firm to your resolve. One day we will meet again and speak of our journeys.
—Your sister Ying.”

Ji Ying blew gently to dry the ink.

Changde, eyes still wet with tears, handed over her small note. The merchant avoided reading it, folded it, and tucked it into his robe.

Ji Ying gave her two letters to Changde, who carefully stored them.

“Thank you for taking care of me, Sister Changde. All people live under the same Heaven and Earth. Even if we are three thousand li apart, we still share the same world. If we meet again someday, I will repay your kindness.”

Changde wept again and embraced Ji Ying tightly.

“My sister is smart and sincere. You will surely find worthy friends and broaden your knowledge in your travels. When we meet again, you must tell me everything you have learned.”

Ji Ying nodded.

Changde walked away, turning back every four or five steps. Ji Ying waved farewell.

At dawn the next morning, rosy light appeared along the horizon. A line of ox carts slowly departed from the southern gate of Luoyang.

Ji Ying looked back at the distant palace city of Luoyang, which resembled a small mountain on the horizon. She remembered the days in Yuntai Mountain, the Seven Sages of the Bamboo Grove discussing philosophy beside a jade-green pool.

But the bamboo grove of Yuntai had already withered, and of the Seven Sages only six remained.

“I have not yet asked your name or where you come from,” the Shu brocade merchant said, turning around. “You do not look like a member of the Yang clan.”

Ji Ying replied,
“And I have not yet asked your name either. You are not truly a brocade merchant.”

The merchant slapped his belly and laughed loudly.

“Young people are formidable! Life is like a boat floating on a river. Sometimes people share the same boat for a short time—why hurry to harm each other? I will call you ‘Qin Player,’ and you may call me ‘Merchant.’ How about that?”

Ji Ying nodded.

She moved a crate aside and took out her qin bag. Removing the yellow silk covering, she was about to throw it from the cart, but stopped. Suddenly she felt a strange warmth in her heart.

She remembered a joyful afternoon when she was nine years old—the last gathering of the Seven Sages in the bamboo grove. Her father had played a piece then.

From vague memory she began playing it, slowly and with pauses.

"Twin phoenixes hide the brightness of the sun,
their wings folded on the cliffs of Mount Tai.

They raise their heads to drink the morning dew,
then warm their feathers in the rising light.

They cry aloud and play among the clouds,
then descend to rest beside the orchid pool.

They think themselves beyond the dust of the world,
their freedom never to be broken.

But who expected a troubled age—
when hunters came to cast their nets?

Cloud-nets spread across the four horizons,
high snares hanging everywhere.

They struggle to rise, yet cannot escape;
their six wings find no room to beat.

At last their hidden grace is bound by cords,
caught by the age and held in place.

One lone hero breaks away and flies,
crying in grief for those left behind.

He circles back to mourn his companions,
singing sadly above the mountain slopes.

When birds are gone, the good bow is stored away;
when schemes are spent, the body meets danger.

Though fortune and ruin lie partly in oneself,
the roads of the world are steep and perilous.

How could one return to the first pure life,
holding jade and guarding rare treasures?

To wander freely in the clear heavens,
hand in hand forever."

Ji Kang, Five-Character Ancient Verse

Ji Ying imagined a future where she would ignore worldly affairs—playing the qin, forging iron, growing vegetables, bathing in mountain springs, discussing profound ideas with a few close friends.

Would that not be wonderful?

A small dimple appeared at the corner of Ji Ying’s mouth.

1 Sun Deng and Wang Lie (孫登, 王烈): Two reclusive figures admired in early medieval anecdotal tradition. Sun Deng was famed as a wandering Daoist-style hermit, while Wang Lie was remembered in Henei for austere learning and withdrawal from public life.

2 On Dwellings Without Auspicious or Inauspicious Influence upon Life (Zhaiju wu jixiong she sheng lun, 宅居無吉凶攝生論): A debate text associated with Ji Kang and Ruan Kan (courtesy name Deru, 德如). It argues over whether omens, house sites, divination, and spirits truly affect human fortune.

3 Mount Wudang, Mount Emei, and Mount Tiantai (武當山, 峨眉山, 天台山): Three famous mountains linked in later tradition with recluses, Daoist cultivation, and transcendents. In the imagination of the time, such places stood at the far edge of ordinary society.

4 Chang’an and Guanzhong (長安, 關中): Chang’an was the great imperial capital of the Han dynasty. The surrounding Guanzhong basin, protected by mountain passes, formed the strategic heartland of northwestern China and remained a key military region during the Three Kingdoms period.

5 Yang Shuzi (羊叔子): “Shuzi” is the courtesy name of Yang Hu (羊祜, b. 221), the eminent Jin statesman and general. The prestigious Yang clan had many ties to high office.

6 Guan (貫): A string of bronze cash coins tied together. In the late Han and Three Kingdoms period, two hundred guan was an enormous sum—far beyond ordinary travel money.

7 Marquis of Dongwu (東武亭侯): Zhong Hui's noble title at the time. Early medieval officials often held both office titles and ennobled ranks, so a formal announcement might name either or both.

8 Zhenxi Grand General (Zhenxi da jiangjun, 鎮西大將軍): A top western campaign command. “Granted the imperial axe” (假節鉞) refers to the ritual token authorizing a commander to exercise exceptional military power on campaign.

9 Hujun (護軍): A senior military command post concerned with supervising troops and their subordinate commanders. It is not merely “guard duty,” but a substantial field appointment associated with an army staff structure.

10 Yangchun and Baixue (陽春, 白雪): Ancient qin melodies later paired in the phrase “Spring Sun and White Snow,” which came to suggest elegant, refined music appreciated by only a few listeners.
