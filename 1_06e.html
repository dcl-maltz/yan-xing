Yanxing: The Rekindling of Han · Book One: The Land of Heavenly Bounty

Book One: (VI)

← Book One (V) Contents → Book One (VII)

Jiang Kang and Yan Hong were said to be coming to the Imperial Academy square in the evening.

Xiaoyu and Zhuge Shang were still in the city barracks; a servant had been sent to let them know and ask them to come quickly to make the apology.

Ji Ying and Zhuge Mao were walking together toward the Furong teahouse when they heard, even before reaching it, the clamour rising from the direction of the Imperial Academy square.

The day was stifling, and the noise from the square seemed heavy and sluggish with it.

That night, she had run out of the teahouse in that very direction — to find Xiaoyu and Zhuge Shang, to report Jiang Kang as a spy.

"So that night, when Xiaoyu and the others were going into the teahouse to catch the spy — you didn't know about it in advance?"

Zhuge Mao shook his head several times: "If I had known, I certainly wouldn't have let Miss Ji leave. Perhaps gone to the back kitchen, hidden for half a shichen, and none of this trouble would have followed." He glanced toward the crowd in the direction of the square, then added: "Who can say? Yan Hong and the others had already made up their minds to bring people in and take over the square. With the tide running like this, all our efforts may well come to nothing."

Zhuge Mao let out a quiet sigh.

The further they walked toward the Imperial Academy square, the louder the wailing and shouting grew. Cake-sellers, firewood carriers, and white-haired old men leaning on walking sticks had all stopped to watch.

In the square, some fifty or sixty young men in scholars' blue robes and broad-sleeved gowns had gathered, along with several women in loose hair — most in the dress of Confucian students. In front of the gate tower steps, several wide white banners had been erected, bearing the neatly written characters: End the Wars, Bring Peace to the People and The People Are the Root of the State.1 To one side someone had spread out a few worn-through sandals, a tattered coat of armour, and broken swords — as though these were relics gathered up from some battlefield.

"Elder Brother Li! Brother Chen!" Zhuge Mao beamed and walked toward two scholars sitting on the stone-flagged ground. One wore grey-white cloth and had slightly sunken eyes, with a patch of white hair across his brow; the other was pale, lean, and wore a habitually stern expression. These were the same two who had shared Zhuge Mao's table at the teahouse — the thin one was Chen Shou.

Zhuge Mao first bowed to the middle-aged man: "Elder Brother Li — no classes today?"

The man smiled ruefully: "My students have walked out! A few of them are in the square, so I came to sit with them for the afternoon."

Chen Shou said: "I finished today's work at the Imperial Library ahead of time, so I am free to come to the square."

Ji Ying looked at the gate tower that marked the entrance to the Imperial Academy — about two storeys high, its surface carved with a frieze of carriages and horses in procession, banners and honour guards standing in perfect order. Today, to the right of the gate tower, two students were kneeling, begging bowls on the ground before them, though the bowls held not a single coin, left only for passers-by to see. Several others who were good with words stood on a makeshift wooden platform to the left, making speeches at the top of their voices, every other sentence about the hardships of the people.

Ji Ying thought back over her half-year in Chengdu: the shoulder-to-shoulder crowds in the markets, the coin trees flying off the shelves at the blacksmith's stall, the full and buzzing teahouse, the rolling waves of rice on the road to Chaozhen Guan and again on the road to the Zhuge family home. She had never seen a starving body lying in the street.

At the far end of the square — in the corner near the teahouse where she had first met Zhuge Shang and his friends — a few young men still stood scattered about. Today, however, they were no longer crying out for the restoration of the Han. Beside them stood a figure in military dress, someone she seemed to have seen once at the teahouse.

Ji Ying had barely sat down before she felt the heat rising off the stone slabs beneath her, and caught the smell of sweat from the surrounding crowd.

Zhuge Mao introduced the two men. The "Elder Brother Li" was Li Mi,2 formerly a lang in the Imperial Secretariat, and now a lecturer in Confucian thought and moral philosophy at the Imperial Academy. Chen Shou was a records lingshi at the Imperial Library.3 Ji Ying had little interest in official titles, but understood roughly that a lang ranked above a lingshi, and recalled that the Imperial Library was where the court kept its books and documents.

The classics of the moral tradition — one man lectured on them, another read them. Ji Ying had no inclination to ask.

She looked out toward the Imperial Academy inside the gate tower, which was quieter than usual. From a distance she could see several classroom doors standing half shut, a few desks left empty under the covered walkways, papers lifting in the wind and scattering across the ground.

The walkout was real today.

Still more students trickled out from under the gate tower and filtered into the crowd in the square.

"May I ask — why have the students walked out?" Zhuge Mao shifted where he sat.

Chen Shou said slowly: "They look on as the Later Han declines day by day, and they worry for the nation and the people. That is all." Then his voice quickened: "In the days of the Partisan Prohibitions of the Eastern Han, the students of the Imperial Academy already had the precedent of filing petitions and stepping forward bravely under the call of Grand Tutor Chen Fan and Governor of the Capital Region Li Ying.4 Nearly a hundred years on, the students of the Later Han once again set aside personal risk and stand up. It is an honour to be part of this!"

Li Mi nodded: "The sages' books were not read in vain."

Ji Ying thought of the three thousand Imperial Academy students in Luoyang's Eastern Market the year before, who had knelt and pleaded for her father's life.

Suddenly Li Mi narrowed his eyes: "Is this not the young woman who played the qin at the teahouse that evening?" He folded his hands and bowed: "So you are the daughter of Ji Kang of the Seven Sages. I am far away here in the southwest, and yet even I have the deepest respect for your father's courage in holding to his convictions against the tide of the times."

Chen Shou also bowed, his gaze still fixed on the stone slabs at his feet: "That night, when Jiang Kang spoke such offensive words — slandering your honoured father as someone who had incited the students to rebel — I failed to refute him on the spot. I have felt the regret of it for days."

Ji Ying returned the bow casually, but gave them both a small smile.

Zhuge Mao glanced at Ji Ying with a look, then asked in front of Li Mi and Chen Shou: "Miss Ji, you haven't told us yet — why did you come to the Later Han last year?"

Tell them the truth, then.

"My father lived in seclusion and held no office. He only lectured on Laozi at the Imperial Academy. When the numbers listening grew too large, Sima Zhao and Zhong Hui could no longer tolerate him — they trumped up charges and took his life. A place that rotten, I had no wish to stay."

Li Mi and Chen Shou shook their heads and sighed.

Zhuge Mao asked: "In Miss Ji's view, what is the greatest difference between the people of the Later Han and the people of Wei?"

Ji Ying gazed off at the Furong teahouse sign in the distance.

No arrests. No crime. No suspicion. Room for a few words…

"…Open hearts. And kindness."

Zhuge Mao went on: "Just a few years ago, was the emperor of Wei not killed by Sima Zhao's men? Before he died, the emperor said something that became famous: 'Sima Zhao's intentions are known to every man in the street.'5 Even when everyone in the street could see it, no one dared say it aloud — they were afraid their neighbours would report them. The people of the Later Han would never do such a thing."

Was he not already in Chengdu by then?

Zhuge Mao gave Ji Ying a small smile.

Li Mi shook his head; Chen Shou continued staring at the stone slabs, lost in thought.

Zhuge Mao then asked: "And so — does Miss Ji prefer the Later Han?"

Ji Ying gave a reluctant nod: "I have no plans to go back."

"It seems the Later Han has truly won your heart." Zhuge Mao said this slowly and deliberately.

Chen Shou sat up straight and looked to the sky: "That the people of the Later Han are kind and open-hearted, and set visiting guests at ease — that much is true." Then he gave a short, dry sound. "But in the court — to be kind and broad-minded means to get along agreeably with everyone, to say only what people want to hear, to be agreeable on all sides and well liked by all."

Li Mi nodded: "What a waste that Chen Chengzuo is only a petty archivist." Then he smiled ruefully: "That was not meant as a flattering remark."

"In time…" Chen Shou was about to continue when a long groan rose from somewhere nearby.

"All lofty talk of principle — and who spares a thought for the suffering of the living!" a young voice rang out.

At some point the grey clouds had covered the midday sun, and the stifling drowsiness of the afternoon clung to the stone ground and pressed up against every leg and foot. Ji Ying looked around the square and was surprised to find how crowded it had grown; where before they had been sitting at the edge of the crowd, they were now close to its centre. The sound around them was no longer laughter and greetings — it was mostly complaints and sighs.

Chen Shou spoke again, in a low, unhurried voice: "Toward the end of the Eastern Han, the court was full of people who took up offices and drew their pay but accomplished nothing — gilded on the outside, rotten within. Those with genuine talent and principle had no path to advance, and withdrew one after another into seclusion. And so, with villains and traitors in power, there was almost no one left to stop them. Now, the Later Han is not far from that same…"

While he was still going, from the middle of the square came another shout: "The court is packed with idle officials who produce nothing!"

Chen Shou nodded and murmured to himself: "I once calculated — one million people, supporting forty thousand officials?" He began counting on his fingers, his lips moving.

Its mandate renewed. The Later Han princess and her loom. The study with its patched paper window. The plain family supper…

"As things stand — what is most rotten in your nation's court?" Ji Ying asked.

Li Mi gave a short laugh and said nothing.

Chen Shou shook his head: "Too many things to name!" He raised fingers one by one: "Year after year of costly wars abroad, draining talent and resources. The whole court, from top to bottom, visiting and entertaining one another, heaping praise on those who deserved none. Honours, high offices, noble ranks, and posthumous titles heaped on those with no real accomplishment. The most solemn instruments of government, reduced to playthings!"

Chen Shou had grown louder as he spoke, until the last few words were almost shouted. Zhuge Mao did not look at him, but quietly laid one hand on Chen Shou's blue-cloth shoe.

So Chen Shou was on their side too?

"Yi Province grows weak, Ba-Shu faces hardship!" came another shout from nearby.

His feelings now roused, Chen Shou swept his gaze across the students in the square and raised his arm: "Since you have studied the thought of the sages, you should follow their example — stand up at this moment and be the steady pillar in the middle of the current!" He turned back to Li Mi: "There are some old classmates of ours — if they could be brought to the square too, that would help."

Li Mi smiled ruefully: "They are no doubt keeping themselves very busy sitting in the Imperial Secretariat drinking tea."

Chen Shou gave a low, contemptuous snort: "Wallowing in the same filth."

Li Mi added: "Without Chen Chengzuo and Zhuge Zimao doing the honest work at the Imperial Library, good old Xi would be in a sorry state."

Zhuge Mao gave a dry, polite laugh.

Ji Ying knew nothing of the workings of the court, and these two scholars were both so much older than her — she could not find a way into the conversation.

She only felt that among those who had devoted their lives to the Confucian tradition, there was a small portion who had not been entirely hollowed out by it. When they spoke, when they lowered their heads, when they folded their hands in greeting, they still kept something in them that refused to go along with the crowd. In them she glimpsed the shadow of the Luoyang Imperial Academy students of the year before — and, further back, the masters and disciples of a hundred years ago.

"Here they come." Zhuge Mao pointed toward the edge of the square, where a stir of heads was moving.

Ji Ying looked: Yan Hong led more than ten followers — wide-sleeved gowns, caps fastened properly, all in scholars' dark and sombre robes — and walked up onto the wooden platform amid the crowd, his followers taking up position behind him. The tall and fierce-looking Jiang Kang also came with more than ten men, all in short hemp tunics with cloth wraps around their heads — the look of men used to physical labour — and they planted themselves before the platform.

Li Mi gave a faint shake of his head.

Zhuge Mao said: "That night in the teahouse, it seemed you also had something to say about this Yan Hong and Jiang Kang?"

Chen Shou murmured: "Both the Yan and Jiang families are playing their own games — birds of a feather. If Yan Yu were to replace Jiang Wei, then Jiang Shu would…"

Ji Ying had not yet heard the end of it when Jiang Kang and his men thrust their arms in the air together and bellowed in unison:

"Battle after battle, and nothing to show — men sent out and never come home!"

"Protect the people, save the nation — remove Jiang Wei!"

For every eight-character chant from those below, Yan Hong and his faction on the platform echoed it back in perfect time, until the more than a hundred people gathered in the square were picking up the chant one after another, round after round, the fervour building without pause.

Ji Ying found it merely grating and tiresome, when a voice came from behind: "Good — we made it in time!"

Xiaoyu and Zhuge Shang had both changed into plain everyday clothes, and each carried a bulging brocade-wrapped bundle — the gifts of apology.

If we could just make the apology quickly, there would be no need to sit here suffering through all this shouting.

Ji Ying, Xiaoyu and Zhuge Shang exchanged glances and all gave a nod, then rose and started toward the wooden platform.

Zhuge Mao murmured: "No conflict. No mention of the northern campaigns or the restoration of the Han."

The square was fuller still — at least three or four times as many people as when Ji Ying had sat down, and more were being drawn in by the shouting every moment.

The students were busy chanting and paid no attention to the three of them, so Ji Ying had to watch every step, careful not to tread on anyone's hands or belongings. The makeshift wooden platform ahead was barely half the size of the teahouse, already crowded with Yan Hong and his people.

"Battle after battle, and nothing to show — men sent out and never come home!"

"Protect the people, save the nation — remove Jiang Wei!"

The shouting pressed in from every direction, right at her ears. Ji Ying wanted to cover them with her hands.

She watched Zhuge Shang go up to Jiang Kang and say a few words. Jiang Kang's expression shifted; he planted one hand on the platform and vaulted up, then stretched out both massive arms and waved them in the air, shouting: "The grandson of Zhuge Wuhou has something to say!"

Jiang Kang called out twice more, and the square fell rapidly quiet.

Zhuge Shang turned to face the students and called out clearly: "We have come specifically to apologise!"

A hundred pairs of eyes were on them. Ji Ying closed her eyes, drew a deep breath, and spoke: "I am the qin player from the Furong teahouse that night. I failed to verify the facts before acting — I mistakenly believed this Jiang Kang to be a Wei spy and reported him to the Yulin Guards."

Zhuge Shang proclaimed clearly: "The Yulin Guards should not arrest a person without evidence of a crime — that is not the law of the Later Han."

"This was our fault." Xiaoyu said, and with both hands held out the brocade bundle to Jiang Kang on the platform.

Ji Ying, Xiaoyu, and Zhuge Shang spoke together: "We are truly sorry!"

Jiang Kang burst out laughing, then waved the Confucian students behind him to stand aside and clear some space.

When Zhuge Shang, Xiaoyu, and Ji Ying had stepped up onto the platform, Jiang Kang called out: "Didn't catch that — say it again?"

Ji Ying kept her eyes open this time, and said through gritted teeth: "I wrongly identified this Jiang Kang as a Wei spy and went to report it to the Yulin Guards."

Zhuge Shang kept his face expressionless: "The Later Han has laws; a person may not be arrested without evidence of a crime. This was our fault, and we have come to apologise."

"Please forgive us." Xiaoyu said.

"Ha ha ha!" Jiang Kang's laugh shook the air: "You had someone labelled as a spy, and now two bundles of gifts are supposed to make it right?" He flipped open one of the bundles and glanced inside: "How do we know this isn't court money — paid to stop our mouths?"

"This is our own money, nothing to do with the court…" Zhuge Shang had not finished speaking when Jiang Kang flung up his arm and roared:

"A man born and bred in Yi Province — accused by no less than the grandson of the great Zhuge Wuhou of being a Wei spy!"

A chorus of jeers rose from the crowd below.

Jiang Kang swept his voice across the square: "Take a good look! The descendants of the Zhuge family have come today to admit their mistake! And they still talk about restoring the Han?"

Ji Ying saw many students in the crowd below nodding along.

Then came a voice from somewhere in the crowd below: "This grandson of his, hiding behind two women — how does that do honour to your ancestors' great name!"

Another wave of laughter from below.

Ji Ying saw Zhuge Shang's hands clenched into fists, his knuckles trembling; Xiaoyu reached out and wrapped one hand around them.

"Friends!" Behind Ji Ying, Yan Hong raised his ivory fan and spoke up in a loud voice: "Jiang Wei has lost battle after battle, and sends people to write treasonous words on the court pillars, then dispatches these few underlings — using the excuse of catching spies — to arrest respectable citizens at will! This defies all justice! Battle after battle lost — remove Jiang Wei!"

"Battle after battle lost — remove Jiang Wei!" The platform and the square erupted again in passionate chanting.

Zhuge Shang stared down at the wooden planks of the platform.

Suddenly Xiaoyu stepped forward to the centre of the platform and raised her hand: "I am Zhuge Yu, squad leader of the Right Yulin Guards!"

The crowd below slowly quieted again.

Xiaoyu's voice rang out like a struck bell: "Calling for rest and recovery for the people — that in itself is not wrong. On the border, there are victories and defeats alike; that is the nature of war. But spreading the word that the Han army is doomed to fail — that shakes the morale of our own troops. If the enemy hears of it, they will seize the moment to invade. When the time comes, with burning and looting, the peace and abundance of the Land of Heavenly Bounty will be no more!"

A hush fell over the square, and here and there students gave a nod.

Yan Hong stepped forward, fan cutting ahead of where Xiaoyu stood: "Our enemy is numerous and we are few — Jiang Wei lacks the military skill, only knowing how to hurl himself against the odds, gnaw at a pillar, and waste the nation's strength for nothing!"

Xiaoyu fired back: "Holding back a powerful enemy for decades, keeping the whole realm at peace — the Later Han needs exactly this kind of loyal, wise, and courageous commander to lead its finest troops!"

Yan Hong gave a cold laugh, looked sidelong at Xiaoyu, and called out to the crowd: "And you — you are not truly a Zhuge at all. Not even Han. So what gives you the right to lecture us about Han loyalty?"

Yan Hong's supporters on the platform, and Jiang Kang's men below, all broke into laughter.

"Never mind who I am!" Xiaoyu cried in fury: "Right now Grand General Jiang is only maintaining the military farm colonies — there is no plan for a northern campaign! With a powerful enemy at the gates, I ask you to put the wider situation first, and not tear ourselves apart from within!"

Yan Hong shouted back: "That argument has been made before! Jiang Wei has already launched eleven northern campaigns — within a year or two, he will certainly find some excuse to reinforce the front lines and march out for a twelfth! Jiang Wei has no sense of when Heaven's tide has turned — he insists on fighting the Sima house. The people of Yi Province have endured him for twenty years, and they can endure no more. He must be removed at once!"

Xiaoyu had no answer to this; and then Ji Ying heard Yan Hong say Heaven's tide, and in a single leap she was beside Yan Hong, calling out to the crowd: "The workings of Heaven are obscure, and no one knows their course. I grew up in Wei, and the people of Wei never felt that the Sima house had Heaven's blessing or the people's hearts. On the contrary, the treacherous ambitions of Sima Zhao were plain for all to see — the people of Wei only feared the Sima family's power; they did not truly love them. You eat the Later Han's grain and live under the Later Han's protection — do your duty first, and repay what you have been given. How can you put people's effort aside, and go about telling everyone in Chengdu that the Sima house is mighty and that Heaven's tide has turned to the Sima?"

Before Yan Hong could answer, Ji Ying fixed her eyes on him: "If you repay the Sima family this way, don't be surprised when people take you for a spy!"

From a corner of the square came a burst of cheering.

Platform and crowd alike had gone quiet; then Yan Hong, his face flushed red, turned on Ji Ying and screamed: "I love Yi Province — how dare you say you love Yi Province? Jiang Wei is a Wei man, and so are you — Jiang Wei and you are the real Wei spies, treating the Ba-Shu people as less than human! Go back to Wei where you belong!"

And then from below came a shout: "Outsider — get out!"

Ji Ying was taken aback for a moment, then swallowed her fury and yelled back at Yan Hong: "I am a person from Wei, and I was still willing to travel three thousand li to settle in Chengdu — is it not perfectly obvious which place I love? You speak of loving Yi Province at every turn, and yet the man you wish to put in the Grand General's place is — conveniently — your own father! And you still dare to stand at the gates of the Imperial Academy and call yourself a man of virtue! The treacherous ambitions of Yan Hong are plain for all to see!"

Ji Ying heard, from several corners of the square and from the direction where Zhuge Mao, Li Mi, and Chen Shou were sitting, a great deal of vigorous applause — but below, Jiang Kang's men were shouting in disorder, and the clapping was drowned out:

"Remove Jiang Wei!"

"Protect the people, save the nation!"

"The people bleed!"

"All empty talk of principle!"

Platform and square erupted into chaos.

Then Ji Ying caught a glimpse of something flying up from below — a black cloth shoe — she turned sideways and it sailed past, striking Yan Hong full on the chest. Jiang Kang produced from somewhere a wooden pole more than a zhang long, held it horizontally across his body, and together with Yan Hong's men on the platform herded the three of them into a corner. Thick-bearded and fierce-faced, he drove forward to shove the three of them off the platform. Xiaoyu, Zhuge Shang, and Ji Ying grabbed the pole with six hands and held it back with all their strength. Ji Ying glanced back — there was almost no room left on the platform behind them, and below was a mass of men, arms raised and voices howling. Further off in a corner of the square, a few of Zhuge Shang's friends were fighting their way toward the platform. She was pinned in, with nowhere to advance and nowhere to retreat, when from inside the Imperial Academy came the sudden sound of drums, gongs, and strings all playing at once —

Ji Ying followed the sound. Behind four or five musicians came a procession of several dozen people, their dress immaculate and brilliant, every garment clean and fine; the figure at the head was tall and upright, his features well-defined, carrying a white-plumed fly-whisk tilted at an angle in one hand, every movement unhurried and composed.

Every eye in the square was drawn to the elegance of this entourage.

Ji Ying heard students below murmuring:

"The son of Loyal Marquis — here he comes. He has his father's bearing!"

"Loyal Marquis?"

"The emperor's own right hand, not long ago — the one who was buried in great state, his bier lined with eight-chi coin trees. That family."

"Loyal Marquis Chen Zhi — this is his son, Chen Can."6

"The Chen family manner — always carries itself with a certain authority."

The young lord Chen Can led his followers in a sweeping procession toward the wooden platform, and the students sitting on the stone slabs parted of their own accord to let them through. Yan Hong ordered his people down from the platform; Chen Can did not offer any bow but walked straight up with several others dressed as grandly as himself.

The square fell silent in every direction.

"The line of Zhuge Wuhou has come to this — relying on a Western foundling and a Wei woman to advance their crooked arguments. Let me handle this." Chen Can's voice was cool and sharp, and the corners of his mouth betrayed a trace of proud disdain.

Chen Can's gaze swept over Zhuge Shang, then over Xiaoyu, then met Ji Ying's eyes. Ji Ying saw that his brows were finely cut and carried an air of high birth, his glance gracious and lordly — but lacking what she had glimpsed in Li Mi and Chen Shou.

Chen Can addressed the crowd: "You came today because you had wrongly accused someone — which means you were already in the wrong. You came to apologise — which means your position was already untenable. And now you argue back, simply because you cannot bring yourselves to admit it completely."

"You spoke of eating Han grain and doing one's Han duty — very well!" Chen Can raised the fly-whisk and swept it through the air: "Then tell me — Jiang Wei eats the nation's grain too. After eleven northern campaigns, how much land has he taken back? How many cities have been recovered? How many households has he added to the nation's count?"

1 The people are the root of the state (民惟邦本): A phrase from the Book of Documents (尚書), one of the oldest classics of Chinese statecraft; the full line reads: "the people are the root of the state — when the root is firm, the state is at peace." ↩

2 Li Mi (李密) ↩

3 Lang and lingshi (郎、令史): In the Han bureaucratic system, lang (郎) were mid-ranking officials who served as attendants or administrators within a government bureau, while lingshi (令史) were junior clerks responsible for keeping records and handling documents; a lang post carried considerably more prestige and a higher stipend than a lingshi post. ↩

4 Chen Fan and Li Ying (陳蕃、李膺): The leading moral figures of the Eastern Han court in the 160s CE, celebrated for their integrity and their championing of outspoken scholars; both were central targets of the Partisan Prohibitions, in which hundreds of officials and students who dared criticise those in power were purged, imprisoned, or barred from office for life. ↩

5 Sima Zhao's intentions are known to every man in the street. (司馬昭之心，路人皆知): These words were spoken by the Wei emperor Cao Mao (曹髦) shortly before he led a desperate armed sortie against Sima Zhao in 260 CE; Cao Mao was killed in the attempt. ↩

6 Loyal Marquis Chen Zhi and his son Chen Can (忠侯陳祗、陳粲): Chen Zhi (d. 258), posthumously titled the Loyal Marquis (忠侯), was the emperor's most trusted confidant and the dominant figure at the Shu Han court during his lifetime, known for cultivating close ties with the eunuch Huang Hao (黃皓). His oldest son Chen Can inherited the family's prestige. ↩
