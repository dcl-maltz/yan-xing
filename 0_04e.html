Yanxing: The Rekindling of Han · Preface

Preface (IV)

Ji Ying had earlier injured an execution guard at the execution ground. Now, seeing an officer approaching with a group of students from the Imperial Academy, she naturally wished to avoid questioning—yet there was nowhere to hide. In a moment, the two girls were surrounded.

The officer spoke. "At dusk today, rioters caused trouble in the East Market and killed a loyal soldier of the capital. One woman in white escaped. A report has come from good citizens that a suspicious person headed toward the Imperial Academy."

He pointed at Ji Ying in her white robe. "Answer truthfully. Where were you this evening?"

Changde took Ji Ying's left hand. "I am a student of the Imperial Academy. This is my younger sister visiting from our hometown. We spent the whole day studying. Because we were hungry, we went to the West Market to eat Hu-style roasted meat. We have only just returned to our dormitory and were about to study by lamplight. Sir, please show mercy and do not trouble us."

The officer narrowed his eyes suspiciously and turned to the students behind him. "Have any of you seen this person before?"

A young student lifted his candle high, lighting Changde's delicate face. "Yes! I have seen him! Whenever Ji Kang lectures at the Imperial Academy, he attends every session and always sits in the front row!"

Changde squeezed Ji Ying's hand more tightly.

The officer frowned. "You two are suspicious. If you cannot prove your innocence, you will come with me."

Changde stammered, unable to answer. Ji Ying thought to herself: If I surrender, I may lose my life just like Father—framed by Zhong Hui.

So she raised her voice. "What is this? Is it a crime now to listen to someone explain the Dao De Jing?1 Is the Imperial Academy no longer allowed to study?"

A young student suddenly stepped forward indignantly. "The Dao De Jing is a corrupt teaching that misleads people! What nonsense about 'abandon benevolence and righteousness and the people will return to filial piety and kindness,' and 'when families are not harmonious, filial piety appears.' These are harmful words!"

Fortunately, Ji Ying had heard Ji Kang explain these passages before. She answered at once. "The more benevolence and righteousness are forced from outside, the less they arise from the heart. Only when families are already in conflict do people talk loudly about filial piety. You only see the surface of the words and do not understand their meaning—yet you dare call others heretical?"

The young student was speechless and turned to look at an older scholar with a full beard beside him.

The old scholar spoke. "Ji Kang is a man of wild and reckless words. Let me give examples.

"First, in Divination of Doubts,2 he slanders our officials of Wei as 'cowardly and submissive, bending to orders,' and 'decorating falsehood, arrogant and mocking,' and compares himself to the great roc of Zhuangzi.

"Second, in Letter Breaking Ties with Shan Juyuan,3 he mocks the court as 'a kitchen of stinking meat' and 'a rotten dead rat.'

"Third, in Admonition to the Grand Tutor,4 he insinuates that the Duke of Jin5 harbors ambition and that ministers resent their superiors, even urging the emperor to 'cast aside corrupt favorites.'

"If such words are not heresy, what are they?"

Ji Ying thought: This narrow-minded dog remembers everything clearly.

She replied: "So that is your only talent—keeping grudges for the court. You read the classics of sages yet learned to become the court's hunting dog! At your age, do you feel no shame?"

The old scholar's lined face twisted. "Scholars must uphold moral teaching. That is the proper path of the world. If everyone behaved like Ji Kang—despising the court and talking about 'returning to nature'—would not the world fall into chaos?"

Ji Ying thought of her carefree childhood in the bamboo groves of Yuntai Mountain, so unlike the disorder she had seen today in Luoyang.

She answered: "The people farm, fish, hunt, raise their children, and care for their parents. Where is the chaos? It is the court's struggle for power and fame that corrupts the world—using crimes to destroy people, using wealth to tempt hearts, using authority to oppress the common folk. That is creating chaos out of nothing."

Changde stared at Ji Ying in shock.

"A young girl who understands nothing of the greater order," the old scholar said, his voice trembling hoarsely. "The state has its laws. Ji Kang defied authority and brought disaster upon himself."

Ji Ying replied sharply: "So speaking a few words and writing a few essays is defying authority? Then whose rebellion was it when the emperor of Wei bled at the palace gates?"

The students gasped.

The old scholar's lips trembled, and for a moment no one spoke.

Suddenly a bronze oil lamp toppled onto the stone floor. Oil spilled into the cracks, flames flickering and lighting the officer's boots.

The officer placed his hands on his hips, revealing the sword hilts beneath his armor. "I see now. You must be the female fugitive. Surrender, and I will not kill you."

"Sir, my sister spoke rashly, but she is no criminal. My... our father can testify…" Changde drew a slow breath.

The officer shook his head. He crossed his arms, drew both swords in a single reverse-handed motion—shua—each pointing at the girls' chests. "You will both come with me."

The short blades gleamed in the firelight.

Ji Ying hardened her resolve: If I fight, there may still be a chance.

She gritted her teeth and drew the dagger from her waist. With one motion she knocked aside the sword blade and thrust toward the officer's hand. The officer barely pulled back in time.

"You move quickly, girl," he sneered. "But can you move faster than me?"

Both swords struck toward Ji Ying's brow and throat.

She had never seen such deadly techniques. She dodged hastily and countered with her dagger toward his arm. Instantly his sword strokes changed direction like two silver snakes, striking low on the left and high on the right.

Ji Ying blocked the right attack with her dagger and leapt aside from the left. Seeing his chest briefly unguarded, she thrust straight forward.

The officer hurried to defend, but the dagger pierced his leather armor. Ji Ying slashed hard from left to right with all her strength.

The cloth split.

At the same moment she glimpsed the officer's other sword coming toward her. She could not evade in time. A sharp pain exploded in her left arm.

"Ah—!" Changde screamed.

Ji Ying looked down. Blood was rapidly spreading across her forearm, staining her white sleeve red.

The officer stepped back and inspected his wound. His armor had been cut open nearly a foot long. Reaching inside, his palm came away bloody. Without the armor, the blade would have cut deep into bone.

"Die, thief!"

He raised both swords again.

Just as Ji Ying prepared to fight to the death despite her wound, a figure stepped between them.

"Stop!"

Changde spread her arms. "Tell me your name and the name of your superior."

The officer sneered. "I am Qiu Jian. As for my superior… you probably wouldn't dare hear it. The Commandant of the Capital Region. That is enough for you to know. Step aside and do not obstruct the law."

Changde snorted. "Qiu Jian? Good. I will remember that."

She loosened her scholar's cap, removed it, and untied her headcloth. Her long black hair fell across her shoulders. "My aunt is the wife of the Zhongwu Grand General.6 My clan aunt is the mother-in-law of the Duke of Jin.5 My father is Director of the Imperial Library.7 No need to ask who they are, right? Withdraw now, and we will not pursue what happened just now."

Qiu Jian froze.

After a moment he lowered his head and sheathed both swords.

Ji Ying pressed her wound tightly. Hearing Changde's words, she felt a quiet shock: These titles mean nothing to me—but the Duke of Jin is Sima Zhao, the man who killed an emperor. He outranks even Zhong Hui. Changde is actually kin to the Sima clan?

Qiu Jian's expression softened. "My injury is nothing… but the fugitive killed a soldier. If I let her go, I will have trouble explaining it to the Commandant."

Changde cleared her throat and declared loudly: "Everyone who understands the truth knows that Master Ji was wronged. It was your superior who framed him! Killing an innocent man and slandering the most respected scholar of the realm will bring eternal shame to the court. Tell me—are you loyal to the court, or merely loyal to your superior?"

After a long silence, Qiu Jian shook his head helplessly. "I am a minor man with little voice. Matters of the court are beyond me. I only know how to follow orders and keep my small position."

"I understand. Handle it as you see fit. Your post will be safe."

He hesitated again. "…But these scholars may talk."

Changde looked around at the students. "This is my sister. Mind your words. We will still meet at the Academy. If you damage my family's reputation, it will not benefit any of you."

The scholars nodded quickly.

Qiu Jian bowed with clasped fists and ran off. The scholars picked up the fallen lamp and quietly dispersed.

The corridors of the Imperial Academy fell silent, filled only with the chirping of insects.

"Ah…" Changde's knees suddenly weakened and she crouched in front of Ji Ying, her face full of alarm. "Are you alright? Where are you hurt? Is it serious? Should we find a physician?"

Ji Ying was still pressing hard on the wound. Blood had already soaked part of her white sleeve.

"I avoided half of the strike. The cut is not deep—I can still move my arm, so the tendons are probably intact. I have had worse injuries when I was younger. Medicine and bandages will be enough."

Changde nodded repeatedly. "I have medicine in my room… I think." She reached out to support Ji Ying, but Ji Ying blinked and smiled. "My legs are fine. I can walk."

Guided by a few faint lamps outside the dormitory rooms, the two slowly made their way to a small, unremarkable chamber. Changde unlocked the iron latch, pushed open the wooden door, and after Ji Ying entered quickly barred it again. She lit two red candles and stacked several straw mats against the wall so Ji Ying could lean back and sit.

Ji Ying thought to herself: From the way Changde spoke and acted earlier, she does not seem like one of those petty court figures. Yet she used her connection to Sima Zhao to suppress Zhong Hui's men—and it worked. Perhaps people in Luoyang are all like this?

She spoke aloud: "So you really are related to Sima Zhao?"

Changde nodded. "My aunt is indeed the wife of Sima Shi, and a distant aunt of mine is Sima Zhao's mother-in-law. I had no choice but to use those connections just now."

"Thank you for helping me. But do you actually like being related to Sima Zhao?"

Changde shook her head. "If I had a choice, I would rather not be related to them at all. Many people have urged me to marry into the Sima family, but I have refused every time."

Ji Ying thought bitterly: Marriage among great clans is nothing but webs of connections. Insufferably vulgar. Then another thought came to her: But Mother herself was once related to the Cao imperial clan. Why did Father agree to that marriage? Perhaps he simply thought Mother was beautiful. Mother said that her grandmother was once famous for her beauty as well.

As Ji Ying drifted through these thoughts, Changde suddenly closed her eyes and murmured a quiet prayer: "I have failed Master Ji's teachings—but I did it for the sake of his orphaned children."

Suddenly she jolted awake. "Ah! Forgive me—I forgot about the medicine!"

She hurried to a wooden cabinet and began rummaging noisily. Ji Ying looked around the small room. It had no separate compartments and seemed barely large enough for one person to live in. Reed mats served as the bed. On a small desk, a clay candleholder weighed down several rough sheets of paper on which circles and squares had been drawn with numbers written beside them. A small box of dried lilac flowers sat nearby.

Along the wall stood several plain wooden chests stuffed with bamboo scrolls. Some had overflowed and were stacked beside them, each pile almost half a person's height, wooden tags tied to them bearing the names of Imperial Academy courses.

Several pieces of calligraphy hung on the mottled wall—strokes neat and solemn, resembling the stone inscriptions outside the academy gates.

Ji Ying read one silently:

Know the male, yet keep to the female,
and become the stream of the world.
Know the white, yet keep to the black,
and become the model of the world.
Know glory, yet keep to disgrace,
and become the valley of the world.

Ji Ying had never liked memorizing classics, but the words felt familiar—her father had once explained their meaning to her. She thought: Changde truly does not seem like someone from the Sima faction.

After a day that had brought her father's death, a desperate escape, and a life-and-death fight, Ji Ying leaned against the wall. The small room was quiet; Changde was gentle. Exhaustion washed over her, and her right hand trembled slightly as it pressed against the wound.

Soon Changde returned carrying several bottles and boxes. She uncorked a long black bottle first. Ji Ying slowly rolled up her sleeve.

The wound was three or four inches long, cutting deep into flesh.

Changde poured brown powder onto it. Ji Ying endured the sting.

"Ah!" Changde cried. The powder piled into a little mound on Ji Ying's arm; much of it spilled onto the floor. "I'm sorry! I'm sorry!"

Ji Ying calmly spread the powder with her finger and pinched the excess back into the bottle.

Next Changde opened a small porcelain bottle labeled Metal Wound Medicine8 and poured red powder onto the cut. This time the pain burned like fire—worse than being cut again. Ji Ying clenched her teeth as tears squeezed from the corners of her eyes. Changde apologized repeatedly and then opened a clay jar labeled Ma Fei Powder.9

Ji Ying suddenly recalled: When I was eight, I broke my leg kicking a brick kiln. Father mixed this powder into wine and made me drink it—I passed out, and when I woke the physician had already stitched the wound. Why didn't Changde let me drink it before putting on the medicine? That was agony.

Just then Changde lifted the jar as if to pour it directly onto Ji Ying's arm.

"Wait—this powder is for drinking. It must be mixed with wine."

"Oh!" Changde hurriedly stopped. "I'm sorry! I have no wine here, but I can go borrow some. How much do you need?"

"One bowl is enough. But once I lose consciousness, can you stitch the wound?"

Changde looked mortified. "Me? Stitch a wound? …I can barely sew clothes. To be honest, I've never done it—I always take things home to have them mended… Can you stitch it yourself?"

Ji Ying thought: Then I cannot drink the Ma Fei Powder. I'll just have to endure the pain.

"It's not necessary. The cut does not seem too deep—we can simply bandage it. If I avoid moving the arm, it should heal in a month or two."

"But… I have heard that without stitching it will leave a scar."

Ji Ying laughed softly. "I'm not afraid. I already have many scars."

Changde gave a rueful smile and took out several pieces of cloth. Ji Ying chose a simple white strip; Changde cut it into long bands. Soon Ji Ying's forearm was wrapped into a thick white column, the bleeding no longer visible.

Changde wiped sweat from her forehead. "Ji Ying, I envy you so much. You're only fifteen, yet already so clever and brave—and skilled in martial arts. When I was fifteen… I only knew how to bury myself in books and daydream about romance."

Ji Ying thought: Girls from powerful families are raised in comfort—it's no wonder. Yet Changde lives alone at the academy and does not seem to be caught up in any romance.

She asked: "So you have not found someone to marry?"

Changde laughed—an awkward, hollow laugh—her gaze suddenly vacant. "Tell me… you truly had no use for that Zhao Jun?"

Ji Ying frowned. "We have different ideals. Why say more."

Changde nodded slowly. "Perhaps I can think the same way. My… beloved comes from a very different family background. Even if we forced ourselves together, we might not be happy. And now we are thousands of miles apart…"

Ji Ying felt a quiet distaste: In Luoyang, people judge marriage by family rank rather than by ideals. Insufferably vulgar.

She replied: "My father used to say that the true person takes heaven and earth as home. Why cling to marriage and bind oneself to another?"

Changde suddenly shuddered. "Ah! I nearly forgot!"

She ran to another large chest, pulled out several cushions, and then retrieved a long yellow silk bundle with black-patterned borders. Untying the red cord, she revealed Ji Kang's jade-decorated qin.

Seeing her father's instrument safe and unharmed, Ji Ying felt a wave of relief.

Her arm was tightly wrapped, so she bowed awkwardly with both hands. "Thank you, Sister Changde. I don't know how to repay you."

Changde waved her hands quickly. "No need—it was only right for me to help. And if I had been braver earlier and forced that man Qiu Jian away sooner, you would not have taken that sword strike."

Ji Ying smiled. "When my arm heals, I'll play the qin for you."

Changde raised her thick eyebrows with delight. "Wonderful. My own playing is like that of a small child."

Ji Ying pushed the qin toward her. "Isn't it just pressing and plucking a few strings? Try a piece."

"I dare not. It is Master Ji's qin."

"You read my father's writings and study his thoughts. Why not play his qin as well?"

Changde no longer refused. She carefully removed the silk wrapping, cleared the desk of the candleholder, papers, and dried lilac flowers, and placed the qin upon it. Taking a deep breath, she sat upright.

She rolled up her purple sleeves.

Her fingers trembled slightly.

"Go on," Ji Ying urged.

"Don't laugh at me," Changde said.

Finally she began to play.

The sound was clear and distant. Though Ji Ying sat in a tiny dormitory room, she suddenly remembered the bamboo groves of Yuntai Mountain.

Tonight the bamboo seemed straight and orderly, with wide gaps between the stalks where the cool wind whistled through. In truth, Changde's fingers were stiff, her touch uneven; before plucking each string she paused to think.

Ji Ying recognized the melody—it was Flowing Water,10 the story of Bo Ya and Zhong Ziqi, the legendary friends who understood each other's music.

But tonight the flowing water did not run smoothly. It had not yet poured down from the mountains, and it was not clear water—it sounded thick and muddy, like resin, and sometimes even flowed backward. Before Changde had settled one string, she would accidentally strike another, plucking again and again to correct the mistake.

Ji Ying thought: So she truly was not being modest.

She endured the entire performance and forced an appreciative smile. "You played Flowing Water. When my arm heals, I will answer you with High Mountains." Then she thought of having to hear Changde play again and felt a slight regret.

Changde wiped the sweat from her palms onto her sleeve, delighted. "Excellent! You truly are my Bo Ya!"

Just as Changde was about to begin another piece, Ji Ying suddenly heard faint rustling metal armor from a distance. She quickly raised a hand to signal silence.

Changde hurriedly wrapped the qin again. From somewhere nearby came a loud pounding on a door.

"Open up! We are searching for rebels!"

"Not good!" Changde whispered urgently. "Hide in the chest!"

Ji Ying thought quickly: Someone must have reported us after Qiu Jian and the students saw me. Changde's connection to Sima Zhao may not protect us now. If they search the room, she will be implicated. The only solution is for me to escape and go to Uncle Shan Tao's house. But my blood-stained white clothing would make me too conspicuous. Changde cannot convincingly pass as a man… but perhaps I can.

"Sister Changde—do you have a student's robe? The darker the better."

"Yes!"

Changde understood immediately and rummaged through a chest. In moments she produced a dark green robe and a black cloth cap. Ji Ying leaned against the wall and began untying her clothing with one hand. Changde hurriedly turned away.

After a moment she said: "The officer said they were searching for a woman in white. Let me wear your white clothes underneath my own. If things become urgent, I will draw them away—they would never dare harm me."

"…Very well. Thank you."

So the two women changed clothes. Changde, facing Ji Ying as she removed her own student robe, felt rather awkward and looked away. Ji Ying, who had seen the Seven Sages of the Bamboo Grove bathing naked when she was young, felt no embarrassment. Seeing Changde's smooth, pale skin—unmarred by insect bites or scars, even and lustrous as white jade—she realized just how sheltered a life she must have led.

Soon Ji Ying was dressed in the dark student robe. A faint scent of lilac clung to the fabric. Changde hid Ji Ying's white clothing beneath her own garments and helped secure the scholar's cap.

Ji Ying's hair was too long and thick to fit beneath it. She asked Changde to bind it into a bundle, then drew her dagger, slid the blade up beneath the knot, and wrenched upward. The bundle of hair fell away with a sharp ca.

Changde's face showed a pained tenderness. She reached over and tied the cap strings.

The pounding on doors grew louder.

Changde peeked outside. No soldiers were yet in sight.

Ji Ying grabbed the qin, and the two slipped out of the academy. Avoiding the main roads, they took winding alleys. Fortunately no one stopped them. Beneath a sky full of cold stars and faint lanterns, the two women hurried through the night.

Ji Ying glanced down at the yellow silk bundle in her hands. It was the one Zhong Hui had once brought to them, containing that twisted treatise Four Fundamentals. Her father had refused to burn the silk wrapping, saying the cloth itself was innocent.

But now Zhong Hui had caused her father's death.

Ji Ying wished only to be as far from him as possible.

At a fork in a narrow alley, Changde examined a brick wall marker and began tracing directions with her hand while murmuring calculations.

Ji Ying thought: Even if we reach Uncle Shan Tao's house, I have already killed a guard. I will spend my life hiding from the authorities. And this Luoyang is full of corrupt officials and family connections—the whole province is under Zhong Hui's control, with ruthless men like Qiu Jian serving him. Living here in constant fear.

They turned several more corners before Changde slowed to a stop. "Oh no… I miscalculated. At the last intersection we should have turned right to reach Shan Juyuan's house."

Ji Ying suddenly remembered something her father once said: "Ordinary people chase glory. Only those who can leave it behind find true joy."

She had always admired her father's independent spirit—traveling to meet wise men and seeking knowledge. She thought: Father taught me the qin. After hearing Changde play tonight, I know I can earn a living by it. I have his dagger, and today I proved my martial skills can protect me. Why not leave this filthy place and wander the world as he did? Even if fate turns against me and I starve in a mountain bamboo forest, my body will return to nature rather than fall to the swords of Zhong Hui and Sima Zhao.

"Is the robe too thin? You're trembling."

Ji Ying shook her head. "I'm not cold. But I don't want to go to Uncle Shan's house anymore. That is not my destination."

She took Changde's hand. "Thank you for everything. Please tell my mother and my younger brother that I am going to travel the world."

Changde stood stunned for a long time before sighing deeply. "You truly are Ji Kang's daughter. Where will you go?"

1 Dao De Jing (道德經, also known as the Laozi): The foundational text of Daoist thought, arguing that imposed morality distorts rather than improves human nature. Its teachings put it in direct tension with the Confucian orthodoxy that the Wei court used to legitimize its authority.

2 Divination of Doubts (Buyi, 卜疑): A satirical essay by Ji Kang that mocks Wei officials as servile and hypocritical, comparing his own aspirations to the great roc of Zhuangzi. The form—an imaginary dialogue between a questioner and a sage—was a classical Han genre Ji Kang turned to subversive ends.

3 Letter Breaking Ties with Shan Juyuan (Yu Shan Juyuan juejiao shu, 與山巨源絕交書): Ji Kang's open letter rejecting Shan Tao's offer to recommend him for a court post; "Juyuan" (巨源) is Shan Tao's courtesy name. It is widely believed to have contributed directly to Ji Kang's execution.

4 Admonition to the Grand Tutor (Taishi zhen, 太師箴): A poetic essay warning against corrupt favorites and hinting unmistakably at the Sima family's ambitions. It was addressed to the nominal head of state but read by everyone as a rebuke of real power.

5 Duke of Jin (Jingong, 晉公): The title held by Sima Zhao at this time—the ambition Ji Kang's essay had dared to name.

6 Zhongwu Grand General (忠武大將軍): The posthumous honorific of Sima Shi (司馬師, 208–255), Sima Zhao's elder brother, who deposed one emperor and installed another before dying on campaign. "Zhongwu" (忠武) means "loyal and martial."

7 Director of the Imperial Library (mishujian, 秘書監): The official responsible for imperial books, archives, and astronomical records—a position of considerable prestige close to the court.

8 Metal Wound Medicine (jinchuang yao, 金創藥): A standard battlefield dressing of the Han–Jin period, typically a red or brown powder applied directly to cuts and puncture wounds to stanch bleeding and prevent infection. The burning sensation it caused was well known.

9 Ma Fei Powder (mafei san, 麻沸散): An anesthetic attributed to the physician Hua Tuo (c. 140–208), dissolved in wine and drunk to induce unconsciousness before surgery. Unlike Cold-Food Powder, which was a stimulant, Ma Fei Powder was purely medicinal.

10 Flowing Water and High Mountains (Liushui 流水, Gaoshan 高山): Two celebrated qin pieces traditionally associated with Bo Ya (伯牙) and his friend Zhong Ziqi (鍾子期), the only listener who could truly hear what Bo Ya's music expressed. Their friendship became the classical image of zhiyin (知音)—a person who understands another's heart.
