Yanxing: The Rekindling of Han · Book One: The Land of Heavenly Bounty

(I)

← Preface (V) Contents → (II)

CLANG! CLANG!

From the city tower came the sound of the bronze bell. Three rounds of drums followed, and from all directions came the crowing of roosters.

Ji Ying opened her eyes unwillingly. Through the gaps of the bamboo curtain, a few slanting rays of morning sunlight streamed in.

Another hour of sleep would be nice.

On the first day, one shall not be late.

She kicked aside the quilt, sat up, changing into a dark indigo wrap-front robe.

Where is the comb?

Good thing the hair had been cut short.

Facing the bronze mirror, Ji Ying dabbed on a bit of powder, testing the dimple on her right cheek.

"Thank you, honoured guest."

She picked up her qin, wrapped it in a yellow silk bag embroidered with black dragon patterns.

A dagger? No need.

She took a deep breath. The air was cool and damp.

Along the Jin River, the mist had not yet fully dispersed. Passersby strolled at ease. Small groups of elderly men sat on stone stools—some chatting, some simply sitting.

Ji Ying walked faster than the others. She passed rows of small, old houses, their doors half open.

Inside were low tables, clay jars, straw mats—yet no one was there, and the doors were not closed.

Two soldiers passed by, with no weapons at their waists.

Ah—they even smiled at me. One of them seemed to have only one arm.

The other limped slightly.

Oh no—I forgot to return the greeting.

The marketplace was noisy, filled with merchants and customers. The street was narrow, with stalls on both sides connecting overhead. From the awnings hung strings of red tasselled lanterns, like ripe fruit.

At the rice stall, white rice was piled high. The vendor lazily adjusted the scale without shouting. Beside it were straw ritual objects, with seven or eight dried ducks hanging, their skins glistening with oil. Nearby, buckets held plump live fish, water spilling over the rims. Radishes, garlic, scallions, fish, meat, and dried goods were piled in front for selection, while shopkeepers stayed inside to enjoy the shade.

What vegetable is this? I've never seen it before.

Not far away, steam rose, and the scent of rice drifted over.

Ji Ying bought a steaming hot flatbread, added chili sauce, and ate as she walked, blowing on it between bites.

She passed a blacksmith shop, its sign reading "Pu Yuan." She suddenly stopped.

There were no swords, spears, or shields—only thick kitchen knives, thin fruit knives, and long, slender tailor's scissors.

The blade patterns were uneven and broken. There were black specks across the metal.

Poor hammering. Incomplete folding. Insufficient heat. Impurities not fully removed.

Below the window stood rows of five-foot bronze coin trees, mounted on ceramic lion bases. Six layers of branches spread outward, each with four limbs. On them were carved hollow bronze dragons, phoenixes, coins, and chariots, swaying in the wind as if coins might fall to the ground.

Heh. The ones I made have all been sold.

The shopkeeper, a slightly plump middle-aged man, smiled broadly. "Miss Ji, want to come back and make money trees? I'll pay you more."

"No. I have a new job now!"

Working as a smith wasn't the problem. Making burial goods for the dead—that was what felt meaningless.

Ji Ying arrived at the Imperial Academy of Chengdu. Compared to Luoyang, the buildings were a few stories lower, and there were no stone classics or carved steles.

In front of the academy was a square, about half the size of the eastern market of Luoyang.

On ordinary days, it was a place where old men took walks. But this morning, two or three students were shouting loudly:

"Restore the Han court!"

"Reclaim the Central Plains!"

Ji Ying rolled her eyes.

Arrogance of the ignorant.

She walked to a teahouse by a pond. There were already many guests inside, but she could not enter yet. Several servants were removing the old signboard, while a new one leaned nearby, waiting to be put up.

Yesterday, this place was still called Yanxing Pavilion. From today on, it will be renamed Furong Pavilion.

In the middle of the pond, lotus flowers had risen above the water, their buds just beginning to open. A few fat fish swam leisurely in circles below.

Cicadas chirped somewhere unseen. A flock of sparrows flew in and vanished among the branches of a large tree.

I am not them. How would I know if they are happy?

At last, when the new sign was hung, Ji Ying entered the teahouse.

There was no designated seat for a qin player. The owner pointed to a corner, laid down several cloth cushions, and had a servant bring over the removed signboard—Yanxing Pavilion.

Four tea bricks were placed beneath it for support. The signboard was then covered with plain silk, and the qin was set on top. Inside a bamboo frame lay a bundle of bamboo slips listing various piece titles.

This will do for now.

Ji Ying plucked the strings.

"Lushui" unfolded slowly. The sound was cool and flowing, winding around stones, continuous and unbroken.

Occasional harmonics rose lightly—sunlight reflecting on water, flickering like scattered gold.

The fat fish in the pond continued their slow circling, while the lingering tones dissolved into the cicadas and sparrows.

Father would never have imagined that I would travel alone to Chengdu, thousands of li away, and live by the craft he taught me—playing the qin in a teahouse.

Back home, no one drank "tea," that bitter thing. But Father would likely enjoy its faint fragrance and lingering sweetness.

Mother and Brother are with Uncle Shan. They should be doing well.

When the piece ended, the guests applauded. At a table behind, four young men clapped especially loudly.

"Thank you, honored guests."

"We haven't seen you before, miss. Are you new here?" one of them asked, stepping forward.

"I arrived in Chengdu last year."

"Where is your hometown?"

"Henei, Shanyang."

"Henei? Where is that?"

"Northeast of Luoyang. Five days' walk."

"You are from Wei?"

Ji Ying nodded. A murmur spread through the teahouse. The other three young men also came forward.

"Do you like the Later Han?"

"…Yes."

"What do you like about it?" the man pressed.

"…Chengdu is not like Luoyang."

"Abandoning darkness for light—good!"

"…What?"

Ji Ying saw him raise a thumbs-up. He looked about twenty years old, with a straight posture and neat appearance. His face still carried some youth, yet his bearing was refined. At his waist hung a jade pendant carved with a dragon—clearly not a commoner.

"What is different between Chengdu and Luoyang?" he continued.

"…The temperament of the people. You can tell from the way they speak and think."

"Oh?" The young man pondered. "You can tell who is from Wei?"

"…Probably."

"Do I seem like someone from Wei?"

"…No. If you were, you would have already declared your prestigious lineage and spoken of rank and profit."

The young man's eyes lit up. He was about to continue when a companion reminded him, "We should head to the Taixue square!"

The four men bowed to Ji Ying, left some coins, and departed.

The qin sounded again—this time "Baren." The melody was plain and stretched, moving in small steps. Guests came and went without pause. Servants carried tea and dishes, calling out as they moved.

"Hey, do you really think the court's 'hundred-value coins' and 'five-hundred-value coins' are good policy?"

"Strengthening the state and the army is of course good policy. What else would it be—bad governance?" A table of scholars spoke loudly in front of Ji Ying.

"In my view, it's nothing more than the court lacking funds, using coinage to extract wealth from the people."

"How so?"

The man grew animated, his voice rising above the qin. Ji Ying watched them, though they did not notice.

He gestured on the table: "Suppose the government mints a coin worth one hundred, and exchanges it for one shi of grain. The grain goes into the state granary, and you receive a coin worth one hundred. But if you take that coin back to the market, you may not get a full shi of grain again. Repeat this, and the people's wealth diminishes, flowing into the state."

His companion laughed. "Not so. When the state fixed prices, one coin for one shi—you could still exchange it back. Where is the loss?"

The man shook his head. "Think further. Each year the government mints new coins. Money increases in the market, but grain in the fields does not necessarily increase. More money, same goods—prices rise. Not only grain—everything. Look at houses and land. Have they ever become cheaper over the decades?"

His companion nodded. "That… is true."

The man continued: "When common people's money loses value day by day, and famine or rent comes due, they are forced to sell their land and homes. The wealthy—who have grain in their granaries and coin in their vaults—buy low. Acre by acre, house by house, everything passes into their hands. In the west of the city now, the finest lands and houses belong mostly to the Chen, Li, Huang, Jiang, and Yan families."

"The court is blind—seeing only short-term gain!" The man slammed the table. Bang!

Guests turned their heads. Ji Ying stopped playing.

The man flushed with embarrassment and quickly settled his bill.

By noon, diners arrived in crowds. Ji Ying glanced at a nearby table—steamed fish, braised meat, pickled vegetables, stir-fried dishes, a pot of tea. Only two people were eating, and the price did not seem high.

Her stomach rumbled softly.

She went to the kitchen entrance and asked if there was leftover food. A servant, without hesitation, brought her a bowl of white rice and ladled some fish broth over it.

After eating, she took a bamboo slip from a guest. The requested piece was "Tang Yao."

Her left hand pressed the strings; her right hand plucked.

The opening tones were broad and slow, like morning mist just dispersing—distant mountains faint, never fully clear.

A few harmonics rose lightly, then returned to the main tone, like birds calling briefly in a courtyard at dawn before flying away.

The music of the Three Dynasties—plain, steady, moving only between gong and shang—like ripples across water, layer upon layer, yet never stirred by wind.

Each note was proper, like court ritual—step by step, bow by bow, without the slightest error.

What a dull piece.

The guests who had requested "Tang Yao" sat close by, all elderly, their hair and beards white.

"I only heard this morning!" one said. "Two days ago, a great tree in the palace suddenly snapped without cause. Yesterday, twelve characters appeared on a pillar—words foretelling the fall of the state!"

"What twelve characters?"

The old man lowered his voice. "Many and great—awaiting union; prepared and bestowed—how shall it return?"

"What return?" another old man cupped his ear.

"It means: the northern great state has many people and will unite the realm. And our previous Emperor of the Later Han, named Liu Bei—'bei' meaning 'to prepare'—has prepared this small state. And the present Son of Heaven, Liu Shan—'shan' as in abdication—will yield it, like Yao and Shun, to the north."

Another elder frowned. "To use the emperor's personal names in the palace, to speak of the fall of the state—how can such recklessness happen?"

"Rebellious villain."

"Open treason."

Ji Ying paused her playing. "What punishment would the person who wrote those words receive?" she asked.

The old man looked confused.

"The one who uses the emperor's name to spread talk of downfall. What crime is that?"

"What crime?"

"…Inciting the people? Disturbing order?"

"What disorder?"

"He wrote on a palace pillar that the emperor would lose the state. Should he not be arrested?"

"Why arrest him?"

…Not arrest him?

Father should have come to Shu long ago.

Around midday, another table requested "Feilong."

The opening was swift—like wind sweeping clouds apart. Heavy presses fell in succession, like a long dragon bursting from the mist.

High strings rang bright; low strings responded deep. They chased each other, like a coiling dragon—diving, rising.

"This Grand General…" a guest said. "Defeat after defeat, yet still exhausting the people for war."

"Exactly. Lost a battle last year, and this year he's sending people to the front again."

Another replied, "That battle at Houhe was a draw! We withdrew in good order. This year, just reinforcement."

"You believe that? Reinforcement? Next month he'll find another excuse to send people to die."

"If you don't want to serve, sit in the shade."

"Only fools want to go."

"And you're clever—preserving your life. Haven't you heard? 'When the nest is overturned, no egg remains unbroken.' When Wei comes, you'll be first to die."

Ji Ying glanced at the speaker.

"Then you go restore the Han! If you dare go to Tazhong, fight on the front line, and bring back a few heads—I'll respect you."

"I want to! To see General Jiang, old General Liao—veterans who once wore the Yellow Turban—how many stories they must have! It's just that my mother won't let me go."

"Who would let their son follow Jiang Wei year after year to seek death?"

"This is taking the initiative—controlling the size of the war. Do you even understand warfare?"

"With just Yizhou against the entire Central Plains? Even if they lose twenty thousand and you ten thousand, who goes down first? If you can't win, you don't fight—that is strategy!"

"The Han army has strict discipline, strong training—millions united. Crossbows fly—whsshh—" The man gestured excitedly. "Wei troops collapse in panic!"

Ji Ying let out a sudden snort of laughter before she could stop herself. The speaker looked up, was mocked by his companions, and flushed red.

"Feilong" ended in a rapid flourish that rose into a long, soaring tone, as its wings broke into the sky.

"Thank you, honored guests."

Before long, a middle-aged couple came and sat to Ji Ying's right.

The wife wore jade ornaments and fine silk; the husband wore a black head-wrap, dressed in a dark red short robe, a leather belt at his waist, and a seal and ribbon hanging—clearly a court official.

The wife requested "Kunji" and smiled kindly at Ji Ying.

"You are young, yet you have come out to make a living—quite courageous."

Ji Ying lowered her fingers onto the strings. The music rose high, like a pheasant lifting its head and calling, spreading its wings. Then the tone softened, like young birds stepping lightly through fallen leaves, searching for food, giving occasional short cries.

The wife asked, "Today, when you meet the Son of Heaven, do you think His Majesty will allow you to go to Hanzhong?"

The husband glanced around, then answered in a low voice, "His Majesty only evaded the matter. It still depends on Attendant Huang. I told Huang Hao that I have managed Jiange for many years. If I am sent to the front lines of Han or Le fortress, I would be the most suitable candidate. Or better yet, let me guard Yang'an Pass—no matter how many troops Wei sends, I can trap them in Hanzhong."

"And what did Huang Hao say?"

The husband replied, "That eunuch—always polite, yet says nothing. After all that talk, nothing is decided." He sighed. "Yan Yu bribed him and rose three ranks in rapid succession. I have been honest all my life, taking not a single coin from subordinates or the people—what wealth do I have to satisfy him?"

"As long as you act with integrity, you have no shame."

"If Chancellor Zhuge were still here, how would these foxes and dogs be allowed to ruin the court?"

"Why not seek the Chancellor's son, the Wei General?"

"He does not involve himself in affairs." The husband shook his head. "Ten years ago, Grand General Fei was murdered by a Wei agent. Since then, our Later Han has had no pillar of state. To think it has fallen to this… state." He sighed again.

"Why not become that pillar yourself?"

Ji Ying glanced at the wife. Her expression was firm.

The husband laughed. "What—enter the palace and assassinate Huang Hao?"

"One less villain is a blessing for the realm! I will ensure you have no worries behind you."

At the height of "Kunji," the strings tightened, a sharp rising note splitting the air like a pheasant bursting from the forest.

What a resolute woman.

Yet to urge her husband to become an assassin—somehow, it fell short. If she went herself, would that not be better?

The husband shook his head. "Kill one corrupt minister, another will replace him. Even if Huang Hao dies, there will be another. I only lament that I, Ma Miao, was born at the wrong time—a thousand-li horse without one who knows how to use it."

Guests came and went. Discussions of family and state never ceased. The Furong Pavilion thrived. Before long, the sun was setting.

After a full day of playing, listening to cicadas and birds, Ji Ying felt content. But when she counted her coins, they were far fewer than what she earned making bronze coin trees in the smithy yard.

As she prepared to return to her lodging, passing by the Taixue square, she saw five or six youths still gathered.

"Reclaim the North!"

"Restore the Han!"

Their voices were hoarse, yet still full of fervor.

As Ji Ying passed, one of them said, "We should have asked Xiao Yu to gather some Yulin Guards." As he spoke, he suddenly noticed her.

"The qin player from Wei!"

The young man ran over, the jade pendant at his waist swaying.

Ah—the one who had praised me earlier for "abandoning darkness for light."

"I am Zhuge Shang!" he said, bowing formally.

"He is the eldest grandson of Chancellor Zhuge!" one of his companions added, clapping him on the shoulder.

Zhuge Liang's grandson.

In Wei, the grandson of Sima Yi—the son of Sima Zhao—would be surrounded by dozens of attendants and guards.

Yet Zhuge Liang's grandson stood here with only a few companions, unguarded. Was he not afraid of being cut down by Wei agents?

"My name is Ji Ying. I have no glorious ancestors."

"Miss Ji," Zhuge Shang bowed deeply. "You said you prefer Chengdu to Luoyang, correct?"

"Yes."

"You also said you can tell who is from Wei just by how they speak?"

"…I suppose. Your friend just now—he disclosed your family background without hesitation. That is like someone from Wei."

"Ah!" the companion exclaimed. "My mother is indeed from Chang'an!"

Zhuge Shang raised both thumbs. "Remarkable! Would you be willing to help us?"

"Help you shout 'down with Wei'?"

The group burst into laughter.

"No." Zhuge Shang's expression suddenly turned serious. "Yesterday, something happened in the palace. If you can distinguish people from Wei, perhaps you can help."

"What happened?"

"Two days ago, a pillar in the palace—someone wrote—"

"Twelve characters. Saying the state will fall, right?"

The group gasped.

"You already know?"

"Four tables of guests today spoke of it. A great tree broke—an ill omen. Then someone wrote twelve characters on a pillar, making wordplay out of the emperors' names. No one thinks the writer should be arrested."

Zhuge Shang's eyes shone. "We have truly found the right person! If you can assist—this would be a great fortune for the Han. I would be deeply grateful."

"You want me to identify which guest is a Wei agent who wrote those words—so you can arrest him?"

Zhuge Shang clapped. "Exactly! Brilliant!"

Even if there is a Wei agent, why should I condemn some unlucky man for you?

"…As those guests in the teahouse said earlier—speaking, writing—there is nothing serious about it. Don't concern yourself."

Zhuge Shang looked disappointed, then cupped his hands again. "Others may not care, but we do."

"Then find someone who cares to catch spies. I have already told you how to recognize people from Wei."

"You are skilled—you must have other insights." Zhuge Shang insisted. "Miss Ji, you only need to observe the teahouse for suspicious individuals. There will be payment every day. When we catch the spy, my father will reward you generously!"

Father.

"…I am only a qin player. I'm sorry." Ji Ying shook her head and strode away.

"Then—who seems suspicious today?" Zhuge Shang called after her.

Don't arrest him. Let him write.

Let him write twelve thousand characters—write for a lifetime—cover every pillar in the palace, every stele in the academy.

Heaven shows no favoritism.

Give him a chance. Let his good and evil return to himself.

Do not let his retribution fall upon his wife and children.

"If you let traitors go, what if others are harmed?" Zhuge Shang shouted from afar.

"Would you allow villains to twist right and wrong, and lead people away from what is good?"

Ji Ying stopped.

"Take care of yourself."

She walked on.

Through the bustling market, past the smithy, she paused at a bookstall. Most books were about divination and fortune. But in a narrow gap between bamboo baskets, she found a fragment of the Liezi.

She picked it up and read:

"Forms need not be alike, while wisdom may be the same. Wisdom need not be the same, while forms may be alike. The sage values shared wisdom and disregards shared form. The common man clings to shared form and neglects shared wisdom. Those who resemble me in form, they draw near and love; those who differ, they distance and fear."

Ji Ying lifted her head, thinking.

People of Shu and people of Wei may differ in form, yet if their thoughts are aligned, they may still be friends.

With most of the day's earnings, she bought the fragment of the Liezi. With what remained, she bought a small flatbread.

She read as she ate. The air by the Jin River was cool. The houses that had stood open in the morning were now lit within. The scent of food drifted out—fish, meat, garlic, scallions—blending together. Occasionally, children ran out into the street, and no adult hurried to pull them back. Voices of neighbors, the clatter of bowls and chopsticks, carried faintly through the walls.

By the time she returned to her lodging, it was already dark. From a distance, she saw someone standing outside her door.

A soldier, clad in silver armor, holding a bundle.

As she drew closer, she saw long hair falling from beneath the helmet—it was a woman.

"Are you Miss Ji Ying from Wei?" the female soldier asked.

Her voice rang like a bronze bell, yet was soft as silk. Though it was dark, Ji Ying saw her strong build, full oval face, amber-toned skin, high cheekbones, deep-set eyes—bright and almost otherworldly.

"How do you know I am from Wei?"

The woman smiled. "I am an immortal—I calculated it with a flick of my fingers."

Her smile brought her features alive—red lips, white teeth.

A goddess from the Western Regions!

"Ha! Just joking. My cousin, Zhuge Shang, told me. May I come in? I have something good for you."

"…What?"
