Yanxing: The Rekindling of Han · Book One: The Land of Heavenly Bounty

Book One: (V)

← Book One (IV) Contents → Book One (VI)

A person of integrity — who has done nothing wrong — must still bow and apologise?

What would Father say?

Why get tangled up with them at all. Let us go back to Yuntai Mountain together — forge iron, talk freely, and be at peace.

Before heading out to the Zhuge household, Xiaoyu stopped at a roadside stall and bought food ingredients as gifts.

"Is this not your own home?"

"Not quite. My brother and I rent rooms near the Imperial Academy and the Imperial Library.1"

The four of them — Zhuge Shang, Zhuge Yu and her brother, and Ji Ying — left through the western gate of Chengdu and walked for about half a shichen.

The world opened wide before them: pale blue mountains, layer upon layer, stacked along the line where sky met earth.

The rain had passed by now. Evening orange clouds spread from behind the mountains, and the paddies glowed warm red; the rice shoots swayed gently, their gaps catching scattered gleams of sunset light.

Mulberry trees stood in neat rows, their branches dense with leaves, swaying in the drowsy warmth of the wind.

Ji Ying had expected the Zhuge family estate to be a sprawling mansion with high walls and towers, but the compound was mostly small tiled cottages — the walls plain and unadorned, the gates of wood, the fences of bamboo, with no lacquer or carved ornament anywhere. Three or four servants walked toward the house with shoulder-poles and baskets, chatting as they went; the baskets were piled high with fresh green mulberry leaves.

When they reached the Zhuge gate, the house was modest — one corner of the brick wall had crumbled away. The courtyard was not wide, and along the walls ploughs and rakes, hemp rope, and bamboo ware were arranged in their proper places; several bamboo baskets sat below the window, full of freshly washed, still-damp clothes. Through the window, there was a loom.

"Mother!" Zhuge Shang waved to his mother at the loom.

Seeing the visitors, Zhuge Shang's mother came out of the room at an unhurried pace.

Her clothes were no different from those of any commoner, and she wore no jewellery.

Xiaoyu had more on her face than she did.

"Shang, where is your father?"

"Something urgent — he has gone to the palace. Tonight we have a guest from far away! With Xiaoyu and Brother Maozi — there is confidential business to discuss."

Zhuge Shang's mother raised her brows, her expression tranquil.

"This is Miss Ji from Henei Commandery. She plays the qin beautifully."

Ji Ying bent in a small bow and smiled.

"Don't be formal. Go and see to your affairs."

Zhuge Shang's mother said no more and went back inside to her loom. The silk cloth was already more than half done — pale and lustrous.

Ji Ying watched her settle before it, left hand drawing the thread, right hand passing the shuttle, her ten fingers nimble, the loom clicking in its steady rhythm like a musician keeping time, weft and warp entering the cloth in turn.

A princess of the Later Han, and she too lives by a loom?

Zhuge Shang and Xiaoyu took the food they had brought to help the kitchen servants, and Ji Ying glimpsed the kitchen beyond — pots and jars hanging at different heights, each in its proper order.

Ji Ying wandered in the front room. The tables and cases were neatly set; the bookshelves and lamp stands were each plain and simple; the trunks and bamboo mats showed signs of age, yet everything sat in exactly the right place.

The home of the Wei General — the emperor's own son-in-law — and its furnishings were no different from those of my rented room.

Only my room is far messier.

A framed scroll hung on the wall, mounted on the same white silk as the cloth on the loom, with a wooden rod at the top hanging from a wall hook.

The paper bore several horizontal and vertical creases; it had yellowed with age and was dotted here and there with small brown spots. Several dozen characters were written on it. Ji Ying did not read it carefully, only caught the words simplicity and serenity2 in passing, and gave an unconscious nod.

The script was something between clerical and seal script — each stroke level and upright, exceptionally precise.

On the same wall beside the scroll hung a large map of provinces and commanderies, nearly the height of a person: from Guanzhong at the top, down to…

She had never heard of "Nanzhong."3

And below Nanzhong, there were still the commanderies of Jiao Province.3

How vast the world is.

On the map, Ji Ying traced her journey of the year before — how she had come from Luoyang to Chengdu, playing the qin, riding ox-carts, playing the qin again…

Half a map, and it had taken three months.

She looked for a quicker route: from Luoyang, through Tongguan, Chang'an, Hanzhong, Yang'an Pass, Jianmen Pass, Zitong…

Too many place names to remember.

This house of Han — four hundred years of it — she had once heard from someone, long ago, that it began in Hanzhong.4

Ji Ying wandered and looked about, and before long the meal was ready. She sat on the side with Zhuge Mao and Xiaoyu. Zhuge Shang also had a younger brother named Zhuge Jing,5 who sat across from Ji Ying — the same age as her, it turned out, and not much for conversation.

On the table: steamed cabbage with bean paste, bamboo shoots braised in fermented beans, a whole fresh fish steamed clean, freshly cut mallow soup, pickled vegetables, and tofu.

On Yuntai Mountain, Ji Ying had never shared so many dishes with so many people at once.

Zhuge Jing, across from her, passed over a pot of millet rice.

"Thank you."

Zhuge Shang moved to pour Ji Ying some wine.

"No thank you — I'll just have tea."

Zhuge Shang said: "Miss Ji, you are the best judge of who is a Wei man."

Not this again…

"Tell us — is Brother Maozi a Wei man?"

Zhuge Mao turned his head, his face expressionless.

"Obviously."

If Zhuge Shang was asking, it must be so.

Zhuge Shang and Xiaoyu burst out laughing.

"Impressive. I came at about Miss Ji's age. Five years have gone by in a blink." Zhuge Mao rose and took a wine cup from Zhuge Shang with both hands, sipping a small mouthful.

So he is not born a Zhuge?

"Where in Wei are you from?"

"Somewhere south of Luoyang, near Xiangyang."

Zhuge Mao reached his chopsticks toward the steamed fish and cut off a small piece.

Three thousand li — how did he make that journey?

A merchant's ox-cart?

"You see, now you've confused Little Sister Ji Ying. How is it the Zhuge family has a Wei man on one side and someone from Dayuan and the Yuezhi6 on the other?" Xiaoyu pointed at her own face: "In truth, my brother and I are both orphans — adopted children of the Zhuge family."

Orphans without a father. I am an orphan now too.

A servant brought round another full pot of millet rice, pale wisps of steam curling from the earthenware.

Xiaoyu said: "My brother and I share the same foster mother. Does Little Sister Ji Ying remember where she lives?"

"Chaozhen Guan?"

"Yes!" Xiaoyu looked a little sheepish: "I keep saying I will take you there. Soon — I promise!"

Zhuge Shang picked up a piece of tofu: "She is my father's elder sister."

The tofu slipped through his chopsticks before it reached his mouth and fell back onto the dish in a pale lump. Zhuge Shang ate it in one bite anyway.

So Zhuge Zhan was the younger brother of Xiaoyu's foster mother.

I hope Ji Shao and Mother are well, and have good people around them at Uncle Shan's.

"Actually, I also have a younger brother."

Zhuge Shang asked: "How is it we have never seen him?"

Xiaoyu shot Zhuge Shang a look.

Zhuge Shang stumbled over his words, then asked instead: "How much older is Aunt than Father?"

"Jianxing five, Zhangwu two, Jian'an…"7 Xiaoyu counted on her fingers.

"Twelve years older," Zhuge Jing answered.

I used to find Ji Shao too small, always in the way.

Perhaps Zhuge Zhan and his elder sister are not quite like brother and sister.

Lady Liu said: "To say nothing of Miss Ji and Maozi being Wei people — the Zhuge family itself came from Langye Commandery, and the Han imperial house of the Later Han, and my own Zhang clan, both came from Zhuo Commandery. All from the north."

Zhuge Shang drained a full cup of wine: "The people of the Later Han come from all nine provinces — and from the Western Regions too!"

Everyone grew lively and raised their cups; Ji Ying drank half a cup herself.

The wine carried a faint sweetness of fermented grain, and left a gentle warmth in the throat.

Though she normally ate little and the food did not have too many spices, Ji Ying ate until she was full.

After the meal, the four of them went back inside, lit the oil lamp by the wall, and were just settling down to sit beneath the large map. Hoofbeats clattered outside, and there were grinds of wheels on stone — Lady Liu stopped the loom, and everyone rose to receive the arrival.

The carriage had not yet come to a full stop when someone already pushed back the curtain and stepped out.

Zhuge Zhan stepped through the door, the hem of his robe sweeping in a gust of air. The black outer robe over his official dress had caught some mud from the ground; his leather belt was fastened tight. He removed his official cap; the black headwrap beneath was dishevelled.

Lady Liu stepped forward to take his cap and outer robe, and told Zhuge Zhan that food had been kept for him.

Zhuge Zhan gave only a small nod.

"Have you settled on how to make the apology?"

"We just finished eating and were about to discuss it," Zhuge Shang said.

"Remember: apologise only. No conflict, no mention of the northern campaigns, no mention of restoring the Han."

Zhuge Shang pressed his lips together and gave a reluctant nod.

Zhuge Zhan's gaze moved toward Ji Ying — but his eyes landed on the map behind her.

Ji Ying turned; Zhuge Zhan's gaze was resting on the region around Hanzhong.

Lady Liu said nothing, only stood at his side, holding his clothes.

A servant laid out fresh bowls and chopsticks; the dishes touched one another with a soft clink.

"I will go to the study to draft a memorial to the emperor. Discuss it and settle it quickly." Zhuge Zhan did not sit down to eat, but went straight into the inner room.

That cavalry dispatch this afternoon — it had clearly been urgent.

Before long, Zhuge Jing came in with a scroll, sat down by the oil lamp at the wall, and told the others to go ahead and talk without minding him.

The four settled themselves. Ji Ying glanced at the paper window by the lamp: several patches of different shades had been pasted over tears — the old ones yellowed, the new ones white — like a worn coat roughly mended.

Zhuge Shang clenched both fists: "I cannot stand it. We have to bow to that kind of scoundrel?"

Xiaoyu frowned too.

Zhuge Mao said: "He had his great moment last night, then got hauled away by a squad of Yulin Guards. When everyone loses, no one loses."

"Even the dead come out alive when Brother Maozi talks." Zhuge Shang shook his head in reluctant exasperation.

Xiaoyu glanced toward the study and lowered her voice: "Uncle said earlier that the Yan Hong faction will cry foul — claiming they were targeted by Grand General Jiang in revenge, and that the Later Han's ruling clan cannot tolerate dissent. The apology is necessary. We cannot let Yan Hong and Jiang Kang use this to draw in more followers."

Zhuge Mao looked at Ji Ying: "Last night, you and I both heard Jiang Kang say in person that he wanted to fill the whole Imperial Academy square."

"We must act quickly."

Jiang Kang was the one who slandered and insulted my father — and yet I am the one who must apologise.

But if nothing is done, this ugliness will only spread further.

Just like Zhong Hui — who had my father killed, and then went off to become whatever Grand General, commanding all the armies.

Ji Ying held a slow breath in her chest and let it out.

Xiaoyu lowered her voice again: "Uncle said the apology needs to be seen. Should we hold another tea gathering, perhaps?"

"No need to go to that trouble." Zhuge Mao said: "The Yan Hong faction has already begun gathering in the Imperial Academy square. I passed by this morning and recognised a few faces. I will go and find out when Jiang Kang and the Right Grand General's son are due to appear — and then we go to the square to find them."

But is he not a Zhuge?

"Zhuge Shang and Xiaoyu were the ones who had Jiang Kang arrested. And you are family. Why would Jiang Kang's group not be on their guard against you?"

Xiaoyu and Zhuge Shang exchanged a glance.

"Why would they be on their guard?" Xiaoyu looked genuinely puzzled.

Ji Ying's mind went back to the teahouse and the Imperial Secretariat.

Why make an arrest?

What crime had been committed?

The Land of Heavenly Bounty. It can hold a few words.

Zhuge Mao smiled at Ji Ying: "You will get used to the people of the Later Han." As he spoke, he smoothed the bamboo mat beneath him.

Zhuge Jing turned a page of his book beside them, the sound so soft it was as though he feared to startle anyone.

A patch of window paper lifted in a gust and trembled a few times.

A small insect, drawn by the lamplight, had climbed up and was clinging to the paper, refusing to fall.

Outside the window the dusk was thickening; mulberry leaves rustled softly in the evening wind. A servant lowered a half-rolled bamboo blind partway down.

Xiaoyu looked at Ji Ying with a slightly aggrieved expression: "When we face Jiang Kang in the square, you say that I mistook him for a spy and went to report him to the guards. Will that do?"

"That will do."

I will treat him like a stone.

A stone in a latrine — I would not look at it, not smell it; I would take Zhong Hui's treatise on talent and nature, lay it over him, and let them rot together.

"After Ji Ying speaks, I will follow with the apology — that the Yulin Guards should not have arrested someone without evidence of a crime, and that this is not how the law of the Later Han works. And then we offer him something as a gift by way of amends." Xiaoyu patted her pocket and glanced at her brother.

Zhuge Mao nodded.

"If our apology is sincere enough, perhaps he will be moved — and even come to support the restoration of the Han!"

Silence fell over the room.

Even the lamplight went still, neither swaying nor flickering.

Xiaoyu reached up and touched the tip of her nose.

"My brother is the best persuader among us — shall we try?"

"Yan Hong wants to use the anti-war cause to win popular support, so his father Yan Yu can take the Grand General's command and army. Jiang Kang is his mouthpiece and his fist. Both of them have too much at stake — they are almost impossible to bring over. What great-uncle Zhang said is what matters most: winning over the eight or nine parts in ten who have not made up their minds, and making sure they are not pulled away."

Zhuge Shang frowned: "But Father also said we cannot stand in the Imperial Academy square and shout for the restoration of the Han. So they shout freely and we say nothing — that means the whole square will be theirs. How are we supposed to win any hearts at all?"

Silence again.

Ji Ying heard the loom clicking — Lady Liu had not yet retired, and was still weaving by lamplight.

Warp and weft, one after another — in time it would become the plain white silk used to mount the scroll on the wall, bearing up the ink-brush words.

"It is true that if they can shout in the square and we cannot shout back, we are at a disadvantage." Zhuge Mao said: "Winning hearts must happen outside the square. The city is not only Imperial Academy students — there are clerks, common people, and families of veteran soldiers, all with their own opinions. We should seek out those who are trusted and willing to speak."

"Then perhaps my brother can build more connections among the respected figures at the Academy and the Library."

Zhuge Mao said: "I am only a petty official drawing a small stipend.8 The senior officials at a hundred or a thousand shi8 I may not even get to see — but Chengdu has thousands of clerks and junior scribes,9 and I can always get a few words in with them when the chance arises. Civil servants live off the court's salary; they will not want to smash their own rice bowls."

Everyone nodded.

The loom in the room paused for a moment, then slowly began turning again.

Ji Ying's thoughts drifted back to the teahouse the night before.

"Even the stiff-necked scholars and clerks who talk about Heaven's mandate going to the Sima house — can they be reasoned with?"

Zhuge Mao did not answer at once; he first turned up the wick of the lamp a little.

"Perhaps they do not truly believe it themselves. But Miss Ji has just come from Wei and understands the Sima house well — why not come with me and speak to them?"

"That suits me."

At least it gives me something useful to do.

Hearts do not need to be won over. Just tell them the truth.

Last night in the teahouse, the Jiang Kang and Yan Hong faction talked endlessly about Heaven's mandate — but was it truly Heaven's mandate?

When something is repeated enough times, even bystanders start clapping along, starting to believe it.

Heaven's mandate — how could anyone know that?

It is nothing more than the consequences people bring upon themselves.

"But there is still one thing I cannot quite see around." Zhuge Mao said: "After the apology, the Jiang Kang faction may seize on it and say: good — these people have finally come to their senses, and they too now support recalling Jiang Wei; Heaven's mandate clearly belongs to the Sima house; the Later Han has run out of fortune. What then?"

Ji Ying said: "If they bring up Heaven's mandate again, we can say: the workings of Heaven are murky and beyond knowing. You have grown up in the Later Han and enjoyed everything it has given you — how can you turn around and seek favour from the Sima house?"

"Say exactly that." Everyone nodded.

"And if they press again about recalling the Grand General, we can say: calling for a halt to the northern campaigns, allowing the people to rest and recover, is not unreasonable in itself. But to say outright that the Han army is bound to lose — that shakes the morale of our own troops and gives the enemy reason to attack."

"If the enemy attacks, none of this tranquil life will be attainable." Zhuge Mao nodded.

"Exactly!" Zhuge Shang said, excited: "The argument is on our side — that will put a stop to their arrogance!"

"Winning by reason is best." Zhuge Mao nodded.

From the corner of her eye, Ji Ying caught sight of Zhuge Jing in the lamplight. He had been reading with his head bowed the whole time; only now did he look up and give everyone a faint smile, then lower his eyes again. Ji Ying glimpsed the heading of the passage he was reading — Rectification of Names10 — but could not name the book.

The lamplight fell quietly on the page, making the ink characters sharp and clear, and stretched across the map on the wall — its corners curling slightly, the thin shadow trembling back and forth.

Xiaoyu spoke again: "I want to play my part too. Give me a few hearts to win over."

Zhuge Mao looked serious: "Keeping the soldiers' loyalty is the most urgent thing. Even among the troops there are squad leaders and unit commanders well liked by their men."

Everyone looked toward Xiaoyu.

Xiaoyu smiled: "The Yulin Guards are all sons of veteran officers and sons of good families — their loyalty to the Han house is absolute, no need to worry there. But I do not know the troops of Shu Commandery and Chengdu well. One of these days I will put on my military uniform and take a deliberate walk along the city walls and through the barracks. Zhuge Shang can come with me."

Zhuge Shang said: "Xiaoyu has beauty enough to do the work of a hundred of me."

"The grandson of the great Zhuge Wuhou — and it falls to a Dayuan and Yuezhi woman to help restore it!" Zhuge Yu laughed, pointing to her own face.

Zhuge Shang drew himself up: "Right! Leave it to me!"

Zhuge Mao nodded: "With the scholars and the soldiers both accounted for — we can make this work."

Everyone agreed; the plan was settled, and they would each act on their part come morning.

The night had grown late. Xiaoyu raised the matter of the qin that Zhuge Wuhou had once used to drive back the enemy, and Zhuge Shang asked his younger brother to fetch it. Before long, the instrument was brought out.

"This is a family heirloom qin," Zhuge Shang said. "Legend has it that my grandfather once played this very instrument on the city walls and frightened off an entire enemy army. Miss Ji — please try it."

A qin that the Chancellor himself had played?

"That seems rather improper."

Zhuge Shang said: "Don't be shy — Zhuge Jing plays it too."

Lady Liu also stopped the loom and stepped out of the room to invite Ji Ying.

Ji Ying declined no further. She sat down, carefully took up the qin, and placed it on the low table.

The instrument was heavy.

"What shall I play?"

Xiaoyu asked: "Is there something that can frighten away an enemy?"

"This qin is old — do not play too forcefully." Zhuge Mao glanced at Ji Ying.

From Luoyang to Chengdu, she had practised from memory for three months — but had never played for the teahouse guests.

"What about the Guangling Melody?"

"Zhuge Jing — do you know this piece?" Zhuge Shang looked at his brother.

Zhuge Jing said: "I know the legend behind it, but I have never heard it played nor played it myself."

"What is the legend?" Xiaoyu asked.

Zhuge Jing began to speak, unhurried: "Six hundred or so years ago, there was a butcher named Nie Zheng who had some skill in fighting. A nobleman travelled great distances to find him, made a generous gift to honour his aged mother, and asked Nie Zheng to kill an enemy for him. Nie Zheng refused — he still had his elderly mother to care for and could not take the risk. Later, after his mother died, Nie Zheng sought out the nobleman and carried out the killing. But Nie Zheng himself was badly wounded, and before he died he used his short sword to destroy his own face. When his elder sister heard the news she ran to find him, and wept over his body. She said: my brother feared implicating that nobleman, feared implicating me — that is why he disfigured himself. She wept three times aloud, then took the short sword and stabbed herself, dying at her brother's side. Later generations, moved by this story, set it to music as the Guangling Melody."

A story of a brother and sister, too.

And so everyone asked Ji Ying to play the Guangling Melody.

Ji Ying plucked a single string — then stopped abruptly.

This instrument sounds nothing like Father's qin, nothing like any qin I have played before.

The tone was not the usual clear, desolate cry. It was more like cold iron drawn from its scabbard, like a frost wind splitting open the wild.

A gust of night air swept in, and Ji Ying felt a shiver pass through her whole body.

She drew a slow, deep breath, and the opening phrase of the Guangling Melody rose.

The notes came one after another — like a cold blade pressing into bone, like a fierce wind lashing a banner, like armoured horses striking earth, standing beneath the moon over a desolate city.

But the killing force was not in noise and clamour. It lived in a stillness that cut deeper and deeper into the bone — eerie, unfathomable.

That stillness concealed a danger no one could see the bottom of, with no knowing how deep it ran, no reading whether it was substance or shadow.

Not like the loud clamour in the teahouse. More like a blade not yet drawn from its sheath, buried in the hearts of ten thousand people of the blazing Han.

The music ended. A cool wind drifted in and stirred the mounted scroll on the wall — the one bearing the words simplicity and serenity.

The Zhuge family all rose to their feet, ready to applaud, then held back for fear of disturbing Zhuge Zhan in the inner room, and only called out their praise in hushed voices.

Zhuge Shang dropped his voice to a murmur: "Together, we will go to the Imperial Academy square — and drive them back!"

1 Imperial Library (秘書台): The imperial office responsible for custodying the court's books, maps, and official records. Not to be confused with the Imperial Secretariat (尚書台), which handled the daily administration of government. ↩

2 Simplicity and serenity (淡泊、寧靜): Two words from the Admonition to My Son (誡子書), a letter attributed to Zhuge Liang. The most celebrated lines read: "Without simplicity of wants, one cannot clarify one's aspirations; without serenity, one cannot go far." ↩

3 Nanzhong (南中) and Jiao Province (交州): Nanzhong was the Shu Han name for the territories south of the Yangtze basin — roughly present-day Yunnan, Guizhou, and southern Sichuan — home to numerous non-Han peoples. Jiao Province lay further south still, covering modern-day Vietnam and Guangdong. ↩

4 Hanzhong as the origin of Han (漢中): The Han dynasty takes its name from the Hanzhong basin, where its founder was enfeoffed as King of Han before his conquest of the empire in 202 BCE. ↩

5 Zhuge Jing (諸葛京): Zhuge Zhan's second son. ↩

6 Dayuan and the Yuezhi (大宛、月氏): Dayuan was a Central Asian kingdom in the Ferghana Valley, famous for its "heavenly horses"; the Yuezhi were a nomadic people originally from the western steppes who later founded the Kushan Empire. ↩

7 Era names (年號) — Jianxing (建興), Zhangwu (章武), Jian'an (建安): Chinese dynasties marked time through era names proclaimed by the reigning emperor; a new reign or significant event would prompt a new era name. Jian'an (196–220 CE) was the last era of Eastern Han, followed by Zhangwu (221–223 CE) and Jianxing (223–237 CE) of Shu Han. ↩

8 Shi ranks (秩等): Han dynasty official grades were expressed in shi — a unit of grain volume — but these designations functioned as rank titles rather than literal grain payments. The scale ran from 10,000 shi down to 100 shi for officials. ↩

9 Clerks and junior scribes (書佐、脩行): Shuzuo (書佐) were copyists who assisted senior officials with paperwork; xiuxing (脩行) were junior functionaries who carried out directives and ran errands within a bureau. ↩

10 Rectification of Names (正名): A chapter title found in both the Analects of Confucius and the Xunzi, holding that correct governance begins with calling things by their proper names. ↩
