Yanxing: The Rekindling of Han · Book One: The Land of Heavenly Bounty

Book One: (IV)

← Book One (III) Contents → Book One (V)

By the time Ji Ying followed Xiaoyu and Zhuge Shang to the Imperial Secretariat,1 the clear morning had turned to overcast and drizzle, and distant thunder rolled in from afar.

I am here today to serve as a witness — to make the charge against Jiang Kang as a spy stick.

Ji Ying thought back to the night before.

Before her stood several old and weathered buildings, ranged to either side, each taller than the Prince of Beidi's residence, their double doors hung with plaques reading Bureau of Personnel, Bureau of Granaries, and the like. Covered corridors and courtyards connected the buildings, and clerks with scrolls tucked under their arms shuffled through them with unhurried steps.

Strings of raindrops fell from the eaves.

Two clerks leaned against a pillar, speaking in low voices, their manner easy — clearly not discussing official business.

Zhuge Shang explained that all affairs of the realm passed through the Imperial Secretariat — received, sorted, recorded.

And one of the three men who currently shared its authority was his father, Zhuge Zhan.

The three of them moved down the covered walkway without pausing, when hoofbeats suddenly sounded ahead — a black horse galloped through the rain from left to right.

Ji Ying saw that the soldier on the horse was clutching a leather satchel to his chest, three long pheasant feathers thrust upright from it.2

The soldier dismounted and jogged through the courtyard, clerks stepping aside to let him pass, before turning into a two-storey building.

The same building the three of them were heading for.

Inside, the room was dim; the first thing to hit them was the smell of oil lamps mingled with a faint trace of mould.

The soldier was nowhere to be seen — only wet footprints left across the floor tiles, the grout between them blackened, packed with years of grime.

Both sides of the passage were stacked shoulder-high with bundles of yellow paper and bamboo slips. Many of the yellow-paper bundles had their edges curling loose; several of the cords had slackened.

Through an open doorway, a clerk lay sprawled face-down across a low desk, an inkstone beside him untouched.

The passage was so narrow that a clerk coming the other way had to turn sideways to squeeze past.

The three rounded several corners, and came face to face with a man of short beard, his hair shot through with grey, long narrow eyes, a pale complexion, and a taut expression.

Ji Ying saw he wore a black robe and black cap, a green cord at his waist — the dress of an official.

"Great-Uncle Zhang."3 Zhuge Shang and Xiaoyu gave a deep bow; Ji Ying did not follow.

"Zhuge Shang, what did I tell you last time?" The man's face was stern. "Stop assuming everyone is a spy! Look at the trouble you have caused this time!"

So we arrested the wrong person?

The great-uncle's eyes swept over Zhuge Yu and Ji Ying, then back to Zhuge Shang: "And you were in the armors of Yulin Guards to make the arrest — did you have to make such a spectacle of it?"

"How much trouble did we cause?" Zhuge Shang asked.

The great-uncle beckoned the three of them closer, and when they had all gathered near, he dropped his voice:

"Whatever your father says in a moment — do not argue. Do as he says. There is something pressing these next few days. Do not make more trouble."

Zhuge Shang asked: "So Jiang Kang is not a spy?"

The great-uncle looked slightly vexed: "Think about it — his father commands troops on the frontier. If he were a spy, why not simply surrender the pass? Why go to all this trouble, sending his son to spread rumours in Chengdu?"

Zhuge Shang replied: "Perhaps his father is not a spy, but he is."

The great-uncle frowned: "Even if he were, you cannot arrest him."

"If he is a spy, why can he not be arrested?"

The great-uncle's expression shifted: "Do not breathe the word spy again. Drop this matter. Go read the Xunzi4 — and practice your weapons with Xiaoyu."

As the great-uncle brushed past the three of them, he patted Zhuge Shang on the arm, then looked Ji Ying up and down.

Even if he is a spy, he still cannot be arrested?

So if I give testimony and swear that Jiang Kang is certainly a spy — that would actually make things worse?

Zhuge Shang looked thoroughly bewildered: "Is he a spy or isn't he?"

"Stop asking!" Xiaoyu pressed a finger to her lips.

The three climbed a creaking wooden staircase, turned another corner, and came out into a large room. Low desks stood before three of the walls; on the wall straight ahead hung a large wooden plaque, and beneath it sat an official in a black robe, his expression severe. Before the desk stood a clerk in a blue-green robe, his back to them.

"Come in," said the official, setting down the scroll in his hands.

Ji Ying followed the others inside and took her place beside Xiaoyu. When she looked at the official, she found herself drawing a sharp breath. He was in the prime of his years — long brows, clear eyes, a straight nose, a composed mouth. His features were fine and pale as carved jade, the kind of composed bearing that needed no announcement.

"Wei General." Zhuge Shang and Zhuge Yu gave a deep bow.

Zhuge Zhan's expression remained severe; his gaze moved to Ji Ying.

"Wei General, this is Miss Ji Ying, who was present at the Yanxing teahouse last night. She can serve as witness to the arrest of Jiang Kang." Xiaoyu said.

Zhuge Zhan nodded: "All three of you — tell me: what grounds do you have for saying Jiang Kang is a spy?"

After a brief silence, Zhuge Shang spoke: "Wei General, Jiang Kang was assembling a crowd, spreading slander against the court, and propagating the enemy's line. Last night, he was caught in the act."

Zhuge Zhan did not react. He simply looked at Zhuge Shang.

That is a father looking at his son.

That look — a little like the look Father used to give me, standing at the forge under the willows.

"Wei General, last month the right battalion of the Yulin Guards received word that spies had confirmed to be operating in Chengdu and that we were to be on high alert. Jiang Kang was spreading slander against Grand General Jiang, undermining the morale of our troops — and so we judged him to be a spy."

Zhuge Yu nudged Ji Ying's hand.

At this point, there is nothing for it but to tell the truth and see how he deals with it.

Ji Ying drew a slow breath.

"Wei General, last night I heard with my own ears: Jiang Kang declared that Heaven's mandate had passed to the Sima house, that rule through filial piety was the natural order, that Jiang Wei could not read Heaven's will, and that the people of Ba-Shu should not be dragged down to their deaths at his side. He also claimed that the writing on the palace columns — the prophecy of dynastic ruin spelled out in the emperor's name — was the work of Jiang Wei's agents, planted as a pretext for purging his opponents. I lost my temper and argued back; he insulted my late father, then lifted a table to strike me. In a rage, I accused Jiang Kang then and there of being a spy. Whether he truly is one…"

"…I cannot say for certain. If I named the wrong man, I am deeply sorry."

Father always said I acted without thinking. It is quite possible I got the wrong person.

Silence settled over the room, broken only by the sound of breathing.

"You heard this as well?" Zhuge Zhan looked toward the clerk in the blue-green robe.

"Wei General, Jiang Kang did indeed say those things that evening."

Ji Ying knew that voice.

She turned to look.

How is it him? The man who had been sitting beside Chen Shou — that man who kept speaking of Heaven's mandate?

He had come after me saying something about tomorrow being a new day, about pure talk being fine…

Is he not in league with the teahouse crowd?

Zhuge Zhan spoke slowly, one phrase at a time: "You have been honest. You are loyal subjects."

It was praise, yet Zhuge Zhan's face held no pleasure.

"You may detest him — that is your right. But as to whether Jiang Kang is truly a Wei spy, there is at present no solid evidence."

Zhuge Shang said: "Wei General, what he said was so offensive — does that not count as evidence? What more would he have to say?"

Zhuge Zhan said: "Words alone are not sufficient grounds for a criminal charge."

"But Wei General — he said Heaven's mandate belongs to the Sima house, that the Later Han has lost Heaven's favour. Is that not enough to make him a spy?" Xiaoyu asked.

Zhuge Zhan shook his head slowly.

"But Wei General, those were not ordinary words!" Zhuge Shang pressed. "That scoundrel fabricated a slander — claiming it was the Grand General who sent men to write the prophecy of ruin on the palace columns, to give himself a pretext. To invert right and wrong so completely — and still you will not arrest him?"

Zhuge Zhan shook his head again: "There is no law under which to charge him."

"Father!" Zhuge Shang's voice trembled. "If you let him go, people will say that even the son of Zhuge Wuhou5 himself believes Heaven's mandate has passed to the Sima house! How can we answer to our ancestors?"

"The Imperial Secretariat is not a place for airing family grievances!" Zhuge Zhan fixed Zhuge Shang with a sharp look.

Ji Ying thought of her father.

At least this is not a place where speaking gets you killed.

Seeing that Zhuge Shang was about to argue again, Zhuge Zhan raised a hand.

"Hear me out first. Then you may respond."

Zhuge Zhan rose to his feet — nearly as tall as Father.

Behind his head, the large plaque on the wall bore eight characters in heavy ink: Though Zhou was an ancient house, its mandate was renewed.6

The brushwork was dense and forceful, yet the turns of each stroke were unlike anything Ji Ying had seen — each stroke fell with deliberate weight.

Like Father at the forge in the bamboo grove, driving hammer after hammer.

Zhuge Zhan spoke unhurriedly: "Set aside for now whether the Later Han has — or ought to have — a law to punish him. Let us consider what happens next."

A low rumble of thunder sounded beyond the window; a slanted gust drove rain against the bamboo blinds with a soft, steady tapping.

"By making an arrest — and in the name of the Yulin Guards, no less — you have given people all the more reason to believe it truly was Grand General Jiang who sent men to write that prophecy on the palace columns, as a pretext to sweep away his opponents. Before, it was only Jiang Kang alone holding forth in a teahouse. Now that you have arrested him, it will not be just one person saying it any longer."

His tone carried no reproach. It was more like pressing on a bruise.

A breath of cool air lifted the bamboo blind; a scatter of fine rain pattered across the desk by the window.

"Last night, every official and student in that teahouse saw it: one man said something disagreeable, and the Yulin Guards burst in to arrest him. From now on, that entire group will be even more convinced that this corrupt court of the Later Han will come for them at any moment — and charge them for it."

Zhuge Zhan rose and lifted the bamboo blind. Through the gap between the buildings, the corner of the Imperial Academy square came into view.

In the distance, a dozen or so figures stood huddled under umbrellas at the edge of the square.

"Their numbers will only grow."

"If they keep growing, are we simply to stand by and do nothing?" Xiaoyu asked.

Zhuge Zhan shook his head: "We cannot arrest them."

He says only that they cannot be arrested…

What else is there to be done?

And yet in Luoyang, the Sima house and Zhong Hui had done far more than arresting people.

Did the students on the eastern market grounds in Luoyang grow in number, then?

Or were they frightened into silence — not one of them daring to speak another word?

And so, Heaven's mandate passed to Jin.

Fine rain fell across the roof tiles of the courtyards; clerks moved through the covered walkways below; outside the corridors, a few oiled-paper umbrellas drifted slowly past.

Zhuge Zhan continued: "And that is to say nothing of Jiang Kang's father — a field commander on the frontier. When he hears that his son has been arrested in Chengdu, he will not be easy in his mind. The enemy in the north, too — if word of last night reaches them, they will take notice. This is far from a small matter."

The enemy in the north — that means the Wei forces.

If I were a Wei spy…

I, too, would want a word with Jiang Kang's father.

The room fell quiet, filled only with the dull roll of thunder and the rain against the bamboo blind. Zhuge Shang stood with his head bowed and said nothing; Xiaoyu bit her lip; a long silence stretched on with no one speaking.

Then, abruptly, Zhuge Shang clasped his hands and said: "Wei General, this began with me. I did not think it through, and I have caused this much trouble. I am truly sorry."

"Wei General, I was the one who pressed hardest for this. The fault is mine. What can be done to make it right?"

"…I was the one who named him as a spy. Let me do something too."

Zhuge Zhan nodded: "He has already been released. Young people act on impulse — that is understandable. But the one who tied the knot must be the one to untie it. You will have to take responsibility."

Zhuge Zhan looked at Zhuge Shang: "Go and apologise to Jiang Kang. In public, where many people can see. Let them understand that the arrest was wrong."

Ji Ying thought of that night — the faces of Jiang Kang and his circle.

Nothing more than the son of some Right Grand General, currying favour on his father's behalf.

And Jiang Kang had said Father chased after fame and glory — that pure talk was useless.

What does he know?

Why should I have to apologise to him?

"That is all. There is something pressing these next few days; I must go to the palace to confer." Zhuge Zhan gathered the scrolls from his desk.

Several patches of ink on the scrolls had bloomed and blurred — they had caught the rain.

Then something slid from the pile and fell to the floor.

A single long feather, black and white.

Is that not from the satchel on the courier's back?

Zhuge Zhan glanced down at it and left it where it lay.

"Remember this: if they stand in the square and shout about Heaven's mandate, about Han or Wei, about the Grand General — none of that is against the law. Let them shout. And do not stand before them shouting your support for the Grand General's northern campaigns or the restoration of the Han."

Zhuge Shang took this badly: "They can shout whatever they like, but we cannot?"

Zhuge Zhan let out a quiet breath and glanced up at the plaque: Though Zhou was an ancient house, its mandate was renewed.

"This is the Land of Heavenly Bounty.7 It can hold a few words."

The Land of Heavenly Bounty? I have never heard that phrase before. Did they coin it themselves?

It can hold a few words.

Ji Ying thought again of the eastern market grounds.

Father. The students. The long blades. The dagger…

Zhuge Zhan's gaze moved to the clerk in the blue-green robe: "Zhuge Mao — you were not in a position to make the apology, but you bear responsibility too. Do your part."

"I am deeply sensible of my responsibility, and greatly troubled by it. Your nephew will find a way to soothe Jiang Kang and Yan Hong."

"While you are at it, look into what lies behind Yan Yu." The Wei General seemed about to say more — then stopped.

"Understood."

Nephew. He is not one of the teahouse crowd, on Jiang Kang's side?

And yet he is also Zhuge Zhan's nephew — and answerable to him?

Zhuge Zhan tucked the scrolls under his arm: "Do not let this drag on. Settle it among yourselves tonight." He stepped through the door, then turned back: "If you need a meal and a place to sleep, go home with Zhuge Shang."

Once Zhuge Zhan had gone, the young people released a collective breath of relief.

Ji Ying watched the clerk in the blue-green robe. He was propping his chin in his hand, lost in thought.

"You are a Zhuge as well?"

The clerk in blue-green came to himself, and bent in a bow: "Zhuge Mao,8 at your service. The mao of 'lush and flourishing.'"

"He is my elder brother." Xiaoyu said.

Ji Ying studied Zhuge Mao: average height, unremarkable features, beardless — neither handsome nor plain, the sort of face I might have passed a hundred times on a Chengdu street without registering it. His blue-green robe was the same one he had been wearing the night before; there was a small grease stain at the collar.

He does not look much like Xiaoyu.

Ji Ying thought back to the teahouse.

"You were sitting at Chen Shou's table. Whatever Yan Hong and Jiang Kang said — sending more people to the square, recalling Jiang Wei, claiming Jiang Wei had men write on the palace columns — you were in the thick of all of it."

Zhuge Mao looked a little embarrassed: "Miss Ji has a sharp memory. I only said what I was able to believe."

"So it was you who were embedded among them, passing word to Xiaoyu?"

Xiaoyu had said those men would definitely go to the teahouse that day.

Zhuge Mao gave a rueful smile: "I did not set out to infiltrate their circle. My friend Brother Chen happened to invite me along."

Zhuge Mao's eyes had narrowed to thin lines.

"So it was you who posted the Prince of Beidi's notice on my door?"

When the teahouse owner told me not to come back, Zhuge Mao was standing right at the door at that moment; and Xiaoyu already knew where I live.

Zhuge Mao lowered his head and clasped his hands high in an extended bow.

Ji Ying was still suspicious and was about to press further when someone entered — the man they had passed downstairs earlier.

"Great-Uncle Zhang." Zhuge Shang and the others bowed.

The great-uncle did not return the courtesy; he walked straight to the wall and shut the windows one by one.

"You are not happy about going to apologise. Are you."

Zhuge Shang nodded: "We were standing by what is right; he turned truth upside down — and now we are the ones who must apologise? Where is the justice in that? If we go and bow to him, won't it only make that scoundrel more insufferable?"

"I am only a soldier, but I have read the sages, and I have never read that a person of integrity must debase themselves like this. A jade vessel may be smashed — a resolve cannot be bent!"

"Very well — be a person of integrity." The great-uncle wiped a few drops of rainwater from the desk. "But we have not yet reached the point of smashing jade. Live a few more years."

The great-uncle turned, and his expression had settled into something warm.

"Did your father not explain it to you? You are not admitting fault to this man."

"Then to whom are we apologising?"

The great-uncle did not answer Zhuge Shang at once. Instead he settled himself slowly behind one of the side desks.

"In your estimation, of the people living in the Later Han today — how many want to restore the Han house, and how many want to surrender to the Sima house?"

Zhuge Shang and Xiaoyu exchanged a glance.

"Seven in ten for Han, three in ten for Sima?"

The great-uncle gave a short laugh: "Wildly wrong. Add those two together, and you would not reach one or two in ten. The great majority are ordinary people with no strong opinion either way."

No opinion?

The great-uncle went on: "Of those outside shouting for Jiang Wei's recall — how many truly believe what they are saying, and how many were simply pulled along to shout? Have any of them actually served under Jiang Wei, read his memorials, or come to know what manner of man he is?"

Great-Uncle Zhang looks old enough to have known Jiang Wei in person.

"They follow the crowd — and the world is full of people who follow the crowd," the great-uncle said. "They fear battle. They fear defeat. They fear death. Someone has told them that this man called Jiang Wei, this court of the Later Han, will lead them to defeat and send them to their deaths — and so they are willing to believe it. Your mother is an imperial princess; my sister is the empress; we are both kin to the Han imperial house. If the Han imperial house arrests people on a whim and makes those people fear the court even more — if suddenly the very act of opening your mouth becomes a crime — they will draw together all the more, and want all the more to see this dynasty replaced. Do you understand?"

A murmur of understanding went through the young people.

"Strength of arms alone is not enough. We must learn to win people's hearts."

And Yan Yu, and Yan Hong — they too are working to win people's hearts.

Must hearts be won? Can people not judge for themselves?

The great-uncle unrolled a scroll on the desk: "Are you loyal subjects of the emperor?"

"Of course!"

"Do you wish to preserve the foundation of the Later Han?"

"Of course we do!"

"Then do not, at this moment, drive even those who never thought of surrendering to start thinking about it." The great-uncle extended one finger toward the door.

Zhuge Shang and Xiaoyu put their heads together, and agreed they would go to the Zhuge household to talk it over.

Ji Ying stood where she was and watched the three of them conferring.

The throne, the dynasty, the Zhuge house, the Liu house…

What has the Later Han's foundation to do with me?

"Little Sister Ji Ying — come have dinner at the Zhuge house? There is a qin there — the very one Zhuge Wuhou used to drive back the enemy!"

1 Imperial Secretariat (尚書台): The central administrative organ of the Han and Wei dynasties, responsible for receiving, processing, and dispatching all official documents and imperial decrees. By the late Later Han period, it had become the effective hub of day-to-day government. ↩

2 Three pheasant feathers (三根雉羽): Urgent military dispatches were marked by inserting feathers into the satchel — the more feathers, the greater the urgency. Three feathers signalled the highest level of emergency. ↩

3 Zhang Shao (張紹): Second son of Zhang Fei, one of the founding generals of Shu Han. Zhang Shao's sister was the Empress Zhang, consort of Liu Shan (劉禪, the reigning emperor); their daughter married Zhuge Zhan and became Zhuge Shang's mother. ↩

4 Xunzi (荀子): The collected works of Xun Kuang (c. 310–235 BCE), a Confucian philosopher who argued that human nature requires shaping through ritual, education, and institutional design. ↩

5 Zhuge Wuhou (諸葛武侯): The posthumous title of Zhuge Liang (諸葛亮, 181–234 CE), Chancellor and architect of the Shu Han state. Wuhou — "Martial Marquis" — was the honorific by which he was worshipped in Chengdu to this day. ↩

6 Though Zhou was an ancient house, its mandate was renewed (周雖舊邦，其命維新): A line from the Book of Odes (Shijing), "Ode to King Wen." It was later adopted as a classical formulation of reform and renewal — that age is no obstacle to regeneration if the will to renew is present. ↩

7 Land of Heavenly Bounty (天府之國): A traditional epithet for the Chengdu Plain and the Sichuan basin, referring to its extraordinary agricultural fertility and natural defences. ↩
