Yanxing: The Rekindling of Han · Preface

Preface (III)

← Preface (II) Contents Preface (IV) →

The last gathering of the Seven Sages of the Bamboo Grove had taken place four years ago. In the years since, Ji Kang had wandered across the land, seeking out reclusive scholars and also avoiding the vengeance of Zhong Hui—until the day he decided to return to Luoyang.

Cold wind pressed down the branches, yet it could not lift the scattered dead leaves lying across the stone pavement. Rows of humble houses stood shoulder to shoulder; market towers and pavilions rose one after another. Men and women, young and old, filled every level. On this day the alleys of the Eastern Market of Luoyang were packed beyond passage.

Three thousand students from the Imperial Academy had crowded into the execution ground. Troops of the Commandant of the Capital surrounded the platform, their halberds and axes gleaming in tight ranks. Rain had fallen earlier in the afternoon, and droplets still clung to the sleeved iron armor of the soldiers.

Ji Ying knelt among the front rows of the academy students. Her rain-soaked robe clung tightly to her skin. She trembled with cold, yet she did not lean against the students beside her for warmth.

To her right sat an academy student named Zhao Jun. He was eighteen, tall in stature, with a narrow pointed head and sharp black-and-white eyes. From time to time he glanced toward Ji Ying.

Memories of her childhood in Mount Yuntai with her father filled Ji Ying's eyes with tears.

At the edge of the execution platform were her mother and younger brother, dressed in mourning hemp. On the platform itself sat two figures.

The taller one was her father, Ji Kang.

The shorter one was Lü An, whom she called Uncle Lü.

"Discussion of ideas is no crime!"

"Release Grand Master Ji!"

Zhao Jun and the other students shouted the slogans again and again. From dawn until evening their voices had grown hoarse.

"Let the Son of Heaven grant a special pardon!"

"We ask to study under Master Ji Shuye!"

Ji Ying did not hope for a miracle. In her heart she had already prepared for the worst.

After Sima Zhao had murdered the emperor,33 his power dominated the Central Plains. Her father's death sentence had been personally ordered by him. What could the newly installed Wei emperor—only two years older than Ji Ying—possibly do?

Also present were Shan Tao, Gentleman of the Ministry of Personnel, and Ruan Ji, Commandant of the Infantry. They too were powerless, sitting beside her mother and brother.

The sun finally broke through a gap in the clouds. Rays of evening light slipped between the buildings of the Eastern Market. One beam happened to fall upon Ji Kang.

In that glow, his white robes shone all the more unbowed and uncompromising.

"Lü An of Dongping!" the execution officer shouted.

Inside and outside the market all sound ceased.

"Unfilial toward his mother, corrupting the moral order of the age!
Beheaded in the marketplace!"

Lü An slowly stood and walked to the front of the platform. Then he knelt down, his eyes vacant.

"Do you have any final words?"

"The annals of history will vindicate me!"

Lü An raised his arms and roared. The iron chains at his wrists clattered loudly.

The execution officer signaled the executioner.

The man gripped a ring-pommel saber with both hands. The blade, six feet long, slowly rose.

Suddenly Lü An began trembling and laughing wildly.

"Innocence! Innocence! Ha! Ha ha ha!"

The blade fell.

A cry rose from the execution ground. Some covered their faces, others turned away. Blood spurted three feet high from the severed neck. Lü An's body collapsed heavily. His head rolled aside.

An execution guard picked it up, placed it into a wooden box, and handed it to a weeping family member dressed in mourning hemp.

Ji Ying caught a glimpse of Lü An's final expression.

His eyes were wide open. His mouth hung open, drenched in blood.

"Ji Kang of Qiao Commandery!"

"Inciting the masses and corrupting the moral order of the age!
Beheaded in the marketplace!"

Ji Kang slowly stood.

He walked forward and sat down calmly in the pool of blood Lü An had left behind.

"Any final words?" the officer asked.

"Allow me to play a piece upon the qin."

Ji Kang's expression was exactly as it had always been.

The officer hesitated.

Below the platform, the academy students had already brought forth a qin, the seven-string Chinese zither.

The music of the Bamboo Grove's blacksmith still soared and surged—yet the six sages who once sang with such ardor alongside him were gone.

Since Ji Kang had returned from his wandering, Ji Ying had often heard her father play this very piece.

It was called The Guangling Melody.34

The music struck like an axe splitting bamboo, like a sword piercing armor. It soared like cranes crying along sheer cliffs, like gibbons howling through empty valleys. It thundered like a waterfall crashing upon rocks, shattering cold mountain springs.

So pure was this music—asking nothing more than to reach the emperor above and move the common people below.

Yet the emperor was far away within the palace city.

And between Ji Kang and the common people stood three thousand academy students.

Ji Ying heard the Guangling Melody.

She heard the quiet sobbing of the academy students.

She also heard the endless murmuring of the townspeople watching from the market towers.

Ji Kang's music was too far from them.

Ji Ying looked up at the vast grey clouds overhead.

She touched the scabbard at her waist. It was the weapon her father had forged for her protection.

A blacksmith without want. A father steadfast and true.

The "perfect man" she had always believed him to be had met such an end.

Heaven had no eyes.

Heaven itself deserved punishment.

The music stopped.

The execution officer gestured for the executioner to step forward.

Silence fell across the entire Eastern Market.

Ji Kang turned and looked at the long shadow cast by his body across the execution platform.

"In the past," he said slowly, "Yuan Xiaoni of Chen Commandery wished to learn the Guangling Melody from me. I refused to teach him. From this day forward, the melody will become a lost sound."

He placed the qin beside him and closed his eyes.

The saber slowly rose.

Suddenly Ruan Ji, standing beside Ji Ying's younger brother, shouted in fury:

"Innocent! Innocent!"

At once the academy students in the square shouted the same words.

"Innocent! Innocent!"

Before ten voices had cried out, the call spread to a hundred. From a hundred to a thousand, wave upon wave, surging like a flood tide.

The six-foot blade rose behind Ji Kang.

Ji Ying took a deep breath and forced her eyes wide open.

She would watch her father through this final moment.

The blade fell.

The shouting stopped instantly.

There was no pain upon Ji Kang's face.

Ji Ying felt a dark dizziness.

It was as if she had fallen into the deep green pool of Mount Yuntai. Through the cold water she could still faintly hear voices above.

"They framed a virtuous man!"

"Conscience is dead!"

"Heaven will punish them!"

Three thousand academy students rose to their feet, arms raised, roaring in anger. The sound shook the sky.

The back rows pushed the front rows forward.

Ji Ying was carried unwillingly toward the execution platform.

The front ranks of students crashed into the soldiers' armored line. The soldiers raised their halberds and spears, blocking the students who surged forward like a furious tide.

In the chaos Ji Ying saw her father's body still seated upright upon the platform.

Then an armored guard grabbed his arm and dragged it.

The body collapsed.

Her father, who had never bowed to power in his entire life—

How could he be dragged away by a mere soldier?

"Dog thief!"

Ji Ying forced her way through the crowd like a madwoman. With several swift strides she reached the front ranks.

Three lines of iron-clad soldiers could no longer withstand the fury of three thousand students. Their formation shrank backward toward the platform.

"Let go of my father!"

Ji Ying shouted wildly and threw herself toward a gap between two soldiers.

Perhaps the soldiers had heard she was Ji Kang's daughter and showed mercy. Perhaps Ji Ying's mountain upbringing had given her agile strength.

Somehow she slipped through.

A group of academy students followed behind her, crashing through the breach in the line. The opening tore wider.

At that point the dark robes and blue robes of the scholars poured forward like a collapsing dam.

Ji Ying and Zhao Jun were swept with the tide.

"Protect Master Ji's body!"

The students rushed up the execution platform shouting.

Several execution guards saw the students charging like wild beasts. They stumbled backward, drew their swords.

One student rushed forward—

"Ah!"

Blood splashed across the patterned stone tiles.

"Soldiers are killing students!"

"Murder!"

One fallen student brought ten more charging forward. They seized an execution guard, wrestled him down, and took his weapon.

Other guards believed a rebellion had begun. They turned their axes downward and struck.

White blades entered.

Red blades emerged.

A student cried out softly as a guard kicked him down and trampled him.

More students surged forward, grabbing spears, struggling face to face, pulling from behind, wrenching weapons away and stabbing the guards in rage.

For a moment the execution platform was filled with screams and blood.

Amid the chaos Ji Ying searched desperately around the platform.

But she could not find Ji Kang's body.

Through the gaps between people she suddenly saw an execution guard lifting a qin from the ground.

"My father's qin!"

Ji Ying shouted and ran forward. She seized the tail of the instrument with both hands.

The guard was huge, his face fierce.

"Filthy peasant! Get lost!"

Ji Ying was thin and weak. The guard dragged her across the platform.

Suddenly two more hands grabbed the qin.

It was Zhao Jun.

"Leave Master Ji's qin!"

The guard roared in anger and raised a long three-pronged iron spear.

He thrust downward.

Ji Ying rolled aside. The spear struck a chain on the ground, scattering sparks.

Zhao Jun lost his grip on the qin and fell. The guard stepped on the instrument and stabbed toward Zhao Jun again.

But Zhao Jun abandoned the qin and scrambled backward. The guard pursued, and he leapt off the platform and fled.

Ji Ying picked up the qin.

Part of it had been snapped and crushed underfoot. Blood stained its surface—she could not tell whether it belonged to Lü An or her father.

Fury burned in her eyes.

She drew the steel dagger from her waist.

The blade shone cold and bright.

Ji Ying raised it high, aiming at the strip of flesh between the guard's armored collar and helmet.

"Ha!"

She leapt upward with all her weight and drove the dagger down.

The guard screamed.

He collapsed to the ground.

When Ji Ying pulled out the dagger, a thin jet of blood burst from the wound.

The man's face twisted in agony. Blood sprayed across the execution platform.

Ji Ying froze.

She had grown up roaming the mountains and had always been swift and agile.

But this was the first time she had truly wounded a man.

Blood of her father.

Blood of Lü An.

Blood of the academy students.

Blood of the soldiers.

All of it mixed beneath the chaotic footprints across the platform.

The screams of the soldiers.

The roars of the students.

The final echo of the Guangling Melody.

The gasp of the crowd when the blade fell.

All seemed to float above the cold green pool in her mind.

None of it felt real.

She came back to her senses.

Before her was only a mass of chaos. Zhao Jun had already disappeared in the confusion.

Suddenly a voice called from behind her.

"Wait!"

Ji Ying spun around sharply, her dagger already angling for a strike.

"Ah!"

A startled cry rang out.

Looking closely, she saw a student in purple robes sitting on the ground, pale with fright.

"…Are you Ji Kang's daughter, Ji Yanshao?"

Ji Ying's right hand still gripped the dagger tightly, cold and stiff.

The student raised a trembling finger and pointed at the broken qin lying on the ground.

"Forget the qin!"

"That is my father's qin!"

"No!"

Ji Ying looked again.

The qin was light-colored, and the position markers were made of wood. In the urgency of the moment she had not noticed.

"Where is my father's qin?"

"This place is dangerous. I… I will take you to get it. Come with me quickly!"

Ji Ying saw that the student's eyes were swollen and red. His voice was hoarse but carried a softness. His purple robe was still damp from the rain.

"…Alright."

Ji Ying could not bring her father back.

But she had to recover the qin—the instrument Ji Kang had loved most in his life. She would guard it forever. When she saw it, it would be like seeing her father again.

Amid the chaos of the Eastern Market, the purple-robed student led Ji Ying quickly toward a corner of the execution ground. He knocked loudly on the door of an inconspicuous house.

After several knocks the door opened slightly, revealing the face of an old servant.

The student hurried Ji Ying inside and shut the door and windows tightly behind them.

All at once the world became quiet.

Ji Ying felt dazed.

"I'm sorry, Miss Ji. You should really have time to rest. But things outside are chaotic. Soldiers will soon seal off the Eastern Market."

The student led Ji Ying through a simple front hall and kitchen. Before opening the back door he carefully stepped onto a table and peered out through a small square window, looking left and right.

"Quick—while no one is there!"

Ji Ying felt as if she were walking in a dream. She could hardly think and stared at him in confusion.

"What happened was," the student explained. "When Master Ji was arrested, his qin was confiscated. Several of us students managed to request it and hid it temporarily in the dormitories of the Imperial Academy."

He smiled faintly.

"The qin destroyed earlier was mine."

He looked relieved.

"That instrument was fortunate enough to have Master Ji play a piece upon it and to let three thousand kindred listeners hear it. Its name will be remembered in history—it has not lived in vain. Though I fear it was made in Eastern Wu and hardly worthy of Master Ji."

He paused.

"Master Ji's own qin should naturally be returned to his family."

Ji Ying did not fully follow his explanation. She only sensed that his words were gentle and refined. His features were delicate, and his voice soft.

"…Thank you."

"Shall we go?"

The student stepped toward the door, then suddenly stopped and turned back.

"Ah! I forgot to introduce myself. Very rude of me. My surname is Yang, courtesy name Changde. I come from the Mount Tai Commandery."

Ji Ying nodded and followed him outside.

The alleyways were dark and narrow. If she stretched out her arms she could touch both brick walls. Even raising her hand brought her fingers against the roof tiles above.

Changde hurried ahead in small steps while Ji Ying followed behind in a daze.

At every intersection he stopped, looked carefully to both sides, then leaned close to a street marker set into the wall to confirm the direction.

After passing seven or eight intersections and turning three corners, the path suddenly opened.

Before them lay a broad avenue.

Great mansions lined both sides. Pavilions glittered with luxury, towers rose in elegance, and the residences of powerful families stretched deep within their walls.

At the far end of the avenue stood the various government offices. Beyond them rose the palace city, magnificent gates and towers surrounding a vast central hall.

Ji Ying suddenly remembered something the elders had once said.

Two years earlier, beneath one of those palace gates, the Wei emperor Cao Mao had been pierced through by a spear and killed.

"The Imperial Academy lies to the southeast," Changde said quietly. "To avoid suspicion from the guards, it would be best if you hide your weapon and walk calmly like other passersby."

So Ji Ying walked beside him along the avenue.

People flowed along the road. Ox carts and sedan chairs moved through the crowd, their parasols bright with color.

Two middle-aged women in colorful robes walked ahead of them.

When Ji Ying drew closer she heard their conversation—and they were speaking about her father.

"Why did Ji Kang have to die?"

"He only has himself to blame. He befriended the wrong person! That friend beat his own mother!"

"Serves him right!"

The words jolted Ji Ying fully back to the streets of Luoyang.

"That is not true," she murmured. "Lü An was framed by his own elder brother."

The two women heard her.

"What would a young girl like you know?"

Ji Ying answered loudly, "That villain violated Lü An's wife and then accused him first!"35

"Hmph! A rapist and a man who beats his mother—none of that family can be good people! Birds of a feather flock together. How good could Ji Kang be?" the woman on the right said with hands on her hips.

Ji Ying shouted angrily, "Ji Kang was innocent!"

Passersby began turning their heads.

Seeing her fury, the two women quickly moved aside, glaring resentfully as they muttered to themselves.

After several hundred more steps they passed a large three-story residence.

An old man and his grandson stepped down from a sedan chair, dressed in fine silk.

As they prepared to enter their home the old man was lecturing the boy—and again the topic was Ji Kang.

"When you grow up, never become like Ji Kang. Standing alone against the court—how could he avoid suffering?"

Ji Ying glared at the old man.

He continued speaking to his grandson.

"You may read many books, but it is more important to know how to live. 'The highest goodness is like water.' Only by remaining flexible can one endure."

Ji Ying trembled with anger and pointed at him. "Is that living as a human? That is living like pigs and dogs!"

The old man's lips quivered, unable to answer. His grandson burst into tears.

Changde pleaded softly, "Please—let it go. Let's go."

Just then a carriage drawn by two horses rolled directly toward them.

Changde and the grandfather stepped aside, but Ji Ying remained standing in the road.

There were four people in the carriage. The leading man was well dressed, his face flushed with drink.

"Move aside! Trying to let me run you down and extort money from me? Not a coin will you get!"

Seeing Ji Ying glaring at him, he sneered drunkenly.

"Oh! Such a vicious-looking girl! What family raised such a shrew?"

"My father is Ji Kang!"

"Never heard of him. Must be some insignificant clerk!"

Ji Ying exploded with rage. "Worse than pigs and dogs!"

She drew her dagger and leapt onto the carriage in three swift steps. In an instant the blade was pressed against the man's throat.

Then she suddenly remembered the chaos at the execution ground and pulled back slightly.

The drunken man stared wide-eyed, trembling with the smell of wine.

The servants in the carriage shouted.

"Don't hurt the young master!"

"We'll give you money—whatever you want!"

Below the carriage Changde shouted, "The Imperial Academy is just around the corner!"

"Why wasn't it you who died today!"

Ji Ying cursed.

She did not want to injure him again, so she reversed the dagger and struck him hard across the nose with the handle.

The man screamed.

Ji Ying leapt down from the carriage. Changde grabbed her arm and pulled her away.

Behind them the drunken man shouted hoarsely, "You think academy students are so great? You're only fit to serve me!"

Ji Ying started to shout back, but Changde only murmured again and again, "Let it go… let it go…"

"Do such people deserve to be called human?"

Ji Ying panted with anger. Though her mind was no longer clouded, she felt exhausted and hollow.

Changde sighed softly.

"Luoyang has always been like this. After a while people grow used to it."

He led her through several more turns until another broad avenue appeared before them.

Not far away stood a magnificent gate.

Above it hung a great plaque: Imperial Academy of Luoyang.

Before the gate stood dozens of stone steles taller than a man.

Ji Ying stepped closer.

Both sides of each stele were carved with dense lines of clerical script. The last light of the setting sun reflected across the characters like silver sparks.

Ji Ying faintly remembered her father once telling her about these steles.

Changde pointed at one particularly tall stone and smiled shyly.

"This stele of the Stone Classics was written by my grandmother's father. His calligraphy is beautiful, isn't it?"

Ji Ying thought silently, My own great-grandfather was the Chancellor Cao Cao. Yet the Cao family is now almost wiped out.

Looking carefully, she saw the carved text was from the Analects.

"The Stone Classics contain the Confucian texts?"

"Yes. The Seven Confucian Classics. For nearly a century the Imperial Academy has used them as the standard for teaching and interpretation."

Ji Ying immediately lost interest when she heard talk of teaching and textual commentary. She started toward the academy gate.

But Changde suddenly stopped.

Inside the academy grounds soldiers in iron armor were already standing guard. Blue banners fluttered beside the main hall.

"No matter. We will use the back entrance," he whispered.

They slipped into the narrow alleys beside the academy buildings.

Ji Ying grew increasingly frustrated and finally shook off his hand.

"Hiding from rats on the left and running from watchdogs on the right—why can't people live openly and uprightly?"

Changde slowed his steps.

"Because too few dare to live as true human beings."

"Were there not three thousand academy students in the Eastern Market today?"

Changde hesitated.

"I hope so. But my father is an official. I have heard him say that many scholars read books only to bow before fame and profit. They praise those in power."

"When the next righteous man is persecuted, they will remain silent to protect themselves."

He tapped lightly on a bronze ritual cauldron standing beneath the academy gate.

"From students of the Imperial Academy to officials of the state temples, everyone seems eager to step into that cauldron—to be stained by the muddy water inside until they become numb and blackened."

Ji Ying spat angrily, "A shameful world! Shameful people!"

Changde nodded.

"Today they slaughter the finest minds of the nation. It is like the persecutions of the Partisan Prohibitions36 under the late Han emperors Huan and Ling."

He paused and lowered his voice.

"…It would have been better if the students had not clashed with the soldiers. Zhong Hui's spies are everywhere in the capital. He is narrow-minded and ruthless."

"Yanshao, you must conceal your identity. Speak cautiously and avoid quarrels, or you may attract Zhong Hui's attention."

Ji Ying already hated Zhong Hui. Hearing him call her by her courtesy name annoyed her.

"Call me Ji Ying. Ying, like the Ying of Chunyu Tiying.37 No need for courtesy names."

Changde smiled.

"Truly worthy of Master Ji's daughter. Ji Ying—an excellent name! My own given name is very ordinary. It comes from the Daoist text Laozi:40 'When constant virtue is complete, one returns to the original (樸，Pu).' My name is Pu. I am twenty-three this year. A pleasure to meet you."

"A pleasure. Do you like Laozi?"

Changde thought for a moment.

"I would not say I understand the Laozi. But I love listening to Master Ji explain it."

"Where did you hear him?"

"During these past two years he lectured at the Imperial Academy on Laozi and Zhuangzi,41 and used metaphysical philosophy to reinterpret the Stone Classics. Students filled the corridors and even stood outside the windows."

"His criticisms of classical scholarship were sharp and profound. Just as his poem says: 'The hidden dragon nurtures the spirit body, its scales leaping in the orchid pool.'"

"How fortunate we were."

Ji Ying suddenly asked, "Then why did Sima Zhao want to kill my father?"

Changde replied,

"Compared with Master Ji, the court-loyal scholars in the academy can only repeat empty Confucian phrases. When they lose an argument they resort to insults and shouting."

"Zhong Hui once told Sima Zhao: 'The Duke of Jin need not worry about the realm. Only Ji Kang must be feared. He is a sleeping dragon—he must never be allowed to rise.'"

Ji Ying clenched her teeth. "Zhong Hui, that villain!"

Changde said quietly,

"A few of us academy students have been deeply inspired by Master Ji. We wish to live simply and never betray our principles."

"We have already begun compiling copies of Master Ji's writings so that his words and deeds may be passed down. Future generations must remember his spirit."

Through her grief and anger, warmth suddenly stirred in Ji Ying's heart.

Though her father had died, his spirit had moved those who came after him.

"…Thank you."

Changde continued,

"We will take care of Master Ji's legacy. But you injured a soldier today. The authorities may pursue the matter."

"Tonight I will take you to retrieve the qin and then escort you to reunite with your mother and brother. They are currently staying at the residence of Shan Juyuan of the Ministry of Personnel. You may hide there for the time being."

Ji Ying sighed gloomily.

When Father wandered four years ago, I stayed less than a year at Uncle Shan's home before falling ill from boredom. Who knows how long I must endure it this time?"

She thought again:

Father committed no crime yet suffered execution. Today I truly injured someone and have become a criminal. Father always said my temper was too fierce. Now he is gone, and I have already caused trouble. How can I face his spirit in heaven?

Then another thought came:

But if I become weak and retreat, that would also betray him.

Changde asked suddenly, "Do you have a husband? If Zhong Hui seeks revenge, he should be warned as well."

Ji Ying frowned. "I am fifteen. Why would I have a husband?"

"You are only fifteen? Yet you are taller than I am!"

He looked at her with surprise.

"I guessed because the academy student who seized the qin with you earlier sat beside you all day. His eyes never left you…"

Changde looked embarrassed.

"That was Zhao Jun. He was the one who brought my father back from Ye Commandery. Since then he has been close to our family."

She remembered Zhao Jun fleeing earlier.

"I understood him long ago. He wants to become a great official so his parents can enjoy wealth and glory. I would never marry such a worldly man."

Changde gave a helpless smile.

"…But that is also a kind of filial devotion."

"What is filial devotion worth!"

Changde paused before speaking again.

"Perhaps you are right. One always knows in the heart what kind of person suits oneself. To entrust one's life to the wrong person brings suffering forever."

He sighed quietly.

"Yet sometimes when one meets the right person and cannot be together… the regret lasts a lifetime. Fate can be cruel."

Ji Ying stepped a little closer and smelled a faint scent of flowers.

She had already guessed the truth.

"It seems you are Sister Changde."

Changde nodded awkwardly.

"I should have told you earlier. The Imperial Academy only admits male students, so I had no choice."

"How did you know?"

"You are obviously a woman."

Changde looked doubtful.

"Really? Some classmates often ask me to study together… could it be because they know?"

Ji Ying thought silently of the famous beauty He Yan,42 whom her mother often praised.

"Not necessarily," she said calmly. "Ruan Ji once wrote a poem: 'The splendid youths of old—Anling and Longyang43… may we become two birds flying wing to wing.'"

"Perhaps they take you for Lord Anling or Lord Longyang."

"That will not do," Changde said, shaking her head.

Then she suddenly blushed.

"Since you mentioned that poem… I have long wondered whether Ruan Ji wrote it for Master Ji…"

Ji Ying shrugged.

"Aunt Ran once suspected that too. She even drilled a hole in the wall to spy on them when my father and Uncle Ruan stayed overnight."

"She imagined too much. My father merely admired him. The poem was meant for the world to read."

"Truly worthy of the Seven Sages of the Bamboo Grove—beyond rigid morality, following nature!"

As they talked about Ji Kang's past, night gradually fell.

They reached the back gate of the academy dormitories.

Buildings of various sizes stretched before them. Torches flickered in the distance, and voices could be heard shouting.

Changde took Ji Ying's hand and led her toward the dormitory halls.

Suddenly a harsh voice shouted from behind them.

"You two in white and purple robes—come here for questioning!"

Changde's whole body froze.

Ji Ying turned sharply.

A short, thin officer in leather armor stood before them. His face was narrow and monkey-like, with sharp suspicious eyes.

He carried twin swords at his waist.

Behind him more than ten academy students in plain robes approached, holding bronze lamps.

33 Sima Zhao's murder of the emperor: In 260, the young Wei emperor Cao Mao, frustrated by the Sima family's stranglehold on power, personally led a desperate palace coup. He was cut down in the street by soldiers under Sima Zhao's command—the first time in Chinese history a subject had openly killed a reigning emperor. Sima Zhao suffered no serious consequences.

34 The Guangling Melody (Guangling San, 《廣陵散》): An ancient qin composition of unknown origin, celebrated for its turbulent, defiant character. Ji Kang's performance of it at his execution became one of the most famous scenes in Chinese literary history. He declared it a lost melody upon his death, as he had refused to teach it to anyone.

35 The Lü An case: Lü An's elder brother Lü Xun had violated Lü An's wife. Rather than face accusation, Lü Xun struck first and reported Lü An for striking his own mother—a capital offense under the principle of filial piety. Zhong Hui used the case as a pretext to bring Ji Kang down alongside his friend.

36 Partisan Prohibitions (Danggu, 黨錮): Two waves of political purges under Han emperors Huan (r. 146–168) and Ling (r. 168–189), in which eunuch factions systematically imprisoned, banned from office, or executed hundreds of scholar-officials and their associates. The persecutions became a byword for the destruction of integrity by corrupt power.

37 Chunyu Tiying (淳于緹縈): A young woman of the early Han dynasty who petitioned Emperor Wen to abolish mutilating punishments in order to save her father's life. Her courage moved the emperor to reform the penal code. She became an enduring symbol of filial devotion and female moral courage. The character 縈 (yíng) in her name means "to encircle" or "to linger in the heart."

40 Laozi (老子, Lǎozǐ): The founding text of Daoist philosophy, traditionally attributed to the sage Laozi ("Old Master"), likely compiled in the 4th–3rd century BCE. Its eighty-one chapters explore the nature of the Dao, non-action (wu wei), and return to natural simplicity.

41 Zhuangzi (莊子, Zhuāngzǐ): A 4th-century BCE Daoist philosopher and the text attributed to him. Celebrated for its imaginative parables and radical questioning of conventional distinctions—between life and death, self and world, knowledge and ignorance.

42 He Yan (何晏, c. 195–249): A celebrated Wei official renowned equally for his philosophical brilliance and his striking beauty—said to have powdered his face and been admired by men and women alike. He was among those executed when Sima Yi overthrew Cao Shuang's faction. He was also the stepson of Cao Cao, and thus a distant connection of Ji Ying's mother.

43 Anling and Longyang (安陵君、龍陽君): Two figures from the Warring States era celebrated for the devotion of their male patrons—Anling by King Xuan of Wei, Longyang by King Wei of Wei. Their names became classical allusions for intimate male companionship. Ruan Ji's poem invoking them was deliberately ambiguous in its emotional register.
