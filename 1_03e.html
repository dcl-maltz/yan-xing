Yanxing: The Rekindling of Han · Book One: The Land of Heavenly Bounty

Book One: (III)

← Book One (II) Contents → Book One (IV)

Bells and drums, the morning cock — all sounding at once.

Ji Ying lay sprawled in bed, staring sidelong at her father's qin.

After last night's scene at the teahouse, there is no going back.

I will have to go and beg the ironmonger again, hammering coin trees.

Ji Ying dragged herself out the door into the glare of the morning sun. She turned to lock the door — and found a notice posted outside.

Four characters in clerical script on yellow paper:

Musician Wanted

Qin, se, zhu, zheng, pipa.1 The character for qin was written noticeably larger than the rest.

Starting immediately

Remuneration negotiable

Beside the text, a hand-drawn map with an arrow pointing straight to: the Prince of Beidi's residence.2

The Prince of Beidi? Who was that?

There were no identical notices anywhere else on the street.

What a coincidence — a royal household advertising for a musician, and one notice, on my door alone?

Ji Ying thought of the night before last, when Zhuge Yu had shown up uninvited.

Did Xiaoyu put this here?

I need to stop at the market first and buy new qin strings…


This was the place marked on the map.

The front gate was red-lacquered, the paint flaking and patchy, barely grander than an ordinary townhouse.

This is really a prince's residence? Where the emperor's own son lives?

An old manservant opened the gate.

"I am here about the musician position." Ji Ying held up the notice.

In the courtyard stood a tall, ancient tree, its branches thick and spreading, leaves dense — the whole thing tilting and reaching until it cast half the compound in shade.

The old manservant led Ji Ying to the front hall to wait.

Ji Ying settled herself and thought she ought to make a good impression on the master of the house; she began to play the qin softly.

The room was simply furnished: a few calligraphy scrolls hung on the walls, weapon racks with spears and halberds leaned against the corners, stacks of bamboo books piled haphazardly nearby, and from one of the side rooms came the sound of a baby crying.

The residence was not large, but several servants hurried back and forth, carrying bamboo poles, banners, and bolts of cloth.

Ji Ying caught fragments of their low conversation — names, lists of supplies, that sort of thing.

The baby in the side room kept crying without pause. Ji Ying felt her patience fraying; she shifted to gentler pieces, Yangchun and Baixue.

By the second half of Baixue, the crying from the side room had slowly died away. The corner of Ji Ying's mouth lifted slightly.

She played on alone for nearly half a shichen. It was approaching noon, and still no servant had come to attend to her; she had no idea whether this "Prince of Beidi" had even heard her playing, or what he thought of it. Then she caught a burst of sound from behind the building — the crack and clatter of wooden staves, and men calling out "Heh!" and "Ha!" as they drilled.

Qin strings, wooden staves, heh-and-ha calls, servant footsteps, the scrape of things being moved, low voices…

The baby in the side room started up again, and all the sounds crashed together in a wall of noise. Ji Ying felt her head throb and stopped playing.

She looked around. The long-handled weapons propped against the walls were each a wooden pole topped with a short blade — some blades pointing straight forward, some turned horizontally to one side, some combining both. The shafts varied in thickness, the points in length; Ji Ying did not know any of their names.

She sat idle a while longer. Still no one came to speak a word to her, and the noise all around showed no sign of letting up. Ji Ying picked up her qin and followed the sound of the staves to the back of the compound. She rounded the old tree and found herself at a shoulder-height bamboo fence, behind which some twenty young men were sparring in pairs, staves cracking against each other, sweat flying in the fierce midday sun.

One of the young men noticed her:

"Who are you looking for?"

"I am here to audition as a musician." Ji Ying held up her qin.

The young man exchanged a quick bow with his sparring partner and walked toward her. He had a narrow, angular face, sparse whiskers, hollow cheeks — but a pair of protruding eyes that burned with intelligence. He wore plain cloth in blue and grey, the cuffs slightly worn but not a trace of grease or grime anywhere. A black cloth sash was wrapped at his waist, with a short scabbard hanging to the right.

About the same length as the dagger Father made for me.

Is this one of the stave-fighting instructors?

"You are the musician?"

Ji Ying handed him the "Musician Wanted" notice.

He wiped the sweat from his face and read it, then broke into a loud laugh. "This is surely Zhuge Mao's doing!"

Another Zhuge. What was his name again?

"Have I come to the wrong place?"

"Not at all!" the man said. "It may not look like much, but this is indeed the Prince of Beidi's residence. Your accent is not from around here — where are you from, may I ask?"

"Ji Ying. From Shanyang, in Henei. In Wei."

"A Wei woman?" His expression shifted slightly. "How long have you been in the Later Han?"

"A little over half a year."

He gave a small nod.

"And when would the audition be?"

He smiled. "Right now. Play for me."

A stave-fighting instructor oversees the household's music as well?

"Is there anything in particular you would like to hear?"

He thought for a moment, then brightened with sudden enthusiasm. "The Odes3 say: How the deer call out — aye, aye — feeding on the fieldwort. I have admirable guests; the lutes and the pipes sound. Since you have come all the way from Wei, would you play Luming4 for me?"

Ji Ying nodded.

She closed her eyes and let herself picture that open mountain meadow, a young deer picking through the grass.

Her fingers began lightly, a gentle pluck, the strings trembling — early morning in the hills, mist still low.

Soon the sound filled out, the bass strings settling into a steady pulse, as the deer called to each other and came stepping, one by one, from the trees.

Here and there a harmonic rose like a single note on the wind, brushing the grass, stirring the shadows at the forest's edge.

Toward the end, her fingers drew tighter for just a moment — a thread of wariness woven into the harmony — then released, leaving only the resonance to fade.

"Excellent! Magnificent!" The man broke into loud applause.

And from across the yard, the young men who had been sparring with their staves — Ji Ying had not noticed them stop — were standing behind the fence with both arms raised, cheering and clapping.

"Your technique, young lady — the steadiness of your wrists, the quickness of your fingers — you are not someone who only knows how to play the qin."

What?

Without warning, he tossed a short spike sideways through the air. Ji Ying caught the glint of it and saw that if she let it fall, it would land squarely on the qin; her hand shot out and snatched it.

In that reach, she bared the scar on her forearm — three or four inches long.

Ji Ying returned the spike to him. He smiled again.

"…Is there anything else you would like to hear?"

He was still considering when the old manservant came hurrying out and said to him: "Your Highness — Zhuge Shang and Zhuge Yu have arrived."

Ah. This man is the prince?

Ji Ying had grown up on Mount Yuntai; she had no idea how to salute a prince. She scrambled to her feet and gave a deep bow.

"No need, no need. None of that with me." The Prince of Beidi laughed freely.

"Fifth Prince!" Two figures stood at the gate — Zhuge Yu and Zhuge Shang. In the fierce sun, their Yulin silver armour blazed.

Ji Ying had only ever seen Xiaoyu at night; now in full daylight she could see her clearly — that face from the Western Regions, bright and bold and yet still touched with a girl's youth. Zhuge Shang had arching brows and a chin slightly raised; the moment he laid eyes on Ji Ying, his gaze came alive.

"Little Sister Ji Ying!" Xiaoyu cried with delight. "What are you doing here?"

Ji Ying explained about the musician notice and showed it to Xiaoyu.

Xiaoyu read it, gave a soft "Oh," smiled with one corner of her mouth, brought a finger to her lips, and winked her right eye.

"Thanks to Little Sister's identification last night, that villain Jiang Kang is now in custody. We are waiting for Wei General to return to the city and conduct a full investigation."

…I can only hope we have the right man.

By now it was time for the midday meal, and the smell of cooking drifted from the kitchen. The Prince of Beidi invited Ji Ying to join the table — great pots of white rice, plain vegetables and fruit, with fish and meat. The residence had few low tables, so everyone settled in small clusters on the floor, overlapping and mixed together. The hall filled with Shu voices; question after question came Ji Ying's way about life in Wei.

"What does Wei have that the Later Han does not?"

"…In Chengdu, people go out and leave their doors open."

"We Chengdu people are lazy."

"And what does the Later Han have that Wei does not?"

"…A certain stillness."

"There are fewer of us."

Asking me like this — as if I am some rare bird or strange beast on display.

Yet the whole audience sitting right beside me.

"Do the common people of Wei hate the Sima clan?"

Ji Ying thought of Luoyang.

"There are so many common people — some do, some do not."

"And you — do you hate the Sima clan or not?"

"…Forgive me. I would rather not speak of them."

I will not let them dirty this place.

Xiaoyu and Zhuge Shang came to explain the purpose of their visit: the Prince of Beidi had summoned them to demonstrate their martial skills and review the training. And the young men in the residence were all sons of respectable families who had volunteered to take up the staff.

"The Fifth Prince and I trained together as children; we always paired off to practice." Xiaoyu explained.

The Fifth Prince, being the fifth son of the current emperor, bore the family name Liu. His given name was Chen.

After the meal, everyone gathered in the back courtyard and took seats along the walls, leaving a clearing in the middle as the sparring ground.

Ji Ying had been invited to stay and watch.

The first match, to no one's surprise, was between Prince Liu Chen and Zhuge Yu — the weapon agreed upon: the long spear.

Both put on leather armour. The Prince selected two long-shafted weapons from the rack, each capped with a wooden blade pointing straight forward, taller than Liu Chen himself by a full head, the shaft roughly as thick as a fist.

Liu Chen tossed one spear to Xiaoyu across the clearing.

Xiaoyu caught it one-handed, shifted smoothly into a wide stance, and stood perfectly still.

She is strong.

Liu Chen attacked first — a straight thrust down the centre; Xiaoyu met it straight on.

But at the moment of contact, Xiaoyu's spear suddenly twisted and swept Liu Chen's point aside; his momentum was too far committed to recover, and Xiaoyu turned her shaft crosswise, two hands driving the long pole into Liu Chen's chest with full force — he flew backwards and crashed into the weapon rack behind him! Long poles, horizontal, vertical, horizontal-and-vertical, all clattered to the ground.

At the sight of the weapons strewn every which way, Ji Ying nearly lost her composure.

"Brilliant!" Zhuge Shang clapped from the side. The young men ranged around them were silent.

"Hmph!" The Prince of Beidi climbed to his feet, brushed off the dust, settled into his stance, and went back in.

This time, neither rushed. They began to circle — Xiaoyu moving east to south, Liu Chen west to north — like two powerful animals sizing each other up, neither willing to show an opening first.

Every step Xiaoyu took was solid and level, her centre of gravity pressed low, ready to unleash.

Her hands, originally gripping the spear at its middle, began to slide slowly toward the front.

She is going to use it like a short spear?

Without warning, Xiaoyu swung the spear like a staff, twisting at the waist to sweep hard at Liu Chen's legs!

Liu Chen did not try to block — he bent his knees and jumped, the shaft sweeping empty beneath him.

He came down carrying the heavy spear, the landing off-balance — but he used that momentum, reversing his grip in a single motion to drive the blade at Xiaoyu's flank!

If she could not get out of the way, the point would take her square in the ribs.

Xiaoyu did not move. She waited until the point was nearly upon her, then stepped to one side, thrust both hands out, and seized the shaft. She wrenched it toward her with all her strength.

Liu Chen was pulled off his feet as if sucked into a current — and hit the ground face-first with a heavy thud!

"Oh!" A gasp went around the courtyard.

How does Xiaoyu know the exact instant to reach out and catch the shaft?

Strong as she is, she also turns his own force against him — both of them driving together, sending him down.

Xiaoyu stepped forward and pulled the Prince of Beidi up from the ground.

Liu Chen's face darkened. Suddenly he gripped the spear tight, let out a great shout — "Ha!" — and hoisted the heavy wooden weapon above his head, bringing it straight down at Xiaoyu's skull.

That could kill someone!

Xiaoyu clasped both hands tight on her shaft, pivoted at the same moment, and with the force of her waist and arms drove a sweep from lower left to upper right — meeting it head-on!

Crack! The two shafts collided, and Liu Chen's spear was knocked from his hands!

It's coming this way!

Ji Ying ducked; the shaft whistled over her head and struck the brick wall behind her, shaking loose a rain of dust.

When she looked up, Xiaoyu's spear tip was resting against Liu Chen's chest, perfectly still.

Ji Ying thought of Luoyang.

That emperor of Wei, run through with a spear before his own palace gates — he had not been so lucky.

Ji Ying brushed the dust from her shoulder.

Liu Chen laughed and shook his head, rubbing his dust-covered chest. "Xiaoyu has gotten so much stronger…"

"Your Highness receives the heroes of the realm, studies the classics of the sages — while I train every single day: one shichen at dawn, one shichen before sleep. Over time, the gap is only natural." Xiaoyu smiled.

"Good!" the Prince of Beidi declared. "Sincere effort moves even metal and stone!"

"Your Highness is worth ten thousand lives — you need not be obsessed with martial training like a rough soldier. The uses of learning are far greater."

"I hope so." Liu Chen sighed. "Though whether the officials at court still find any use in reading the classics — that I cannot say." He turned to bow to the assembled young men with clasped hands, wiped his face, and crouched down to one side. "Let His Highness catch his breath — who is next?"

Zhuge Shang immediately raised his hand. "I will go. But not against Xiaoyu. I have never beaten her once."

Laughter rippled through the group.

"Xiaoyu has been undefeated for years!" Liu Chen said. He turned to Ji Ying. "Miss Ji — you and Zhuge Shang, why don't you have a round? I think you two are evenly matched."

Me?

"I only play the qin. I have no martial skill."

A flicker of displeasure crossed Liu Chen's face. "Speak honestly before His Highness. Just now you caught that spike with one hand; you ducked under a flying spear without blinking; and there is a blade-scar on your arm. You have faced someone in a real fight!"

Ji Ying thought of Luoyang again.

The instant my dagger went into the execution guard's neck.

The instant my dagger struck through the leather breastplate of one of Zhong Hui's men.

Ji Ying nodded.

Liu Chen's displeasure turned to delight. "Good! Let His Highness see how much you are worth!"

"What weapon do you favour?"

"Only a dagger, and I did not bring it."

"The residence has no wooden daggers." Liu Chen unhooked the short knife from his own sash and tossed it to Ji Ying. "Keep it in the scabbard."

"Understood."

Zhuge Shang picked a wooden sword from the rack — about four feet long.

He hesitated a moment. "I am afraid of hurting Miss Ji. I will hold back."

The Prince of Beidi gave a sharp laugh. "Do soldiers on a battlefield hold back? You go at full strength — full force! If anyone is injured, His Highness takes responsibility!"

My reach is short. If I try to block his weapon like Xiaoyu, I will lose outright.

That spear match just now — a long spear is heavy; it cannot move fast.

And Zhuge Shang's sword is long, which means it will be slow too…

When I was small, I used to scrap with the children near the bamboo grove. The bamboo grew in thick stands, so I took a short stick and worked through the trunks — keeping on the move, never letting anyone read my pattern, tapping someone's shoulder from the left and jabbing at their leg from the right.

Ji Ying moved light on her feet, weaving her pace between fast and slow, heavy and feather-light — like the wind through bamboo, here one moment and gone the next.

"Ha!"

Zhuge Shang slashed down from the upper right!

Step aside!

"Hmm!"

That slash missed — now a cut coming from the lower left!

Dodge!

The back courtyard was wide open — no bamboo to obstruct his long sword, only the ring of people around them…

There is only one way.

Ji Ying gave ground steadily, retreating until she reached the edge of the ring — and Zhuge Yu was directly behind her.

Zhuge Shang drove a cut straight down at her head!

Step aside!

The stroke was heading right for Xiaoyu's right shoulder!

Xiaoyu did not flinch — she raised one hand with an almost casual flick and pushed the wooden sword to the side. Zhuge Shang's blow deflected, his balance broken — and Ji Ying had already stepped around behind him!

Strike at the back!

"Ah!" Zhuge Shang cried out and stumbled forward into the crowd of Xiaoyu and the young men around her.

A wave of cheers went up.

Zhuge Shang's face fell. He turned and attacked again, this time gripping the long sword short and driving it straight at Ji Ying like a spear.

Good — he is putting force into it, which means it is not fast!

Chest! Angle away!

High line! Drop!

Low sweep! Jump!

Zhuge Shang thrust again and again; Ji Ying flew in every direction, here and gone. Gradually she maneuvered back until she was right in front of Liu Chen — and deliberately crouched low.

Come on — middle and low!

There it is!

Ji Ying shifted her body just enough to slip the angle of the blade; the wooden sword tip drove straight at the crouching Prince of Beidi's forehead!

Zhuge Shang jolted awake, pulled back in alarm, retracting the long sword — and in that single instant his long weapon's advantage collapsed. Ji Ying's legs uncoiled and she drove forward, and with a sharp crack the scabbard struck Zhuge Shang's leather-armoured stomach.

"Wonderful!" The crowd burst into applause. Xiaoyu was positively glowing. "So Little Sister knows martial arts after all!"

Zhuge Shang's face had gone scarlet.

The young men all shouted together: "Again, again!"

Use the same trick twice and they will see through it — then there is no winning.

Ji Ying extended her hand and gave back the short knife.

"That is enough. In truth I have no real martial skill. In the open like this, with a short weapon, I relied entirely on everyone in this ring to give me my openings. If I kept going, I have no other ideas."

"You knew when to stop, and you only fought when you saw your chance." Liu Chen tilted his head. "Not just tactics — that is military thinking."

"I have not studied it either."

The Prince of Beidi gave a small, thoughtful nod.

He then set the young men to spar against Zhuge Yu and Zhuge Shang. Liu Chen and Zhuge Shang each held their own among the trainees, trading wins and losses freely — but the moment anyone faced Zhuge Yu, her strikes came fast and landed clean, her strength backed every blow, and no one lasted five exchanges.

After the sparring, everyone gathered in a circle to drink water and dry off.

Liu Chen looked satisfied. "Good. My men have improved again. The day will come when there is use for all of this."

Zhuge Shang said: "Today at the Imperial Academy square, several dozen students showed up chanting 'End the northern campaigns — recall Jiang Wei.' The students calling for the Grand General's removal outnumber ours now, and they are louder. I would like to borrow the Prince's men to join us and add to our numbers."

"Those people are infuriating, to be sure," Liu Chen said, shaking his head. "But my stave-fighters are stave-fighters, not debaters. If it ever comes to blows, His Highness and his men will be there to thrash them one by one." He lowered his voice. "This has happened too suddenly. It is not simple — someone is behind it."

Both Zhuge Yu and Zhuge Shang said Ji Ying knew the inside story. The Prince of Beidi asked, and Ji Ying, seeing no way around it, gave a brief account of the previous night's gathering at the teahouse: court officials, sons of noble clans, Academy students — the ringleader being Yan Hong, son of Right Grand General Yan Yu. She added that they were pushing to install Yan Yu in Jiang Wei's place.

"Hmph!" Liu Chen struck the ground with his staff. "They shout about the affairs of state, but their hearts are full of petty calculations! Some fear dying, some fear losing money, some fear outsiders, some fear Heaven's mandate! All these muddled people gathered in one place, each giving the others courage!"

Ji Ying glanced at Xiaoyu. Her face had gone quiet.

In the eyes of those men who spoke of nothing but "Ba-Shu" and "Yi Province," Xiaoyu is an outsider. And so am I.

A young woman with a face from the Western Regions, grown up in Chengdu — grown up inside Chaozhen Guan, just outside the city walls.

I think of the Liezi fragments: the sages valued what was within, not what appeared on the surface.

What inner understanding, then, might I share with Xiaoyu and these others — people who have given their hearts to restoring the Han house?

Liu Chen went on: "Those rumours — the northern campaigns are doomed, the court is incompetent, the stars have aligned as an ill omen, even a dying tree is an ill omen. These stiff-backed pedants who know nothing but their classics, speaking nonsense about Heaven's mandate and the Heavenly Way. And invoking the names of the late emperor and the reigning emperor to proclaim the empire's end! There has been no such outrage since ancient times!"

The crowd murmured along with mounting indignation.

In Luoyang, that alone would carry several criminal charges.

The people of Shu can speak plainly without fear — so why do they choose to say only the most defeatist, most damaging things?

"Fear for one's wealth, fear for one's life — the muddled and the craven flood the world. No matter!"

The Prince of Beidi rose to his feet, raising his staff high:

"The Later Han was in rot far worse than what we face today — yet our founding ancestor, undaunted through a hundred reversals, built the Later Han from nothing!"

"Children of the blazing Han — are we the kind who cling to life and shrink from death?"

"No!" Zhuge Shang raised his sword and was the first to leap up; Xiaoyu and the young men followed, cheering together in a single voice.

Ji Ying sat on the ground and felt the urge to shout along with them.

Then a servant came in to report: "Wei General urgently requests Zhuge Yu and Zhuge Shang — you are to go to the Imperial Secretariat at once!"

Xiaoyu and Zhuge Shang exchanged an uneasy look.

Xiaoyu said to Zhuge Shang: "Father is dealing with the Jiang Kang matter — he will need more evidence." Then she turned to Ji Ying with an apologetic expression:

"Would Little Sister come with us and serve as a witness? The moment this is over, Elder Sister will take you to Chaozhen Guan."

Me — go and testify before a high official of the Later Han?

1 Se, zhu, zheng, pipa (瑟, 筑, 箏, 琵琶): Four traditional Chinese string instruments. The se is a large zither with multiple strings; the zhu is a struck zither played with a bamboo stick; the zheng is a plucked zither that is the ancestor of the Japanese koto; the pipa is a four-stringed lute.

2 Prince of Beidi (北地王): Liu Chen (劉諶), the fifth son of Liu Shan, the reigning emperor of the Later Han.

3 The Odes (詩經, Shijing): China's oldest anthology of poetry, compiled around the sixth century BCE and traditionally attributed to Confucius.

4 Luming (鹿鳴): A qin piece, meaning dear call.
