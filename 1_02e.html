Yanxing: The Rekindling of Han · Book One: The Land of Heavenly Bounty

Book One: (II)

← Book One (I) Contents → Book One (III)

Ji Ying's hand tightened around the door latch when she saw that the soldier had found her.

I have been in Chengdu half a year, doing nothing but hammering grave offerings in the back of a smithy. I have never once mentioned Father.

She is almost certainly here at her cousin Zhuge Shang's request, wanting me to help identify Wei spies again.

Ji Ying looked at her once more. Her smile was warm and open.

So beautiful, and she brought gifts. She does not seem to mean me harm.

Chengdu households mostly keep their doors half-open; people here are not much on guard.

The Liezi I was just reading: a worthy man seeks those of like mind, not like appearance.

If I, a Wei woman, grow suspicious and shut out Shu people and Western Regions folk alike, that will not do.

Ji Ying lifted the latch and pushed the door open.

She pulled off her muddy shoes and lit the oil lamp. The flame stirred; light and shadow swayed.

Zhuge Shang's cousin also removed her leather boots, unstrapped her silver armour and iron helm, set them to one side, and swept her long hair back over her shoulders.

In the lamplight, her hair caught a faint sheen. Ji Ying felt her gaze drift.

Ji Ying pushed aside the bedding she had never bothered to fold, and the two women sat side by side on the low couch.

"Pleased to meet you. I grew up here in Chengdu — my name is Zhuge Yu,1 the yu from the Odes:2 I think of my lord, gentle as jade. People call me Xiaoyu." Xiaoyu smiled. "How old are you?"

"Ji Ying. Sixteen. Just call me Ji Ying."

"Good — I am three years your senior." Xiaoyu's tone was bright. "Then I shall call you Little Sister Ji Ying!"

Xiaoyu untied the pouch at her side and took out three silk brocade sachets, each about the size of a fist, and set them before Ji Ying.

"First time in the Later Han — you must have a welcome gift."

If I accept these gifts, I will owe her something.

I will have no choice but to help her catch spies.

Xiaoyu smiled. "No need to overthink it. Elder sister gives you these with no strings attached. Open them and see."

Ji Ying hesitated a moment, then picked up the first sachet.

Inside were several flatbreads layered with dried meat strips, still faintly warm, smelling of oil.

"There is a shop south of the city run by people from Chang'an. You should find them to your taste."

The bread was thick and substantial, the edges lightly charred, the smell heavy and dense.

Ji Ying opened her mouth.

The meat was tightly grained, hard to chew, its saltiness opening slowly.

She could not chew it through, and simply swallowed.

"Is it good?"

Ji Ying nodded her thanks, set the bread down, and opened the second sachet.

Inside was a small wooden box with carved designs on the lid.

She opened it: white cream lay within, smooth as a lotus just opening on the water.

"Lotus cream. For the skin."

Ji Ying leaned close and sniffed — a faint, gentle sweetness.

Xiaoyu raised her wrist before Ji Ying: "I use this too. In winter your hands will not crack and bleed."

…Yes. That is her scent.

"It smells lovely."

Ji Ying opened the last sachet and poured out a row of small round pieces of jade.

Qin bridge-markers — the jade was warm and smooth, tinged with a faint fuchsia-pink, glowing softly in the lamplight.

"I thought, well, you are a girl — I searched several stalls before I found this colour!"

Ji Ying touched one lightly with her fingertip. The jade was fine and cool, with a faint warmth beneath.

Her fingertip rested on the jade and did not move.

Father's qin had jade markers — deep green.

Ji Ying tipped the bridge-markers back into the sachet and slowly drew the cord shut.

"…How thoughtful. Thank you for your great kindness, Elder Sister Xiaoyu. I will find a way to repay you."

That was wrong — I misspoke.

"Think nothing of it." Xiaoyu smiled. "I came tonight only to talk with Little Sister Ji Ying — girl talk, just the two of us."

Xiaoyu turned and pointed at the armour by the wall. "Do you know what your elder sister does, wearing that silver armour and iron helm?"

"A soldier?"

"Exactly." Xiaoyu sat up straight. "We are the Yulin Guards — we protect the Son of Heaven inside and outside the palace walls."

Ji Ying looked Xiaoyu over: solid frame, upright bearing, manner frank and easy.

The Yulin Guards protect the palace and the emperor — they would be the trusted kinsmen of the royal house, loyal and worthy, likely skilled fighters too.

…But when the Wei emperor was murdered before the Cao family's palace gates, where were the Yulin Guards?

"Yesterday, something happened inside the palace — word is all over Chengdu." Xiaoyu glanced at the qin on Ji Ying's table. "You may have already heard."

So it is this matter again.

"I heard something. Twelve characters."

Xiaoyu gave a knowing nod. "But that is only the surface." She said, "Like the snow-capped mountains to the west of Chengdu — a little snow on the peak, but the rock beneath is far greater."

"What do you mean?"

"You only just arrived in Chengdu; you may not have noticed." Xiaoyu looked out through the doorway. "I grew up here — I can see the difference. These past few months, in the streets and markets, people are saying the court is incompetent, that Grand General Jiang Wei cares only for his own glory, that the kingdom is finished. Such talk spreads wider and wider, while words of loyalty and principle stand alone against it." Xiaoyu sighed. "Right and wrong have been turned upside down."

Ji Ying recalled the teahouse that afternoon — yes, she had heard people talking about the court in that way.

Xiaoyu continued: "Little Sister, do you know what the place where you play your qin used to be called?"

"Yanxing Pavilion."

"And do you know what yanxing means?"

Ji Ying shook her head.

"The Eastern Han was the fire virtue; so the Later Han also carries the fire virtue." Xiaoyu said, "Yan: the blaze at its height. Yanxing — the revival of the Han house. Since I was old enough to remember, this teahouse was called Yanxing Pavilion…"

"Recently, it changed ownership." A shadow crossed Xiaoyu's face.

A century's aspiration to restore the great dynasty, traded for a Chengdu pond of lotus.

Centuries of the fire virtue, rubbed onto one's hands as skin cream.

Once, my mother's Cao clan who founded the Wei kingdom had been the sworn enemy of every Shu loyalist. These lonely ministers had pledged to overthrow the Cao Wei and restore the Han house.

Now the Han house had been dying for decades, and Cao Wei would not last much longer either.

Both themselves and their enemies — both would be gone.

Xiaoyu leaned closer, her voice dropping: "There is one more thing I must tell you — it must not leave this room. Can Little Sister Ji Ying keep a secret?"

"I am just someone who plays qin."

Xiaoyu nodded. "I know I can trust you. At the start of the month, a border dispatch intercepted a secret letter hidden among a merchant convoy. The letter contained troop movements that had not yet been issued as orders. Whoever knew that kind of information would have to be close to the Chengdu court. Do you understand what I am saying?"

So there really are spies in Chengdu.

But how would I recognize one?

Ji Ying said: "I understand. But in all honesty, the only Wei people I know how to spot are the noble clans — that air of scrambling for name and position. Matters of military espionage are entirely beyond me."

Xiaoyu nodded. "That is exactly why an outsider can sense what is out of place. Little Sister Ji Ying, I only need you for one day. Just one day."

"And if I name someone who turns out to be innocent?"

"We do not harm good people — that above all. We only take the wicked." Xiaoyu nodded.

Ji Ying picked up the Liezi fragments she had bought that day. "Even a single day is too much. To be honest, I only enjoy reading pure-talk texts like the Liezi. I have neither the ability nor the interest for catching spies."

Xiaoyu's face fell a little. She took up the Liezi and turned it over in her hands, then abruptly held the bamboo strips close to her face — not reading, but running her fingers carefully over the red hemp cord binding the strips, then lifting them to her nose and sniffing.

"This red cord looks familiar. Where did you get this book?"

"A bookstall on the way to the Imperial Academy."

"Did you ask the vendor where it came from?"

Ji Ying shook her head. "Maybe Chaozhen Guan?"

Xiaoyu's face lit up: "Yes! That is where I have seen it — inside Chaozhen Guan."

"You can get into Chaozhen Guan?"

"Ha." Xiaoyu laughed. "In all of Chengdu, perhaps no one knows Chaozhen Guan better than I, Zhuge Yu! I grew up inside its walls. Every ten days or half a month I still go back to visit my mother."

She grew up inside?

"Does it have many books like the Liezi?"

Xiaoyu looked up, thinking it over. "As I recall, there are many large shelves." She spread her arms wide. "A hundred scrolls at least, I would say. This book matches what I remember — even the smell is the same."

"With so many books housed there, are there recluses living inside?"

Xiaoyu laughed. "Of course — my mother is a recluse herself!"

"Your mother is a Daoist nun?"

"Not quite, it is a long story. Some other day I will tell my mother about you, and invite you to come and visit. If Little Sister does not mind, you could even stay for a few days! Any text in the classical canon — read as you please, copy it and take it home!" Xiaoyu took Ji Ying's hand in hers.

I spent nearly half a day and came away with only these fragments of the Liezi.

And Xiaoyu's mother is actually a recluse?

I have found one — just like that?

For the sake of books, a recluse — but what if I end up harming an innocent man…

"Elder Sister's generosity is hard to refuse…" Ji Ying lowered her head. "But if I wrongly accuse a good person, I will have ruined his life and destroyed his family. Better to let it go."

Xiaoyu clasped Ji Ying's hands tightly: "Years ago, the Later Han's Grand General Fei Yi3 received Wei defectors at a banquet with sincere goodwill — and one of them ran him through with a sword. How can we do nothing?"

Zhuge Yu's eyes shone with feeling; her voice trembled slightly.

If there truly is a spy… he should be caught.

Xiaoyu continued: "Even if we do arrest someone, we do not convict or execute on the spot — that requires evidence. Everything goes to my uncle. He is the most upright, fair-minded, and principled man I have ever known. He knows the law and keeps to it. He will not cut corners for personal reasons. He will not wrong anyone."

"Your uncle?"

"Wei General Zhuge Siyuan.4"

I think I heard that title today.

Growing up, Father and his friends, speaking of Zhuge Liang — even though he was the chancellor of an enemy kingdom who had led armies against us — almost always spoke of him with respect and admiration.

That family should be trustworthy.

"Just one day?"

Xiaoyu's eyes brightened; she tightened her grip on Ji Ying's hands: "Just one day! Your elder sister knows you are a Wei woman and would not wish to harm Wei people. But for decades, Wei spies have killed our generals, undermined our state, spread lies and turned truth on its head…"

"You need not say more. I agree." Ji Ying drew a slow breath. "I only ask that Elder Sister Xiaoyu choose a day to take me to Chaozhen Guan, to pay my respects to your mother, and to see the library."

Xiaoyu beamed and threw her arms around Ji Ying, holding on.

Strong arms.

That lotus cream smells good.

"When do we catch the spy?"

Xiaoyu's eyes lit up: "Tomorrow!"

"So soon?"

Xiaoyu leaned forward slightly, voice tightening: "The opportunity is rare. Tomorrow, a group of highly suspicious persons will be meeting at Yanxing… the teahouse where you play. Just play your qin as usual. If you believe someone is a spy, note his name, find a pretext to leave, and go to the gate of the Imperial Academy — there will be someone there to meet you. What happens after that, Little Sister need not concern yourself with. Leave it to my Yulin Guards." Xiaoyu patted her chest.

"What if no one seems suspicious?"

"Then do not come out."

"If I do not come out, will you be disappointed?"

Xiaoyu smiled: "Spies want to ruin the court's reputation. If we want to protect it, we cannot go arresting people at random."

Chengdu is nothing like Luoyang.

Here, a man who writes graffiti on a palace pillar playing on the emperor's name goes unpunished.

The Later Han's Yulin Guards should not resort to indiscriminate arrests.

"What if the suspicious persons do not come?"

Xiaoyu placed one finger to her crimson lips and winked her right eye.


The day was grey and damp. The sky hung low and close, and the Jin River ran the colour of dark iron.

Ji Ying carried her qin. Passersby dragged their feet as sluggishly as ever, and she, too, slowed her pace.

The damp seeped up through the gaps in the flagstones; the soles of her shoes were cold.

Out on the Imperial Academy square, Zhuge Shang and his friends were still calling out "Restore the Han house." A few more young people had joined — there were women among them now, hair unbound, not bothering with men's clothes.

Zhuge Shang saw me and waved from across the square with his companions.

Just this one day.

Father's qin: pale cedar on top, dark nanmu beneath, dark green jade bridge-markers.

The music rose again — urgent, then faltering; surging, then breaking off — like a northern gale that comes and almost subsides but will not rest.

I lay awake last night tossing and turning, trying to work out how to identify a Wei spy.

If I were a spy, what would I do?

Identify whoever matters most to the enemy, and assassinate him?

Find whoever has been wronged, who has been pushed to the edge — and buy him?

If I were a spy, how would I react?

If someone kept staring at me, would I fear being found out?

If someone spoke to me, would I guard every word?

Ji Ying spent the whole day on edge.

Every word from every table might be a clue that the man was a spy.

The loud talker — was he spreading rumours?

The silent man — was he carrying some secret burden?

Everyone looked like a spy, and no one did.

By the time she noticed, the light had faded. Customers were drifting away; servants cleared the cluttered cups and plates.

Ji Ying was about to pack up for the day. She thought: I have seen no one particularly suspicious — I have kept my promise, wronged no good person. A faint relief settled in. Then she thought of Chaozhen Guan, of Zhuge Yu needing to take her there — she had let her down after all.

Then all at once a crowd of voices, and more than ten men entered the teahouse. Ji Ying saw them dressed variously — mostly in the robes of Confucian scholars, ranging in age from Imperial Academy students to men past thirty. They settled in twos and threes and fell into casual conversation.

After a short while, two more entered. One was tall and powerfully built, the lower half of his face covered in dense, neatly trimmed short stubble. He wore thick leather boots, and his step fell heavy. His outer robe was coarse cloth, but the buckle on his leather belt was fine and detailed. The other was half a head shorter but full-faced and ruddy, smiling with easy composure. He wore a pale white silk gown and carried a folding fan with an ivory handle — the look of a young man of good family. The moment these two arrived, the teahouse owner hurried forward to greet them, then had servants lower the bamboo blinds on all four sides and asked the remaining customers to settle their bills and leave.

Ji Ying set the qin straight, drew a long breath, and closed her eyes.

She had barely touched the first string when the owner appeared before her.

"Miss Ji, it is getting late. Time to head home."

"With so many guests tonight, music is hard to come by — let me play a few more pieces."

The owner hesitated a moment, then said: "You concentrate on playing. Do not listen to what the guests are discussing." He turned away to attend to the young lord.

Ji Ying played through pieces like Tang Yao — monotonous, staid melodies — and deliberately slowed the tempo, so as not to draw attention.

She closed her eyes. She knew the fingering by heart.

She fixed her gaze on no one, so as not to rattle the grass and startle the snakes.

Before long, servants brought out various teas and refreshments. The young lord rose and, one by one, introduced each of the guests — family background, current post, and past deeds. Ji Ying could not keep all the names straight; she gathered: about half were court officials, none of any great rank. The other half were Imperial Academy students, but every one of them was the son of some prominent clan or high official. As for the young lord himself, he too was a general's son — his name was Yan Hong, son of the Right Grand General.

Right Grand General, Left Grand General, Whatever Miscellaneous Grand General. The rites and social order manufacture these titles, and anyone in the room gets a share.

Tedious.

The man beside the young lord Yan Hong — the tall, formidable one with the thick beard — was the son of a border commander, and his name was Jiang Kang.

"First," Yan Hong rose to speak, "we must establish that we, the people of Ba-Shu,5 are united in calling for Jiang Wei's recall and an end to the northern campaigns."

Heads nodded around the room.

Yan Hong proclaimed: "Let each of you speak your best reason, so all may hear. I will begin by calling on Secretary Chen."

This Secretary Chen was a minor official whose work was to catalogue the classics — a slender, scholarly-looking man named Chen Shou6.

Chen Shou unrolled the bamboo slips in his hands; he had prepared in advance.

He read aloud, word by deliberate word: "For decades now, the realm has been divided three ways. The people's allegiances have settled; we are weak and the enemy is strong. To move against the tide is to invite defeat."

His voice was not loud, yet every word came through clearly.

"The name of the Han house carries great weight — but the lives of common people carry greater. Reputation can be debated later; the dead cannot be brought back."

"Year after year of campaigns, unable to win, while the people are exhausted at home, our national strength drains away, and the spirit of the people scatters. Heaven's mandate has been lost."

While the room broke into approving exclamations, Chen Shou finished and sat down. At his table were two others: one dressed in plain white cloth, looking somewhat worn and haggard; the other in a blue-green gown, much younger, glancing around the room.

Oh — he is looking at me.

Ji Ying quickly lowered her head and went on playing.

More scholars and Academy students rose in turn. Their arguments ran along similar lines to Chen Shou's: we cannot win, so why fight; fighting means death, and no one wants to die; the state is weak, the enemy is strong; the Later Han is finished.

When it came to the young man in blue-green at Chen Shou's table — a junior Academy official — he first bowed to the room and said:

"Last year, Jiang Wei ordered our main forces to move to establish military farming colonies7 at Tazhong, submitting a memorial outlining a long-term plan. With our best troops in the Grand General's hands, recalling Jiang Wei could mean a new commander who does not hold the troops' loyalty — and it gives Wei a chance to press a major offensive. Perhaps the safer course is to hold the present situation, and simply cease campaigning."

His argument was different from the others'.

When the scholars had all spoken their piece, the bearded man Jiang Kang rose, his voice like a bell:

"Good! It seems we are all agreed on calling Jiang Wei back to Chengdu — the only question is how urgently. That Jiang Wei is not one of us Ba-Shu men to begin with — he won no favour in Wei and turned traitor, then came to our country and seized a generalship, sending Ba-Shu sons to their deaths for the sake of his own glory. We have endured him for ten years. No more!"

So Grand General Jiang Wei had come from Wei to Shu, just as I had.

Why had he defected? Was "defection" his own choice?

Why did he keep attacking Wei — his homeland? Was it truly for the sake of titles and glory?

Then Jiang Kang went on: "And who is the right man to replace Jiang Wei? I say no one but Right Grand General Yan Yu. The Yan family has put down roots in Yi Province8 for four generations — their ancestral tombs are in Ba-Shu, their land is in Ba-Shu! He fights for his homeland, not some hazy dream of the Central Plains! A man who can protect what is his can protect what belongs to others. Right Grand General Yan has kept watch at the eastern border of Ba for years — he has never recklessly moved troops, never gambled the lives of common people on a battle he could not win. That is love for the people! Love for Yi Province!"

When Jiang Kang finished, a group of Academy students behind him broke into applause.

Jiang Kang then called on the students behind him to speak in turn; some listed Jiang Wei's faults, others sang Right Grand General Yan's praises — all much the same as Jiang Kang.

It occurred to Ji Ying: Right Grand General Yan — is he not the very father of this young lord Yan Hong?

…Not a coincidence.

Ji Ying's qin played on, but the melody had shifted to Xiqin9 — the kingdom that would eventually swallow all six rivals.

The music was taut and striding, like a western wind rolling sand and gravel, iron cavalry crashing through a line.

The young lord Yan Hong rose, bowed in all four directions, and said: "I am grateful beyond words for everyone's support, and will not forget this as long as I live. Now — in recent days, over at the Imperial Academy square, Jiang Wei's partisans have been crying out for the northern campaigns, trying to sway the court and the public. What are everyone's thoughts?"

Jiang Kang called out: "They have people — so do we. Are there more of their outsiders, or more of us locals? Let me summon a hundred men, and overwhelm them on sheer numbers alone. When we do, the court and the public will naturally come to our side."

Heads around the room nodded.

Yan Hong agreed: "I can organise students from the Academy. But with only Academy students, that may be a thin showing. Would any court officials care to join?" He looked toward Chen Shou's table.

Chen Shou rose: "I am of low standing and could not persuade my colleagues. But as long as I am not rostered for duty, I would be willing to come."

The middle-aged man in plain cloth said: "I am sorry — I must care for my elderly grandmother. If you are short of people, I could come for a few hours."

Yan Hong looked toward the young man in the blue-green gown.

"There is one thing that worries me," the young man replied with a bow. "If we send a hundred people, they send two hundred; we send three hundred, they send more in turn — it escalates. At first, the two sides are on opposite ends of the square with space between them, competing only in volume and numbers. But the Imperial Academy square is only so large; eventually the two sides will be pressed together, arguments will break out face to face, perhaps even fighting — and that would not be good."

The big-bearded Jiang Kang said: "Zimao, you worry too much. If we fill the whole square first, where will they stand?"

Laughter rippled through the room.

Jiang Kang got into the spirit of it, and with one hand lifted a low table off the ground, raised it above his head, and took a few thundering steps forward: "Anyone who dares try — they will have to get through me first. I'll put every last one of those silk-fisted posturers flat on the ground!"

More laughter and cheers.

Ji Ying rolled her eyes.

Xiqin finished. She moved into Dongwu10.

The melody was level and regular, like columns of Academy students in iron armour, filing one by one into the square.

In the middle section, the piece opened out, the sound swelling steadily, each wave stronger than the last.

Yan Hong spoke again: "Recently, something happened inside the palace — all of Chengdu knows about it by now, I am sure everyone here is aware. I would like to ask for all your thoughts on that as well."

Ji Ying looked up sharply.

Chen Shou rose: "When a great tree falls without reason, that is Heaven's warning. What Heaven's mandate intends has long been clear to every person in this land — only the courageous dare risk censure and write it plainly, using the emperor's own name."

A murmur of lamentation ran through the scholars.

Names are given by people. What has this to do with Heaven's mandate? Heaven and earth are without pity — what bearing do they have on the life or death of a tree, the rise or fall of an empire?

These pedants read their books to death and then warp everything into this doctrine of Heaven responding to human affairs.

Ji Ying glared at Chen Shou, and felt the cold rising through her fingertips. The climax of Dongwu — rank after rank of soldiers assembled and advancing, in perfect order, driving into the enemy!

Jiang Kang rose: "If you ask me, those characters on the pillar were put there by Jiang Wei's men themselves. This way, he would have grounds to arrest anyone who dares speak truthfully, using the pretext of insulting the emperor — and control the court at his leisure. We are meat on the chopping block! If we do not act now, we will end up in someone else's hands!"

The room broke into murmuring; several heads nodded, voices chimed in agreement.

Then the young man in the blue-green gown rose: "In my view, it does not look like the work of those in favour of the campaigns either. However angry they are, they would not invoke the late emperor's name in this way. Perhaps the person who wrote on the pillar was simply drunk that night, and what was deepest in his heart came out twisted. We should not read too much into it."

Chen Shou rose again: "Whether the pro-war faction did it or not, every person must recognise the will of Heaven and follow the Heavenly Way. The later Han fell to corruption; Heaven's mandate passed to the Wei. Wei also fell to corruption; Heaven's mandate passed again to the Sima clan."

Pedantic nonsense about Heaven's mandate!

"Without recognising the will of Heaven, you toil in vain. Going against Heaven's mandate, you come to a bad end. Can we afford not to be warned?"

Drivel, start to finish!

Ji Ying's breathing came short. She had meant to ease off the pressure in her fingers, but without meaning to she bore down on the strings — she had stopped caring about wrong notes. At the climax of Dongwu, as the two armies met and clashed, note after note struck like a short sword into the throat, a long spear through armour!

Yan Hong said: "The Later Han's mandate is spent — we the Ba-Shu people must make sure of our own survival!"

String-notes burst and broke — slash and cut, bone-deep; the hardest strokes split sinew and shattered bone, blood flying!

Jiang Kang bellowed: "Heaven's mandate belongs to the Sima house — rule through filial piety! Jiang Wei has no read on Heaven's mandate at all — we cannot let him drag us down with him!"

Then a great sharp snap — a string broke!

The music stopped. Every head turned toward the qin player.

Yan Hong said: "It seems our musician has something to say."

Ji Ying did not lower her head.

Jiang Kang spoke out: "You have been listening long enough. Do you support recalling Jiang Wei?"

"Jiang Wei, I do not know. Heaven's mandate, I do not know either."

Jiang Kang gave a sharp laugh. "A girl who does not know the affairs of the realm."

"Heaven's mandate," Ji Ying pressed both hands flat on the face of the qin, each word clear, "is something you and I invented in our own minds. The version you are offering means: whoever wins, their cause was ordained; whoever loses, they violated the mandate. No matter what happens, the reasoning holds. If people believe in that, everyone will grovel before power and oppress the weak."

The room broke into talk.

Jiang Kang said: "Whose side are you on?"

"I am on no one's side. Since you asked me to speak, I am giving you my honest opinion. You are free to disagree with me. I am free to disagree with you."

Yan Hong frowned. "Who is this girl? What clan is she from?"

"I am not from Chengdu. I grew up in Henei. In Wei."

Yan Hong asked: "You are a Wei woman. What reason do you have to listen to our Ba-Shu deliberations? What does Ba-Shu have to do with you?"

"I am just someone who plays qin, and happened to be here. You do not speak for all the people of Ba-Shu either."

Whispers ran through the room.

Jiang Kang: "You are also from Wei? You were sent here by Jiang Wei! Jiang Wei could not get ahead in Wei, so he defected to our Shu and won a generalship — to make a name for himself!"

"Before yesterday, I had never even heard the name Jiang Wei. I am a woman. What title am I seeking?"

Jiang Kang suddenly broke into a loud laugh. "I know! It is your father's title you are after."

Laughter spread through the room.

Half a year in this city, and I had never once spoken of Father.

Contemptible people.

Ji Ying shoved the low table made from the Yanxing plaque aside, sprang to her feet, and planted both fists on her hips: "I am the daughter of Ji Kang of the Seven Sages of the Bamboo Grove! My father lived in seclusion and wanted nothing to do with official life — what title was he seeking? He lectured Academy students on Laozi, and the Sima clan fabricated charges and had him killed! Three thousand Academy students petitioned for my father's life — you tell me what kind of Heaven's mandate Sima Zhao had! I fled here to the Later Han for my life. I smith and play music for a living. What title is my family after?"

A sudden silence in the teahouse.

Then somewhere in the room, a Confucian scholar spoke up: "Pure talk leads the state to ruin — it was never of any use in governing or bringing peace to the realm."

Another Academy student sneered: "The Seven Sages — they learned nothing of use, and thought themselves above everyone else."

You understand nothing.

"Now I understand!" Jiang Kang's laughter boomed out. "You are exactly like Xiahou Ba11 back in the day — when the Cao family and the Xiahou clan fell from favour and feared they would be killed, they fled to Shu. Your father was murdered by the Sima clan, and of course you despise them. A father's murder is a hatred that cannot share the same sky. You simply cannot bear to hear us say Heaven's mandate favours Jin! Still trying to argue back? I say your father Ji Kang was inciting the students — that is why he was killed!"

How dare you say my father deserved it!

Ji Ying roared: "You know nothing — you spew slander! You use your own filthy mind to smear an honest man!"

Jiang Kang erupted in fury. He seized a table with both hands, hoisted it overhead, and came thundering forward — the floorboards rumbling under his boots — and brought it crashing down like a mountain giving way!

Ji Ying grabbed her qin and threw herself sideways; the table struck the Yanxing plaque with a tremendous crack, splintering into flying shards of wood!

The scholars in the room gasped and cried out.

Jiang Kang was left with half a broken table. He kicked over the plaque and tea bricks, and was coming after Ji Ying again. Ji Ying backed away, clutching her qin, when the young man in the blue-green gown stepped forward and placed himself between Ji Ying and Jiang Kang:

"It is late, Miss — time to finish up. Time to finish up!"

His face wore a helpless, apologetic smile.

Hypocrite!

Ji Ying strode quickly to the door, only to find the teahouse owner there, his expression sour. "You play somewhere else tomorrow."

Fine — I will not come back!

"Quite right — the Miss will surely find another place to play." The young man in blue-green was still following behind her. "Wake up tomorrow, and it will be a new day."

Leave me alone!

Ji Ying pushed out the door into the night. The night air hit her face, carrying the wet, fishy smell of a pond.

The young man in blue-green was still murmuring at her back: "State affairs are tiring to talk about. Pure talk is perfectly fine."

Shut your mouth!

In a blaze of anger, Ji Ying broke into a run toward the Imperial Academy square. A few lanterns swayed in the wind, their red light flashing and going dark; the flagstones below lit and dimmed. Around the gate-tower, sure enough, Zhuge Yu, Zhuge Shang, and seven or eight others were waiting in full armour, long blades at their waists, pressed against the wall in the shadows. The moment they saw Ji Ying, every head turned.

Zhuge Yu's eyes blazed: "Did you spot a spy?"

Ji Ying was still gasping for breath: "That villain Jiang Kang — arrest him!"

"Jiang Kang! Brothers — restore the Han house!" Zhuge Shang shouted.

Several Yulin Guards received the order, nodded in unison, and cried out together, long blades clearing their scabbards and raised high overhead!

Amid the ringing of iron armour, the soldiers ran toward the teahouse.

Ji Ying stood there catching her breath, watching from a distance as a group of Academy students scattered in every direction.

Pedants.

A gust of wind — across the street, a lantern rocked violently several times, the flame extinguished, and the flagstones below went dark.

The moon hid behind clouds. Before her, the Imperial Academy square was quiet; behind her, insects chirped without stop.

From somewhere distant came the sound of shouting, then slowly it faded.

That annoying young man in blue-green said: tomorrow, it will be a new day.

So I have to go back to hammering grave offerings for dead people?

If I were a spy, and someone kept staring at me, I would fear being found out.

If Jiang Kang were a spy, would he make himself so conspicuous?

He was loathsome.

But was he truly a spy?
