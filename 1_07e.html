Yanxing: The Rekindling of Han · Book One: The Land of Heavenly Bounty

Book One: (VII)

← Book One (VI) Contents → Book One (VIII)

Ji Ying looked at the fly-whisk — its glossy ebony handle, its loosely gathered white hairs swaying in the warm, damp air.

This Chen Can before her wore a white guan,1 his skin smooth and fair, his brow wide and flat, his eyebrows thick and even, his eyes bright and keenly alive. He looked to be somewhere in his early thirties.

Ji Ying met Chen Can's gaze, and felt something pressing in her chest.

"This young woman from Wei says that Heaven's course is dark and unknowable. Fine — I won't speak of Heaven. I will speak only of facts." Chen Can bent forward, one arm extended in a gesture of invitation: "Since you are from Wei, you should know the answers. Between the Later Han and Wei — which side holds more territory? Which side has the larger population? Which side has the stronger army? Tell me: in which of these does the Later Han have the advantage? Name just one."

To answer honestly would be to let him use the facts as a stand-in for Heaven's will.

Chen Can held her gaze: "If there is one, say so; if there is not, say so. It isn't a difficult question."

What kind of question is that to ask a person.

Chen Can strolled unhurriedly toward Xiaoyu and Zhuge Shang, his voice ringing out: "My earlier question still hasn't been answered. Jiang Wei holds the title of Grand General — he owes the nation his full effort. Over twenty years, how much land has he recovered? How many cities has he taken back? How many households has he added to the nation's count?"

I only learned who Jiang Wei was at the start of the month.

Given the way Chen Can was asking, the answers were already plain enough.

Chen Can's expression remained neutral; he seemed to be waiting with patience, and absently touched the deep-green seal ribbon2 at his waist, then glanced out at the Imperial Academy students below.

The students put their heads together in murmurs. A scattering of jeers began to rise.

"How can you say Grand General Jiang has accomplished nothing?" Xiaoyu stepped forward, her eyes steady: "In the eighteenth year of Yanxi,3 at the Battle of Taoxi,4 the Grand General smashed the Wei army and killed forty thousand enemy soldiers!"

A burst of cheering from a far corner of the square.

Strangely, Chen Can listened to Xiaoyu's rebuttal while nodding and smiling.

"This young lady of the Yulin Guards, from the Zhuge family —" Chen Can gave his fly-whisk a languid turn at the wrist, and its white threads fanned out in a wide arc, drifting lightly across his sleeve: "— she seems not to have answered my question."

Chen Can bent forward again, arm extended: "But since she wishes to speak of Taoxi — very well, I will ask her about Taoxi. At the Battle of Taoxi, how much land did the Grand General recover? How many cities did he take back? How many households did he add to the nation's count?"

Xiaoyu lowered her head.

Back when I was a child in the bamboo grove at Yuntai Mountain, I seem to recall hearing Father's friends speak of Shu raiders coming south, killing and wounding many soldiers.

So many Wei soldiers dead — and yet they still gained no land, no cities?

"You cannot answer, because there is nothing to answer." Chen Can shook his head.

He turned to face the students below, and enunciated each word clearly: "Even in his greatest victory, Jiang Wei recovered no territory! Took back no cities! Added not one household to the nation's count! And yet across eleven northern campaigns, he has already buried tens of thousands of sons of Yizhou, left their bodies to rot on the open wastes of Yong Province and Liang Province, north of Qishan!5"

At these words, a voice erupted from somewhere nearby in the crowd: "Wei man Jiang Wei — he has no regard for the sons of Ba-Shu!"

"The lives of the people of Yizhou cannot be left in the hands of an outsider!" The men brought by Jiang Kang and Yan Hong began to stir and shout.

Xiaoyu scowled and called out over the jeering crowd: "It is precisely this record in battle that has deterred the Wei army — for twenty years they have not dared march south, and the people within our borders have been kept safe!"

Chen Can heard this, and for just a moment looked startled — then broke into a short laugh. He turned back to the crowd and made a deliberate bow: "Did you all hear that? This lady's argument is that, though Jiang Wei has won no great glory in twenty years, the mere fact that Shu has not yet been reduced to cinders and ashes, and its people not yet driven from their homes, means we should be grateful and call for him to remain Grand General for life!"

Laughter broke out from parts of the crowd, and someone shouted: "This counts as an achievement?"

Chen Can raised his fly-whisk and pointed it at Xiaoyu, his voice growing clearer still: "I would like to ask, plainly: is peace something the court owes its people by right — or is it a special favour that Jiang Wei has bestowed out of personal generosity? A Grand General who cannot win victories, cannot bring peace, cannot replenish the nation's resources, and whose only boast in the end is 'we are still holding on' — tell me, is 'still holding on' an achievement, or merely a minimum duty?"

"Minimum duty!" Several voices nearby answered at once. And then noise spread in all directions, a roiling uproar:

"Fabricating border threats out of thin air!"

"Incompetent and without virtue — battle after battle lost!"

"Withdraw the whole army from Tazhong back to Chengdu!"

Xiaoyu stepped forward and cried out sharply: "Do not slander the soldiers at the front and undermine their morale! Were it not for their lives spent fighting, how could the lands south of Hanzhong have remained at peace for decades? That we sit safely in Chengdu today is because our soldiers on the front lines hold on at great cost!"

"Withdraw to Chengdu!"

"Hold on?" came a scornful voice from the crowd. "War without end — you call that holding on?"

"When was the last time a Wei army actually invaded?"

Xiaoyu spoke again, but her voice was swallowed by the noise of the crowd, dissolved into that heavy, damp warmth in the air.

Those Confucian students brought by Yan Hong, those goons brought by Jiang Kang — several dozen of them altogether — always cried out in agreement the moment Chen Can finished speaking. The goons had even brought wooden swords and spears; each time they shouted, they struck their weapon handles against the stone ground with a piercing clatter.

Chen Can looked on with composure, as if appreciating the applause and cheering, waiting for it to spread — to infect the Imperial Academy students across the square, to infect the curious onlookers at its edges: the elders, the women and children, the craftsmen and labourers who had stopped to watch.

When the commotion had subsided a little, Chen Can raised his voice again: "This Yulin Guard lady is always saying we must not undermine morale. Very well — let me ask her." He swept his gaze across the crowd: "Since Jiang Wei has been matched against the Wei general Deng Ai, what has his record been? In the nineteenth year of Yanxi, at the Battle of Duangu,6 how many of our men fell? Last year, at the Battle of Houhe,7 how many of our men fell?"

"More than ten thousand dead in total!"

"Too many!"

"Put Jiang Wei on trial!"

The crowd below erupted in shouting; weapons clattered in a jagged din. Chen Can did nothing but tilt his body slightly, the fly-whisk angled loosely along his elbow, its white hairs swaying with the wind.

Chen Can's voice rose: "Is it my words that undermine morale — or the dead bodies piling up at the front?"

He stretched out his arm, gesturing to invite Xiaoyu and Zhuge Shang: "Please, tell their parents: morale must not be shaken!"

"Recall Jiang Wei!"

"Recall him!"

Below the platform, only a few scattered voices answered at first, and then more and more arms rose into the air. Some pumped their fists straight up; others cupped their hands around their mouths, necks straining with blue veins standing out, screaming themselves hoarse.

"Jiang Wei is guilty!"

Once that chant rose, the men at the front — red-faced and spitting — were already lunging toward the platform, as if the moment one person took a step forward, the whole crowd would surge up to drag someone off.

Chen Can tilted his head slightly toward the crowd and gave another bow, then continued: "These people claim we must not cause disorder in the ranks — but all they truly fear is that the truth might wound the pride of Jiang Wei and his faction. Meanwhile these Chengdu Yulin Guards don't even spare a thought for the dead and wounded at the front. Oh — but of course, your surname is Zhuge!"

Xiaoyu was choked between fury and anxiety; her chest rose and fell.

The men brought by Yan Hong and Jiang Kang burst into mocking laughter, heads thrown back, waiting with relish for whatever Chen Can would say next.

There was excitement in their eyes — the bright satisfaction of watching someone made a fool of.

Chen Can said: "These Later Han nobles and veterans can't abide the turning of the world; they dream of restoring the glory of their ancestors, while caring nothing for the lives of ordinary Ba-Shu people today. You made arrests simply to cover up the court's own failure and corruption! You can't stand to see citizens and Imperial Academy students speak freely about affairs of state!"

"Well said!" came several roars from below, with a burst of fierce applause.

Chen Can raised his fly-whisk toward the sky as though lifting the very current of his words to a higher pitch. The wide sleeves that had hung in perfect lines swept outward as he raised his arm and turned, drawing a broad arc; the jade pendants at his waist swung and swayed.

Chen Can's voice grew louder still: "Not long ago, the Zhuge Wuhou's own grandson was standing on this very square calling for the restoration of the Han. Let me ask him one thing: have you personally been to the front? Have you stood on a battlefield? Have you killed an enemy with your own hands? If yes, I will defer to you. If no — you sit in Chengdu making fine speeches while sending other people's sons to die. And you call that the spirit of Zhuge Wuhou's legacy?"

Zhuge Shang stared down at the platform beneath his feet, fists clenched tight, while from below came unrelenting applause, cheering, and the crash of weapons.

Chen Can's cheeks had grown faintly flushed, his breath quickening; he called out toward the crowd below: "Ordinary families ask only for fewer sons to die in war, and fewer rounds of conscription! We speak on behalf of all the people! Recall Jiang Wei!"

"Recall Jiang Wei — put him on trial!"

"Put Jiang Wei on trial!"

"Guilty! Guilty!"

The cries of "guilty" broke out from one place and then another across the square; before long, a rhythm began to form, and Imperial Academy students took up the chant. With each shout, a row of arms swept up in a wave, rippling from the front to the back of the crowd. Some shouted until sweat poured down their foreheads, yet still pressed their arms down and drove them back up again. Others chanted while turning to look around them, and seeing everyone nearby raise their fists, shouted louder than before, their faces alight with frenzied excitement — until even the bystanders at the edges of the square began to raise their hands and join the cry…

The Eastern Market, the Guangling Melody, innocent, innocent…

Ji Ying jolted back to herself — and just then, a figure in a short crimson robe, a leather belt at his waist with seal and ribbon, in the dress of a military officer, strode directly up onto the platform.

One hand on his hip, the other drawing his sword, he levelled the blade toward the sky; cold light ran along its edge:

"I am Ma Miao, Commander of Jianmen Pass! Hear me!"

The crowd fell somewhat quiet.

Ji Ying dimly recalled Ma Miao from the teahouse — he had mentioned wanting a transfer to a border command post.

Ma Miao bellowed at the crowd below: "Imperial Academy students who haven't enlisted — fine, but don't spout off about war! You haven't seen the terror and brutality of the battlefield! Clever words can't stop swords and arrows. Go back to your books, and stop making trouble for the nation!"

"And what makes you think you have?" came a voice from below.

"Of course I have!" Ma Miao swept his longsword in a wide arc toward the north: "At the battle on the Tao River, we smashed the Wei army — I was serving under the Grand General, in the Wudang Flying Battalion,8 vanguard, left flank company commander! My company of two hundred men killed over a hundred enemy soldiers and seized ten wagonloads of supplies!"

A sudden hush fell over the square.

Ma Miao sheathed his sword: "On the battlefield, morale is everything — the slightest slip can bring the whole thing down. Stop playing games with words and treating war as a sport!" He turned to face Chen Can directly: "Young Sir Chen, have you seen an army collapse? Have you seen tens of thousands of men all scrambling to ford a river at once, drowning in layers of bodies until the water runs still? At the Battle of Duangu, our army had lured the enemy into a trap — we were meant to close in and crush their main force with an ambush. But the Zhenxi General9 arrived a full day late, and the enemy was able to pick us apart one by one. And you put all the blame on Grand General Jiang! You don't understand — so don't presume to speak!"

Chen Can said: "Matters of war are beyond my understanding; I would not dare speak carelessly. I only know that ordinary people ask whether territory and population have grown, and whether they will face conscription again this year. I only ask that question on their behalf."

Chen Can touched the deep-green seal ribbon at his waist, then looked back at the crowd below. The men brought by Jiang Kang and Yan Hong, the Imperial Academy students, and the onlookers remained silent.

Ma Miao snorted loudly at Chen Can, then swung one arm to point at the crowd below: "The man you want to replace Jiang Wei as Right Grand General — you all know whose father he is. I'll ask you: can anyone name a single battle this man has won? Has anyone heard anything to suggest he knows how to run an army? Do you know which palace eunuch he has kept the closest company with at court? And do you know which father among you that eunuch kept closest to — pulling strings and manipulating affairs of state from behind the curtain?"

Ma Miao turned his gaze back. It landed directly on Chen Can.

The colour in Chen Can's face had drained away, leaving it paler than before.

The square remained quiet.

Ma Miao shouted again at the crowd: "Armchair strategists invite their own ruin! When Zhao Kuo replaced Lian Po,10 four hundred thousand Zhao soldiers were buried alive at the Battle of Changping! At the battle on the Tao River, the Wei general Wang Jing11 had no grasp of command whatsoever — even with twice our numbers, within half a day he had mountains of bodies piling up, and the Tao River ran still. You say that Jiang Wei has buried tens of thousands of soldiers over twenty years — but if you replace him with this Right Grand General and send him to the front, within two years, all armies will be annihilated and the whole nation will follow!"

"You lie through your teeth!" Below the platform, Yan Hong levelled his ivory fan at Ma Miao.

"Fabricating facts!"

"This man is one of Jiang Wei's people!" Confucian students brought by Yan Hong pointed up at Ma Miao and shouted abuse.

Ma Miao pointed right back at Yan Hong's faction: "The Later Han has had decades of peace — and what kind of young men has it raised? You can't even treat affairs of war with basic seriousness! You collude together and treat matters of national consequence as private business for your families to play with! Don't let the Later Han be ruined by your hands and leave a name that stinks for ten thousand years!"

"Jiang Wei's man!" Yan Hong's followers erupted in a clamour, weapon handles hammering against the stone ground!

Ma Miao was, after all, only one man; no matter how hard he strained his voice, it was smothered under "Jiang Wei's man" and the relentless hammering. The veins on his neck stood out as he pointed at the crowd below and roared:

"Still playing dumb! Shameless swine and dogs!"

"You're the swine and dog!"

"Jiang Wei's man is guilty!"

While the crowd below was still trading insults with Ma Miao, a powerfully built man had already vaulted up onto the wooden platform — and within three steps, he was standing in front of Ma Miao.

Ji Ying felt the platform shudder under her feet. She looked at Jiang Kang: half a head taller than Ma Miao and half a body wider.

"Since you want to talk about fighting — let me, Jiang Kang, give you a proper match!"

Two wooden spears were passed up from below.

Ma Miao hesitated for a moment, gave a brief bow in all directions, then accepted the spear.

Ji Ying and the others cleared space on the wooden platform and watched from below; the platform itself had been roughly thrown up on four thick wooden posts.

Though the spear was long, Ma Miao gripped it near the middle, his footwork steady, neither foot crossing the other.

Jiang Kang gave him a brief look and the ghost of a sneer, then said nothing and made his move: a direct thrust down the centre!

Ma Miao swept it aside with a lateral block — passable composure; Jiang Kang pulled back in an instant, flicking the tip toward his shoulder; Ma Miao twisted away just in time, but the moment his spear came up half an inch, Jiang Kang had already looped back underneath, sweeping for his knees — Ma Miao stepped back to clear it!

"Yeah! Yeah!" The crowd below roared with excitement.

Ma Miao did not press forward, but in the space of a breath Jiang Kang had already launched five or six moves, each one tighter and closer than the last. Ma Miao held his ground at first, but his footwork was no longer as steady as before; he fell back again and again, and when he was backed into a corner, could do nothing but hold his spear flat and seal off the space in front of him.

Jiang Kang sent another thrust driving full force down the centre. Ma Miao braced both arms against the shaft and met it — the impact carried from the point of contact to both ends, and the sudden weight drove Ma Miao's wrists down and sent him back another half-step, one foot hanging over the edge. Jiang Kang did not pull back but pressed in immediately, driving the momentum forward and adding the full weight of his body to the push. Ma Miao had just started to pull free and reset, but this push threw his breath into disorder and his hands lagged a half-beat behind —

Jiang Kang let out a low grunt. He twisted his shaft and drove with his whole body.

There was a dull thud. Ma Miao lost his footing, lurched sideways, his spear flying from his hands — and he tumbled off the platform!

The crowd let out a collective gasp, then erupted into loud applause and cheering.

Jiang Kang drew his spear back and stood, not even winded, and cast a flat look down at the crowd below.

"I am not even a soldier, and I still beat you? I'd say you're a false military officer!"

"A fake!" someone nearby shouted.

"He's one of Jiang Wei's men too!"

"Try them both together — guilty!"

"Guilty!"

The cries of "guilty" poured in from all sides, while below the platform Ma Miao had hauled himself back to his feet, face flushed red, trading insults with Yan Hong's men. Then a boot came flying at him; Ma Miao knocked it aside with one hand, but right away fruit peel, wooden slats, and even broken chunks of brick and stone came sailing through the air — some missing Ma Miao entirely and striking the people in front, landing on shoulders and heads.

Ma Miao cursed as he retreated, then spun around and made straight for the corner of the square where the crowd was thinnest.

"Don't come back!"

"Don't run away, loser! Come back and die!"

The crowd watched Ma Miao walk away, jeering and laughing without pause.

That day in the teahouse, Ma Miao had what seemed like a hot-tempered wife.

I wonder what she would have done, had she been standing here now.

"This man is a fine commander," Zhuge Shang beside Ji Ying said, tight with frustration. "But he isn't a front-line fighter — that isn't what his talent is for!"

Xiaoyu's brows were drawn hard together; her gaze fixed on Jiang Kang up on the platform. She squeezed her fists shut, then let them fall open again, and murmured under her breath:

"Can I take him?"

If Jiang Kang were allowed to stand there like that, people in the square might truly start to believe that every soldier in the Later Han was a fraud.

At the Prince of Beidi's estate, hadn't Xiaoyu gone undefeated for a long while?

"You can fight him." Ji Ying put a hand on Xiaoyu's shoulder. It was solid.

"Right!" Xiaoyu snatched up Ma Miao's fallen spear, ran three steps, and sprang — she was on the platform.

"Jiang Kang — enough of your arrogance! Come and fight me!"

"A woman?" Some in the crowd laughed; others cheered.

Jiang Kang frowned, and then his eyes brightened: "You issued the challenge — I'm not the one picking on a woman! Don't blame me when the spear shows no mercy!" He fell into his stance.

Zhuge Yu stood at the left of the platform, both hands gripping the spear somewhat closer together than usual, the front hand low, the back hand loose and not rigid, the butt angled slightly down.

Jiang Kang faced her from across the platform, his spear laid horizontally, a half-smile at the corner of his mouth; without a word he lunged forward, sending a swift, dense sequence of thrusts!

Xiaoyu deflected left and right — noticeably faster than Ma Miao had been, the spear following her feet wherever it went, without a moment's hesitation, in one fluid, unbroken motion.

Then Zhuge Yu let out a sharp cry, and with a flick of her wrist swept Jiang Kang's tip half an inch to the side; at the same instant she stepped forward, and drove the shaft's midpoint straight into him with the momentum of her whole body. The two weapons struck with a dull crack!

Jiang Kang's shoulder jerked back and he stepped back half a pace, only to counter at once, angling for her ribs; Xiaoyu pulled her elbow in, turned her waist, and pressed the butt of her spear down — the deflection was precise, catching the attack just outside the line. Both fighters stepped back a pace simultaneously, weapons crossed and still, coiled for the next exchange.

"Yes!" The crowd roared across the square.

Jiang Kang's moves — straight thrusts, angled jabs, draws, and drives down the centre — some of them had turned up in the Prince's estate that day.

Xiaoyu's reactions in combat had the feel of choreography rehearsed in advance, every one landing exactly right.

An officer like Ma Miao, who did not train with a long spear, had been worked into a frenzy; but Xiaoyu, who drilled with it morning and evening and knew the common techniques here inside out, had no such weak point to exploit.

In no time they had exchanged several more rounds; Jiang Kang sent a low sweep at her legs, and Xiaoyu drew her feet back slightly, the shaft cutting across in front of her and stopping it flat. Jiang Kang used the contact to press forward, trying to push her to the edge of the platform; Zhuge Yu sank her shoulder and back, drove from her waist, and pushed back — an inch, maybe two. It wasn't pretty, but it was rock-solid, and Jiang Kang was forced back a step.

Another roar from the crowd across the square.

Just like that — hold the ground, then counter-attack.

Jiang Kang narrowed his eyes. Under the thick beard, his frame was built like an ox.

Then his front hand eased slightly, his back shoulder dropped a fraction, and a sliver of an opening appeared in the space before him.

What was he thinking?

Xiaoyu struck. She stepped forward, turned her hips, and drove her tip cleanly toward the gap Jiang Kang had left — clean, swift, without a trace of hesitation; Jiang Kang's shaft suddenly dipped, not going for her tip at all, but sliding along the middle of her spear and pressing down with full force, his whole body driving in after it. The change came fast and savage — not a direct counter, but a move that seized on the one instant of Xiaoyu's forward commitment, when her most solid step became a fatal crack.

Xiaoyu's expression shifted; she flicked her wrist and tried to pull her spear back, but her whole body was already committed to the forward lunge, and she saw Jiang Kang wrench with another twist of his shaft — her spear was forced outward and to the side, the two of them suddenly close, and once Jiang Kang had made his move, his massive frame came pressing in as well.

Xiaoyu let out a low cry and instead of retreating pushed forward — determined to muscle the situation back by sheer force.

Xiaoyu is strong, but —

The two shafts cracked together with a muffled thud; Xiaoyu was driven back half a step, and Jiang Kang followed with half a step of his own, a foot that fell like a hammerblow, shaking the whole platform with a hollow boom.

Jiang Kang was drawing her into a test of brute strength.

Xiaoyu tilted her head and angled a shoulder back, trying to wrench some kind of opening from within that thundering momentum — and it was this sideways tilt of her head that kept her throat from being driven squarely up into the shaft; Jiang Kang had already followed the angle in, and the foreend of his spear grazed past her neck and shoulder and crashed heavily across her collarbone.

Xiaoyu's footing went to pieces, half her body lurching off-balance, tipping back.

Jiang Kang stepped another half-pace forward, and aimed this thrust directly at Xiaoyu's throat!

A killing move — and Xiaoyu cannot see it coming!

"Your throat!" Ji Ying screamed.

Xiaoyu clenched both hands around her shaft and drove it down with all her strength, her legs kicking back in a desperate leap!

Her spear flew loose!

The tip punched toward her chest!

Xiaoyu's body tumbled backward off the platform in a rear flip, and Ji Ying had already shot forward, seizing her by the arm, and they landed together in a stumbling sprawl on the ground below!

Zhuge Shang rushed to them from the other side, anxious: "Sister — are you all right?"

Xiaoyu's eyes went wide; she could only gasp for breath.

Jiang Kang stood alone on the platform, his spear angled down toward Xiaoyu below.

His chest rose and fell.

"Defeated!" someone behind Ji Ying shouted.

"That's all the Yulin Guards are worth!"

"Western Regions woman — go back to the Western Regions!"

Ji Ying turned and fixed her eyes on the man who had said "Western Regions woman."

He didn't notice. He went on bellowing, one shout after another.

"Jiang Wei's faction — they're all useless!"

"Recall Jiang Wei!" Yan Hong led the chant this time, and the crowd picked it up instantly, "recall Jiang Wei" ringing out in four even beats, wave after wave, right in Ji Ying's ears and spreading out in all directions across the square.

"Recall Jiang Wei!" The goons at the front had stopped at mere fist-pumping and shouting; now they started shoving forward. Some slapped their own chests with open palms as they chanted; some had screamed themselves hoarse and were stamping their heels against the ground instead, raising small clouds of dust, their nostrils flaring with every breath.

A scroll of bamboo went spinning up into the air, and in its wake cloth shoes, cloth bags, fruit, and stone chunks came flying from all directions, scattered and sudden as rain.

Through the gaps in the crowd, Ji Ying spotted Zhuge Mao and Chen Shou waving.

A broken piece of brick came flying; Chen Shou, frozen in place, took it squarely on the head. His cry of pain was swallowed in the noise.

Zhuge Mao pulled Chen Shou along and reached Ji Ying's side: "Come with me — quickly!"

Zhuge Shang snatched up an umbrella someone had thrown and held it above the group, batting away the hail of flying objects, as all five of them ran for the gate tower of the Imperial Academy. Ji Ying had no idea how many things had already struck her across the back.

Through the gate tower, the shouting dulled. Ji Ying was running ahead when she glanced back: Xiaoyu was rubbing her chest; Chen Shou's cheek was streaming bright blood, dripping a trail of red down to the ground, Zhuge Mao holding him up as they lurched forward.

Zhuge Mao pointed to the far end of the Academy buildings: "The Imperial Library — there's medicine, and people!"

The way Chen Can spoke seemed civil enough, but something about it had been wrong all along…

Ma Miao had said — Chen Can's father, Yan Hong's father, both tied to the same palace eunuch…

Who is that eunuch?

Even coming to apologise had only ended up working in their favour.

They had already made up their minds to replace Jiang Wei.

Partisan Prohibitions, a century, last year…

Innocent, innocent, guilty, guilty…

Western Regions woman, outsider, get out…

When scholars stood against power, they were supposed to stand for something real — so why had it all become this today, something to be herded and used at will?

Why do I find myself hating the Imperial Academy students?

1 Guan (冠): A formal headpiece worn by men of rank in ancient China, secured over the topknot with a hairpin and tied at the chin; unlike a hat, it covered only the upper portion of the head, and its style indicated the wearer's status and occasion. ↩

2 Seal ribbon (印綬): A coloured cord worn at the waist from which an official's personal seal was suspended; the colour of the ribbon indicated rank. ↩

3 Yanxi (延熙): The third reign era of the Later Han emperor Liu Shan, spanning 238–257 CE; it was the longest era name of the Shu Han dynasty. ↩

4 Battle of Taoxi (洮西之戰, 255 CE): Jiang Wei's most celebrated victory, in which the Later Han army inflicted massive casualties on a Wei force under Wang Jing along the western bank of the Tao River; despite the scale of the Wei defeat, no territory was permanently recovered. ↩

5 Yong and Liang, north of Qishan (雍州、涼州、岐山以北): Yong Province and Liang Province were the two northwestern provinces of Wei that Jiang Wei repeatedly targeted in his northern campaigns; Qishan, a mountain range in what is now southern Gansu, marked the traditional launching point for Shu Han offensives into Wei territory. ↩

6 Battle of Duangu (段谷之戰, 256 CE): A costly defeat for Jiang Wei, in which a planned encirclement of Wei forces collapsed after the supporting general Hu Ji failed to arrive on time; the Later Han suffered heavy casualties and Jiang Wei was subsequently demoted. ↩

7 Battle of Houhe (侯和之戰, 262 CE): The last of Jiang Wei's northern campaigns, ending in defeat at the hands of Wei general Deng Ai; following this loss, Jiang Wei withdrew his forces to Tazhong in the far northwest, where he was still stationed at the time of this novel. ↩

8 Wudang Flying Battalion (無當飛軍): An elite Later Han infantry unit originally raised by Zhuge Liang from the tribal warriors of Nanzhong (present-day Yunnan); renowned for their skill in mountain terrain and their discipline under fire, they formed the backbone of several of Jiang Wei's northern campaigns. ↩

9 Zhenxi General (鎮西將軍): The officer in question was Hu Ji (胡濟), who held the title of Zhenxi Grand General and was assigned to rendezvous with Jiang Wei at Duangu; his failure to arrive on time turned a planned victory into a rout. ↩

10 Zhao Kuo, Lian Po, and the Battle of Changping (趙括、廉頗、長平之戰, 260 BCE): At Changping, the state of Zhao replaced its seasoned general Lian Po with the untested Zhao Kuo, who had only studied war in books; the result was a catastrophic defeat in which an estimated 400,000 Zhao soldiers were encircled and buried alive by the Qin army. ↩

11 Wang Jing (王經): The Wei general who suffered the catastrophic defeat at Taoxi in 255 CE; though he survived the battle, he was later executed in 260 CE — not for military failure, but because he refused to report to Sima Zhao that the Wei emperor Cao Mao had plotted against the Sima family. ↩
